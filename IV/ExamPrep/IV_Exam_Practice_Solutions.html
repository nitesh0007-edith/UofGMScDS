Stevens' Psychophysical Power Law & Chart Critiques

Outline the basic concept of Stevens' psychophysical power law, and why it is important in the design of information visualisation systems. In your answer, outline three different common styles of chart or graphical encoding, and critique each of them in terms of this power law.

[15 marks]

Part 1 — What is Stevens' Power Law?

Stevens' Power Law is a psychophysical principle describing the relationship between the physical magnitude of a stimulus and the magnitude we perceive it to be. It is expressed as:

P = k × Sn P = perceived magnitude  |  S = physical stimulus  |  n = exponent  |  k = scaling constant

The crucial insight is that perception does not scale linearly with physical reality. The exponent n varies by sensory dimension:

Visual ChannelExponent (n)What this means
Length / Position≈ 1.0Perceived accurately — 2× longer looks 2× longer
Area≈ 0.7Underestimated — a bubble 4× the area looks only 2.6× bigger
Volume≈ 0.6Even more underestimated
Brightness≈ 0.5Severely underestimated

Part 2 — Why it Matters for Visualisation

Stevens' law directly determines how accurately users can read quantitative information from a chart. When n = 1.0, users extract accurate data values. When n < 1.0, they systematically underestimate larger values — creating a misleading picture that cannot be fixed by just labelling the data. This is why channel selection is a fundamental design decision, not an aesthetic one.

Design Rule: Always choose visual channels with exponents close to 1.0 for quantitative data. This is the scientific foundation behind Cleveland & McGill's ranking: position (n≈1.0) > length (n≈1.0) > area (n≈0.7) > volume (n≈0.6).

Part 3 — Critique of Three Chart Types

📊 Bar Chart
GOOD — n ≈ 1.0

Bar height encodes data using length, which has an exponent of approximately 1.0. When bars share a common baseline (x-axis), users are actually judging position on a common scale — the highest-ranked channel in Cleveland & McGill's hierarchy.

A bar twice as tall is correctly perceived as representing twice the value. This makes bar charts among the most accurate quantitative encodings available. The requirement of a zero baseline is critical — truncating the y-axis inflates the visual effect and creates a lie factor > 1.

🥧 Pie Chart
POOR — angle perception

Pie charts encode data using angle. Although Stevens' original law didn't assign angle a neat exponent, empirical studies (Cleveland & McGill 1984) show angle judgements are systematically inaccurate — ranked only 4th in their hierarchy.

Users struggle to compare non-adjacent slices, and both very small and very similar slices are nearly impossible to differentiate. Some designers use area (the slice area), which is even worse (n≈0.7). Pie charts are defensible only for showing rough proportions with ≤ 5 distinct categories.

🫧 Bubble Chart
WORST — n ≈ 0.7

Bubble charts encode a third quantitative dimension using circle area, which has an exponent of approximately 0.7. This means a bubble representing 100 will be perceived as representing only 1000.7 ≈ 25.1 on a normalised scale — a severe underestimation of larger values.

Some designers encode data in the radius rather than area to compensate, but this worsens the distortion further since perceived area grows as r². The only mitigation is explicit labelling, which defeats the purpose of visual encoding. Bubble charts should be used only when the task is detecting rough relative magnitude, not precise comparison.

Marks Breakdown

~4 marks: Correct statement of the law with formula and exponent examples

~4 marks: Clear explanation of why it matters — channel selection, systematic error

~7 marks: 3 charts critiqued with specific reference to the law — not just "good/bad" but WHY in terms of n

Small Multiples Design — Rat Growth Experiment

Multidimensional data is common in science and industry. Here is a biologist's experimental data scheme recording measurements of rats' growth:

ID: integer; // unique measurement ID, e.g. 1234 dateOfTest: date; // day of measurement, e.g. Jan 19, 2006 animal: integer; // rat identifier, e.g. 2345 comment: string; // lab notes, e.g. "Doesn't look too healthy" weight: real; // mass in grams, e.g. 150.9 length: real; // length in cm, e.g. 22.1 food: integer; // food type identifier, e.g. 3

Outline a system design for a visualisation centred on the small multiples design technique. Justify the way you categorise and graphically encode this data.

[15 marks]

Step 1 — Categorise the Data

Before designing the visualisation, we must classify each attribute by type, as this determines what visual encoding is appropriate:

weightQuantitativePrimary outcomeY-axis position — line chart height
lengthQuantitativeSecondary outcomeSecondary panel or dot size (optional)
foodCategorical (nominal) — 4 typesThe small multiples dimensionOne panel per food type

Step 2 — Why Small Multiples?

The core research question is: "Does food type affect rat growth over time?" Small multiples are the ideal technique here because:

  • There are 4 food types — a natural, small set of categories to partition by
  • The comparison task is cross-panel: "does the growth curve in panel A look different from panel B?" — this is precisely what small multiples enable (Tufte: "eyes beat memory")
  • Showing all 4 foods on one chart would require colour-coding by food AND by rat, resulting in a cluttered, unreadable display
  • Side-by-side panels allow direct visual comparison with no need to remember what the previous state looked like (unlike animation)

Step 3 — System Design

Step 4 — Justification of Choices

  • food as the panel dimension: It is the independent variable being studied. Partitioning by this nominal variable creates a meaningful comparison structure — the analyst can immediately see whether growth curves differ across foods.
  • Position for weight and date: Both are the most important quantitative/temporal attributes. Using position maximises accuracy (Stevens n≈1.0) and places them on the highest-ranked channel.
  • Hue for individual rats: rat IDs are nominal — no rat is "more" than another. Hue is the ideal associative variable. However, with many rats, colour-blind-safe palettes (e.g., ColorBrewer) should be used.
  • length not shown directly: Adding a second y-axis (dual-axis chart) would be a design pitfall — Munzner warns against this as it creates misleading apparent correlations. Instead, length could be a secondary panel row or dot size.
  • comment as tooltip: Free text cannot be encoded visually in a quantitative sense. Displaying it on hover respects the details-on-demand principle without cluttering the main view.
  • ID ignored: A unique numerical identifier carries no data meaning for visualisation purposes.

Interactive Filtering — Bank Trades Database

Imagine you work in a bank where a database of financial trades is key. Attributes include: trade amount (£), yield %, days until maturity, customer ID, currency, trade date, and employee ID. Workers use SQL but training takes a long time and error rates are high. Outline an alternative system based on interactive filtering and querying that is guaranteed to have no SQL errors.

[15 marks]

Why SQL Fails Here

SQL is a text-based, declarative language where errors arise from: syntax mistakes (missing semicolons, wrong JOIN syntax), logical errors (wrong WHERE conditions), and semantic errors (filtering on the wrong field). Training takes long because users must learn a formal grammar. Even expert users make errors. The result: wasted time, incorrect reports, and potentially costly financial decisions based on wrong queries.

Core Design Goal: Replace free-text SQL with a constrained, visual interface where it is structurally impossible to form an incorrect query — because the interface only offers valid operations.

The Dynamic Query System Design

Following Shneiderman's mantra — "Overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand" — the system is structured in three layers:

System Layout — Dynamic Query Interface for Bank Trades

Justification vs SQL

AspectSQL SystemDynamic Query System
Error guaranteeSyntax + logic errors commonImpossible to form invalid query — interface constrains input
TrainingLong; requires learning formal grammarMinimal — sliders and dropdowns are intuitive
ExplorationRequires knowing what to ask in advanceDrag slider and see results change — encourages discovery
FeedbackWrite query → execute → wait → see resultsImmediate visual feedback as controls move
OverviewNo overview — must write a query to see anythingAlways shows all data; filtering highlights a subset
UndoMust rewrite queryMove slider back — instant reversal (Shneiderman: reversible actions)
Key Point for Exam: "No SQL errors" is guaranteed because the system only accepts structured inputs (sliders with valid numeric ranges, dropdowns with valid categorical values). The query is built automatically from widget state — the user never writes any code. There is no syntax layer to get wrong.

Parallel Coordinates & SPLOM — Climate Dataset

A climate scientist has a dataset of 5,000 weather stations worldwide, each with 8 measured attributes: temperature (°C), humidity (%), wind speed (km/h), rainfall (mm), air pressure (hPa), latitude, longitude, and altitude (m). She wants to explore correlations between these variables and identify unusual stations. Compare the use of a Scatterplot Matrix (SPLOM) and Parallel Coordinates for this task. Recommend one approach and justify your choice, describing how interaction would support the analysis.
[15 marks]

🔷 SPLOM — Description & Strengths

A SPLOM (Scatterplot Matrix) creates a grid of every pairwise scatter plot. For 8 dimensions: 8×7 = 56 scatter plots (or 28 unique pairs). Each cell shows two variables plotted against each other, making it easy to spot positive/negative correlations, clusters, and outliers for any pair of variables simultaneously. The diagonal typically shows variable names or histograms of individual distributions.

Strength: All pairwise relationships are visible at once — no need to navigate or sequence views. Brushing and linking: selecting a cluster in one scatter plot highlights those same stations in all other plots, revealing multi-dimensional patterns.

Weakness: With 8 dimensions, each individual plot is very small. At 5,000 points per plot, overplotting becomes severe. Scaling to 8 dimensions × 28 plots means each plot is roughly 1/28th of the screen.

🔶 Parallel Coordinates — Description & Strengths

Parallel coordinates uses 8 vertical axes (one per attribute) with each station drawn as a polyline connecting its value on each axis. With 5,000 stations, the display will be dense but patterns emerge as bundles of lines with similar trajectories.

Strength: All 8 dimensions shown simultaneously. Correlations between adjacent axes are immediately visible (parallel lines = positive correlation; crossing lines = negative). Handles more dimensions than SPLOM before becoming unmanageable. Brushing on one axis (e.g., select high altitude stations) instantly highlights those lines across all other dimensions.

Weakness: Only reveals correlations between adjacent axes — axis ordering critically affects what you can see. With 5,000 items it becomes cluttered; alpha (transparency) helps but doesn't fully solve it.

✅ Recommendation: Parallel Coordinates (with caveats)

  • Dimensionality: 8 dimensions is at the upper end for SPLOM usability (24–28 small plots). Parallel coordinates handles this more gracefully in a single coordinated view.
  • Task fit: The scientist wants to find "unusual stations" — outliers appear as lines that cross the general flow. This is easier to spot in parallel coordinates.
  • Interaction: Allow axis reordering so the analyst can test all adjacency relationships. Brushing on latitude/longitude axes to select a geographic region, then observing how temperature, humidity, and pressure behave for those stations, directly supports the correlation-discovery task.
  • Complement with SPLOM: Use SPLOM for a preliminary pairwise overview to identify the most interesting axis pairs, then configure parallel coordinates to put those axes adjacent.
  • Apply Shneiderman's mantra: overview (full parallel coordinates with all stations) → filter (brush on axes to narrow down) → details-on-demand (click an outlier line to see the station's full record).
Practice Q2

Bertin's Visual Variables — Hospital Patient Dashboard

A hospital wants to display a real-time dashboard showing all current inpatients. For each patient, the following attributes are recorded: ward (A, B, C, D, E), risk level (Low, Medium, High, Critical), age (years), time since last observation (hours), and whether they have an active alert (Yes/No). Design the visual encoding for this dashboard, justifying each choice using Bertin's visual variables framework. Identify any encoding choices that would be inappropriate and explain why.
[15 marks]

🔷 Data Classification First

  • Ward (A–E): Categorical/Nominal — 5 categories, no order implied
  • Risk level: Ordinal — Low < Medium < High < Critical (ordered, not numeric)
  • Age: Quantitative — continuous numeric value
  • Time since last observation: Quantitative — hours, continuous
  • Active alert: Binary/Nominal — Yes or No

🔶 Encoding Choices & Justification

  • Ward → Colour Hue: Hue is associative — no ward appears more important than another. 5 distinct hues is within the limit of ~5–8 distinguishable categories. Use a colour-blind-safe palette (e.g., ColorBrewer Qualitative) and add a redundant spatial grouping (position on the display) to support users with colour vision deficiency.
  • Risk level → Colour Value (lightness): Risk is ordinal — it has a natural order. Colour value encodes order effectively: light pink (Low) → medium red (Medium) → dark red (High) → near-black red (Critical). Value is dissociative — the darkest (most critical) patients visually dominate and attract attention, which is exactly the desired behaviour for a safety-critical dashboard.
  • Age → Position (X-axis) or Size: Quantitative attribute — position on a common scale (n≈1.0) is most accurate. In a scatter-plot-style layout, age could drive horizontal position. Alternatively, dot size (also quantitative, n≈1.0 for length) could work, though area underestimates (n≈0.7) so size should be used cautiously.
  • Time since last observation → Position (Y-axis): Quantitative. Higher on the y-axis = longer wait. Combining with risk level creates a 2D risk map: top-right (long wait + critical) patients are clearly in the most urgent quadrant.
  • Active alert → Shape or Added mark (enclosure): Binary nominal — shape is appropriate for nominal data, and a flashing border or enclosure (Gestalt principle) for "Yes" makes alerts immediately pre-attentive.

❌ Inappropriate Encoding Choices

  • Using hue for risk level: Hue is NOT ordered — Bertin classifies it as poor for ordinal data. "Red = critical, blue = low" is not intuitively ordered and different people assign different severity meanings to colours. Colour value (lightness) must be used instead.
  • Using size for ward: Size implies order — bigger = more. Wards have no ordering, so using size would falsely suggest one ward is "more" than another. Shape or hue is correct for nominal data.
  • Using area for age: Due to Stevens' law (n≈0.7), area-based encoding of age would cause systematic underestimation of older patients' ages. Position is far more accurate.
  • Using rainbow colour map for time since observation: Rainbow maps are not perceptually uniform and create false boundaries. A sequential colour map (light → dark) from a palette like Viridis should be used for this continuous quantitative variable.
Practice Q3

Larkin & Simon — Medical Diagnosis System

A medical AI system can present diagnostic reasoning in two formats: (A) a written report listing patient symptoms, test results, and differential diagnoses in paragraphs, or (B) a visual decision tree diagram showing the same reasoning as a branching structure. Using Larkin & Simon's framework, explain which representation would better support a doctor's diagnostic decision-making, and under what circumstances the other representation might be preferable.
[15 marks]

🔷 Larkin & Simon's Framework

Larkin & Simon (1987) distinguish between informational equivalence and computational equivalence. Two representations are informationally equivalent if the same information can be derived from both. They are computationally equivalent only if they require the same cognitive effort to extract and use that information. The decision tree and written report may be informationally equivalent — both contain the same diagnostic logic — but they are NOT computationally equivalent.

🔶 Why the Decision Tree (B) is Better for Diagnosis

  • Locality: In the written report, to understand whether "fever AND cough" leads to a diagnosis, the doctor must search through paragraphs, hold information in working memory, and manually trace the reasoning. In the decision tree, the path from symptom to diagnosis is physically present as a connected branch — related information is spatially grouped at one location.
  • Minimising labelling: The tree's spatial structure encodes logical relationships (branching = "OR", sequential nodes = "AND") without requiring explicit connective words. The written report must spell out "if... then... else..." at every step.
  • Perceptual inference: The doctor can visually follow a branch from the patient's symptoms to the diagnosis — this path-tracing is a perceptual operation (moving eyes along lines) rather than a cognitive one (mentally tracking "which paragraph refers to this symptom?"). The decision tree makes the inference literally visible.
  • Error reduction: In a high-stakes clinical environment, missing a critical branch in the written report is easy. In the tree, missing a branch is harder because the structure makes completeness visually apparent.

✅ When the Written Report (A) is Preferable

  • Communication to patients: A written report is more natural and accessible to patients who are unfamiliar with decision tree notation. The sequential narrative structure matches how people normally communicate.
  • Legal/documentation purposes: Formal written reports are the standard for medical records, insurance, and legal review. Sequential structure supports systematic auditing.
  • Complex probabilistic reasoning: If the diagnosis involves Bayesian probability calculations or nuanced qualifications ("likely X, but given history of Y, consider Z"), these are better expressed in structured prose than a strict branching diagram.
  • When the diagram is poorly designed: Larkin & Simon's "(Sometimes)" caveat — a badly laid out decision tree that doesn't map naturally to the logical structure would be harder to use than well-written text.

💡 Conclusion

For the core task of following diagnostic reasoning at the point of care, the decision tree is superior because it reduces computational load through locality, minimises labelling, and enables perceptual inference. The written report is better suited for communication, documentation, and edge cases requiring nuanced prose reasoning.

Practice Q4

Network Visualisation — Social Media Influence

A marketing team has a directed network dataset of 50,000 social media users, where edges represent "retweet" relationships (User A retweeted User B). Edge weights represent how often A retweeted B. They want to: (i) identify key influencers, (ii) find closely-knit communities, and (iii) check whether two specific users are connected. Critically compare node-link diagrams and adjacency matrix representations for these three tasks, and recommend a strategy that could combine both.
[15 marks]

🔷 Node-Link Diagram — Critique

  • Identifying influencers (Task i): Force-directed layout will naturally place high-degree nodes (many connections) in central positions. The visual "hub-and-spoke" pattern makes influencers visually salient. However, with 50,000 nodes, the display becomes a dense "hairball" — overplotting makes individual nodes and edges impossible to distinguish without aggressive filtering or semantic zoom.
  • Finding communities (Task ii): Clusters emerge naturally in force-directed layouts, as tightly connected nodes attract each other (spring analogy). Colour-coding by detected community (using an algorithm like Louvain) maps well to hue. However, at 50,000 nodes, this requires heavy aggregation or filtering first.
  • Checking specific connectivity (Task iii): Tracing a path between two specific nodes in a 50,000-node hairball is nearly impossible visually. Interactive path-highlighting (select both nodes, highlight shortest path) partially addresses this but is computationally expensive.

🔶 Adjacency Matrix — Critique

  • Identifying influencers (Task i): Rows/columns with many filled cells (dense rows) represent high out-degree (prolific retweeters) or high in-degree (much-retweeted influencers). With proper row/column ordering, these appear as dense rows or columns. Cell colour encodes edge weight (retweet frequency) — a sequential colour map works well here.
  • Finding communities (Task ii): If rows and columns are reordered by community membership (e.g., via clustering), community blocks appear as dense squares along the diagonal. This is a powerful technique for community detection that is completely hidden in a cluttered node-link view.
  • Checking specific connectivity (Task iii): Direct lookup — find row User A and column User B. The cell is either filled (connected) or empty (not connected). This is the matrix's strongest task, making it ideal for this specific query. No visual traversal needed.
  • Limitation: 50,000 × 50,000 matrix = 2.5 billion cells — not feasible to render in full. Only viable after filtering to a relevant subgraph (e.g., top 500 most active users).

✅ Recommended Combined Strategy

  • Overview: Start with a force-directed node-link of the full network, but aggregate nodes into communities (super-nodes). Each super-node represents a community; its size encodes member count; edge weight encodes inter-community retweeting frequency. This makes the macro structure of 50,000 users navigable.
  • Zoom into communities: Clicking a super-node expands it to show the actual users within, revealing the internal community structure.
  • Switch to matrix for connectivity queries (Task iii): Provide a brushing-linked adjacency matrix view of the selected subgraph. Selecting two users in the node-link view jumps to their row/column intersection in the matrix — instant connectivity check.
  • Shneiderman's mantra applies: overview (aggregated node-link) → zoom (expand community) → filter (select subgraph) → details-on-demand (matrix lookup for specific pair).
Practice Q5

Evaluation Methods — NHS Patient Dashboard

You have built a new information visualisation dashboard for NHS nurses to monitor ward patients (similar to the hospital dashboard in Practice Q2). Your manager wants to know if it is better than the existing paper-based system. Describe how you would design an evaluation study to answer this question. In your answer, discuss the choice of quantitative vs qualitative evaluation methods, appropriate metrics, the threats to validity in Munzner's nested model, and any ethical considerations specific to a healthcare setting.
[15 marks]

🔷 Framing the Research Question

Before choosing a method, we must specify what "better" means. Possible dimensions: speed of finding at-risk patients, error rate in recording observations, nurse satisfaction, time to respond to alerts. Different dimensions require different methods.

🔶 Quantitative Evaluation (Controlled Experiment)

  • Design: A within-subjects or between-subjects study where nurses complete a set of standardised tasks (e.g., "Identify the patient with the highest risk level who has not been observed in over 4 hours") using both the dashboard and the paper system, in randomised order.
  • Metrics: Task completion time (efficiency), error rate (accuracy), number of missed alerts (recall), and time-to-first-correct-identification (response speed).
  • Strength: Statistical analysis (paired t-test or ANOVA) produces objective, reproducible results — "the dashboard reduced task time by 34%, p<0.01."
  • Weakness: Lab setting may not reflect real ward conditions (interruptions, stress, multiple simultaneous patients). Task design may not cover all real-world scenarios.

🔶 Qualitative Evaluation

  • Think-aloud protocol: Ask nurses to narrate their thought process while using the dashboard. Reveals unexpected confusion, misinterpretation of visual encodings, or workflow mismatches.
  • Semi-structured interviews post-use: "What was confusing?", "Was there anything you couldn't find quickly?"
  • Strength: Discovers issues that quantitative metrics miss — e.g., nurses might complete tasks fast but via a workaround that will fail in edge cases.
  • Weakness: Small samples, subjective, hard to generalise.

🔷 Munzner's Nested Model — Threats to Validity

  • Level 1 (Domain): Are we solving the right problem? Evaluation should verify that the tasks tested reflect real nursing workflows — failure here means a "successful" evaluation in the lab still doesn't improve patient care.
  • Level 2 (Abstraction): Did we correctly map "patient risk" to the right abstract data type? Evaluation should check whether nurses correctly interpret the risk encoding.
  • Level 3 (Encoding): Are the visual variables (colour value for risk, hue for ward) being used as intended? Eye-tracking studies could verify this.
  • Level 4 (Algorithm): Does the dashboard update in real-time without lag on live hospital systems? Performance benchmarking required.

⚕️ Ethical Considerations

  • Participant safety: Nurses must not be distracted from real patient care during the study. Evaluation must occur in a simulation environment with realistic but synthetic patient data.
  • Patient data privacy: Any real patient data used must be fully anonymised and comply with GDPR and NHS data governance policies.
  • Informed consent: Nurses must consent freely, with no pressure from management, as they may feel obligated to participate given their employment relationship.
  • Generalisability: Results from one ward or hospital may not generalise to others. Report scope of findings clearly.
