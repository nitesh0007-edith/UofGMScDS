🎯 Introduction: The Power of Information

Imagine you're searching for a lost submarine in the ocean. Initially, it could be anywhere. But then you receive a message: "The submarine's last known location was in the northern region." Suddenly, you don't need to search the entire ocean anymore!

This is conditional probability – how our beliefs change when we gain new information.

Scenario Without Information:
Search Area: Entire Ocean (256 squares)
Probability submarine is at any specific square: 1/256

Scenario With Information (Northern Region):
Search Area: Northern Half (128 squares)
Probability submarine is at any northern square: 1/128

📊 Information doubled our probabilities in the relevant region!

🔗 Joint Probability

Definition: Joint Probability

Joint probability is the probability that two (or more) events happen together simultaneously.

\[P(A \text{ AND } B) = P(A, B)\]

This answers: "What's the probability that both A and B occur?"

🎲 Example: Rolling Two Dice

What's the probability of rolling a 3 on the first die AND a 5 on the second die?

\[\begin{align} P(\text{First}=3, \text{Second}=5) &= P(\text{First}=3) \times P(\text{Second}=5)\\ &= \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6}\\ &= \frac{1}{36} \approx 0.028 \end{align}\]

Only about 2.8% chance of getting this specific combination!

🎴 Example: Drawing Cards

You draw two cards from a deck without replacement. What's the probability the first is an Ace AND the second is a King?

\[\begin{align} P(\text{1st Ace, 2nd King}) &= P(\text{1st Ace}) \times P(\text{2nd King | 1st Ace})\\ &= \frac{4}{52} \times \frac{4}{51}\\ &= \frac{16}{2652} \approx 0.006 \end{align}\]

Notice: The second probability changes because we don't replace the first card!

📊 Marginal Probability

Definition: Marginal Probability

Marginal probability is the probability of one event, regardless of other events.

\[P(A) = \sum_{\text{all } b} P(A, B=b)\]

In words: To get the marginal probability, sum out (marginalize over) the other variable.

Why "Marginal"?

The name comes from writing probabilities in a table and summing rows/columns, writing totals in the margins:

Joint Probability Table

Rain (B) No Rain (¬B) Marginal P(A)
Traffic (A) 0.20 0.15 0.35
No Traffic (¬A) 0.10 0.55 0.65
Marginal P(B) 0.30 0.70 1.00

📍 Example: Submarine Location

The submarine is on a 4×4 grid. We have a joint probability distribution over positions [x, y].

Question: What's the probability the x-coordinate is 2, regardless of y?

\[\begin{align} P(X=2) &= P(X=2, Y=0) + P(X=2, Y=1) + P(X=2, Y=2) + P(X=2, Y=3)\\ &= \sum_{y=0}^{3} P(X=2, Y=y) \end{align}\]

We sum across all possible y values to "marginalize out" the y variable.

🎲 Conditional Probability

Definition: Conditional Probability

Conditional probability is the probability of one event given that another event has occurred.

\[P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \text{ AND } B)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(A, B)}{P(B)}\]

Read as: "Probability of A given B"

Intuitive Understanding

Think of conditional probability as restricting your universe:

Venn Diagram Intuition

Total Universe = All Outcomes
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│ ┌─────────┐ │
│ A │ A∩B │ B │
│ │ │ │
│ └─────────┘ │
└─────────────────────────────────┘

When we know B happened:
- Our universe shrinks to just B
- We only care about the overlap A∩B
- P(A|B) = (Size of A∩B) / (Size of B)

🎲 Example: Rolling a Die

You roll a fair six-sided die. Someone tells you the result is an even number.

Question: Given this information, what's the probability it's a 4?

Without information:

\[P(\text{Roll}=4) = \frac{1}{6} \approx 0.167\]

With information (even number):

\[\begin{align} P(\text{Roll}=4 \mid \text{Even}) &= \frac{P(\text{Roll}=4 \text{ AND Even})}{P(\text{Even})}\\ &= \frac{P(\text{Roll}=4)}{P(\text{Even})}\\ &= \frac{1/6}{3/6}\\ &= \frac{1}{3} \approx 0.333 \end{align}\]

The probability doubled! We went from 6 possible outcomes to 3 possible outcomes {2, 4, 6}.

🏥 Example: Medical Testing

Consider a disease that affects 1% of the population.

  • P(Disease) = 0.01
  • P(Positive Test | Disease) = 0.95 (sensitivity)
  • P(Negative Test | No Disease) = 0.90 (specificity)

Question: What does P(Positive Test | Disease) = 0.95 mean?

Answer: Among people who have the disease, 95% will test positive.

This is very different from P(Disease | Positive Test) which we'll explore in Bayes' Rule!

🔗 Key Relationships

The Chain Rule (Product Rule)

Chain Rule of Probability

\[P(A, B) = P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B) = P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)\]

In words: Joint probability = Conditional × Marginal

For multiple events:

\[P(A, B, C) = P(A \mid B, C) \cdot P(B \mid C) \cdot P(C)\]

🎴 Example: Drawing Three Cards

Probability of drawing 3 Aces in a row without replacement:

\[\begin{align} P(\text{3 Aces}) &= P(\text{1st Ace}) \times P(\text{2nd Ace | 1st Ace}) \times P(\text{3rd Ace | 1st, 2nd Aces})\\ &= \frac{4}{52} \times \frac{3}{51} \times \frac{2}{50}\\ &= \frac{24}{132600} \approx 0.00018 \end{align}\]

Very rare – less than 0.02% chance!

Law of Total Probability

Law of Total Probability

If events \(B_1, B_2, \ldots, B_n\) partition the sample space (mutually exclusive and exhaustive):

\[P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A \mid B_i) \cdot P(B_i)\]

In words: Break down A by considering all possible "paths" through which A can occur.

🏭 Example: Factory Production

A factory has three machines:

  • Machine A: Produces 50% of items, 2% defective rate
  • Machine B: Produces 30% of items, 3% defective rate
  • Machine C: Produces 20% of items, 5% defective rate

Question: What's the overall probability a random item is defective?

\[\begin{align} P(\text{Defective}) &= P(D \mid A) \cdot P(A) + P(D \mid B) \cdot P(B) + P(D \mid C) \cdot P(C)\\ &= 0.02 \times 0.50 + 0.03 \times 0.30 + 0.05 \times 0.20\\ &= 0.010 + 0.009 + 0.010\\ &= 0.029 = 2.9\% \end{align}\]

🔓 Independence

Definition: Statistical Independence

Two events A and B are independent if knowing one doesn't change the probability of the other:

\[P(A \mid B) = P(A)\]

Equivalently:

\[P(A, B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)\]

In words: The joint probability factors into the product of marginals.

🎲 Example: Independent Events

Rolling two dice:

  • Event A: First die shows 6
  • Event B: Second die shows 3

These are independent because the first die doesn't affect the second:

\[\begin{align} P(A, B) &= P(A) \cdot P(B)\\ &= \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36} \end{align}\]

🎴 Example: Dependent Events

Drawing two cards without replacement:

  • Event A: First card is an Ace
  • Event B: Second card is an Ace

These are NOT independent:

\[\begin{align} P(B \mid A) &= \frac{3}{51} \approx 0.059\\ P(B) &= \frac{4}{52} \approx 0.077\\ P(B \mid A) &\neq P(B) \quad \text{(Dependent!)} \end{align}\]

Drawing an Ace first reduces the probability of drawing an Ace second!

⚠️ Common Misconception

Mutually Exclusive ≠ Independent

Mutually Exclusive: Events cannot both occur (if A happens, B cannot)

Independent: Events don't affect each other (A happening doesn't change probability of B)

In fact, non-trivial mutually exclusive events are always dependent!

Example: Rolling a die

  • A = "roll is even" = {2, 4, 6}
  • B = "roll is odd" = {1, 3, 5}

These are mutually exclusive: P(A AND B) = 0
But if we know A occurred, P(B|A) = 0 ≠ P(B) = 1/2, so they're dependent!

🌍 Real-World Examples

☔ Example 1: Weather and Traffic

Based on historical data:

  • P(Rain) = 0.30
  • P(Traffic Jam | Rain) = 0.60
  • P(Traffic Jam | No Rain) = 0.20

Question 1: What's the overall probability of a traffic jam?

\[\begin{align} P(\text{Traffic}) &= P(T \mid R) \cdot P(R) + P(T \mid \neg R) \cdot P(\neg R)\\ &= 0.60 \times 0.30 + 0.20 \times 0.70\\ &= 0.18 + 0.14\\ &= 0.32 = 32\% \end{align}\]

Question 2: You observe a traffic jam. What's the probability it's raining?

(This requires Bayes' Rule – see next guide!)

🎓 Example 2: Student Performance

In a class:

  • 40% of students studied hard
  • Of those who studied hard, 90% passed
  • Of those who didn't study hard, 50% passed
\[\begin{align} P(\text{Pass}) &= P(P \mid S) \cdot P(S) + P(P \mid \neg S) \cdot P(\neg S)\\ &= 0.90 \times 0.40 + 0.50 \times 0.60\\ &= 0.36 + 0.30\\ &= 0.66 = 66\% \end{align}\]

66% of students pass overall.

🔍 Example 3: Submarine Search

We have a 16×16 grid. The submarine is equally likely in any square initially.

Initially:

\[P(\text{Submarine at any square}) = \frac{1}{256}\]

After learning submarine is in row y=5:

\[\begin{align} P(\text{Submarine at }[x, 5] \mid Y=5) &= \frac{P([x,5] \text{ AND } Y=5)}{P(Y=5)}\\ &= \frac{1/256}{16/256}\\ &= \frac{1}{16} \end{align}\]

Probability increased 16-fold in that row! Information is powerful.

🎓 Summary: Key Takeaways

  • Joint P(A,B): Probability both events occur
  • Marginal P(A): Probability of A alone (sum over other variables)
  • Conditional P(A|B): Probability of A given B occurred
  • Chain Rule: P(A,B) = P(A|B) · P(B)
  • Independence: P(A|B) = P(A), or equivalently P(A,B) = P(A)·P(B)
  • Law of Total Probability: Break down by all possible conditions
