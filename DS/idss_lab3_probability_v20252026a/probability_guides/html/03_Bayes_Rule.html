🎯 Introduction: Why Bayes' Rule Matters

Bayes' Rule is one of the most important formulas in all of probability and statistics. It's the foundation of:

  • 🔬 Scientific reasoning and hypothesis testing
  • 🤖 Machine learning and artificial intelligence
  • 📊 Medical diagnosis
  • 🎯 Spam filtering and recommendation systems
  • 🧠 Rational decision-making under uncertainty
The Core Idea

Before Evidence: "What do I believe?"
↓
New Evidence Arrives: "What did I observe?"
↓
Bayes' Rule: "How should I update my beliefs?"
↓
After Evidence: "What do I believe now?"

📐 The Formula

Bayes' Rule - Standard Form

\[P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}\]

In words:

\[\text{Posterior} = \frac{\text{Likelihood} \times \text{Prior}}{\text{Evidence}}\]

Bayes' Rule - Expanded Form

Using the Law of Total Probability to expand \(P(B)\):

\[P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A) + P(B \mid \neg A) \cdot P(\neg A)}\]

This form is useful when you don't know \(P(B)\) directly.

Bayes' Rule - Proportional Form

Often we only care about comparing hypotheses, so we can ignore the denominator:

\[P(A \mid B) \propto P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)\]

Read as: "Posterior is proportional to Likelihood times Prior"

Then normalize at the end to ensure probabilities sum to 1.

📖 Understanding the Terminology

Prior: P(A)

What is the Prior?

The prior is your belief before seeing any data or evidence.

Example: "Before testing, I believe the disease prevalence is 1%"

Properties:

  • Represents background knowledge
  • Doesn't depend on the current observation
  • Can be based on expert knowledge, past data, or assumptions

Likelihood: P(B | A)

What is the Likelihood?

The likelihood is the probability of observing the data if the hypothesis were true.

Example: "If someone has the disease, there's a 95% chance the test is positive"

Important: This is NOT \(P(A \mid B)\)! It's \(P(B \mid A)\).

Properties:

  • Depends on your measurement/observation model
  • Higher likelihood = data is more consistent with hypothesis
  • Does NOT have to sum to 1 (it's not a distribution over hypotheses)

Posterior: P(A | B)

What is the Posterior?

The posterior is your updated belief after seeing the data.

Example: "After testing positive, I believe there's an 8.8% chance I have the disease"

Properties:

  • Combines prior knowledge with new evidence
  • IS a valid probability distribution (sums to 1)
  • Can become the prior for the next observation

Evidence: P(B)

What is the Evidence?

The evidence (or marginal likelihood) is the total probability of observing the data.

\[P(B) = P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A) + P(B \mid \neg A) \cdot P(\neg A)\]

Example: "Overall, 10.85% of people test positive"

Properties:

  • Acts as a normalizing constant
  • Same for all hypotheses (when comparing, can be ignored)
  • Ensures posterior is a valid probability distribution

💡 Building Intuition

Why Do We Need Bayes' Rule?

Often we know one conditional probability but need the reverse:

What We Know vs. What We Want

Medical Testing:
✓ We know: P(Positive Test | Disease) = 95%
❓ We want: P(Disease | Positive Test) = ?

Spam Filtering:
✓ We know: P(Word "free" | Spam) = 80%
❓ We want: P(Spam | Word "free") = ?

Weather Prediction:
✓ We know: P(Heavy Traffic | Rain) = 70%
❓ We want: P(Rain | Heavy Traffic) = ?

Bayes' Rule lets us "flip" these conditional probabilities!

🔑 Key Insight

\(P(A \mid B)\) and \(P(B \mid A)\) are generally NOT equal!

Example:

  • P(Positive Test | Disease) = 95% (very high)
  • P(Disease | Positive Test) = 8.8% (much lower!)

This difference comes from the prior probability (base rate).

⚠️ The Base Rate Fallacy

One of the Most Common Mistakes in Reasoning!

The base rate fallacy occurs when people ignore prior probabilities (base rates) and focus only on new information.

🏥 Classic Example: Medical Testing

Scenario:

  • A disease affects 1% of the population: P(Disease) = 0.01
  • Test sensitivity: 95% (P(Positive | Disease) = 0.95)
  • Test specificity: 90% (P(Negative | No Disease) = 0.90)

Question: You test positive. What's the probability you have the disease?

❌ Intuitive (Wrong) Answer: "95% - the test is very accurate!"

✅ Correct Answer using Bayes' Rule:

Step 1: Identify what we know

  • Prior: P(Disease) = 0.01
  • Prior: P(No Disease) = 0.99
  • Likelihood: P(Positive | Disease) = 0.95
  • Likelihood: P(Positive | No Disease) = 0.10 (false positive rate)

Step 2: Calculate evidence P(Positive)

\[\begin{align} P(\text{Positive}) &= P(+ \mid D) \cdot P(D) + P(+ \mid \neg D) \cdot P(\neg D)\\ &= 0.95 \times 0.01 + 0.10 \times 0.99\\ &= 0.0095 + 0.0990\\ &= 0.1085 \end{align}\]

Step 3: Apply Bayes' Rule

\[\begin{align} P(D \mid +) &= \frac{P(+ \mid D) \cdot P(D)}{P(+)}\\ &= \frac{0.95 \times 0.01}{0.1085}\\ &= \frac{0.0095}{0.1085}\\ &= 0.0876 \approx 8.8\% \end{align}\]

Result: Only 8.8% chance of having the disease despite testing positive!

Why so low? The disease is rare (low prior). Most positive tests are false positives from the 99% who don't have the disease.

Understanding with a Population of 10,000

People with disease: 100 (1% of 10,000)
- Test positive: 95 (95% sensitivity)
- Test negative: 5 (5% false negative)

People without disease: 9,900 (99% of 10,000)
- Test positive: 990 (10% false positive)
- Test negative: 8,910 (90% specificity)

Total who test positive: 95 + 990 = 1,085
Of those, actually have disease: 95

Probability = 95 / 1,085 ≈ 8.8%

📊 Most positive tests come from the huge pool of healthy people!

🔄 Sequential Bayesian Updates

One of the most powerful aspects of Bayes' Rule: you can apply it repeatedly!

The Sequential Update Process

Prior₁ → Observe Data₁ → Posterior₁
↓
Posterior₁ becomes Prior₂
↓
Prior₂ → Observe Data₂ → Posterior₂
↓
Posterior₂ becomes Prior₃
↓
And so on...

Each posterior becomes the next prior!

🔍 Example: Submarine Search

Initial State:

Submarine equally likely in any of 256 grid squares

\[P(\text{Location } i) = \frac{1}{256} \quad \text{for all } i\]

Update 1: Search square [10, 7], don't find it

Likelihood of not finding it if it were there: 20% (not perfect detection)

Likelihood of not finding it if it weren't there: 100%

\[\begin{align} P([10,7] \mid \text{not found}) &= \frac{P(\text{not found} \mid [10,7]) \cdot P([10,7])}{P(\text{not found})}\\ &= \frac{0.20 \times (1/256)}{P(\text{not found})}\\ &\text{(decreases)} \end{align}\]

For all other locations:

\[P(\text{other location} \mid \text{not found}) = \frac{1.00 \times (1/256)}{P(\text{not found})} \text{(increases slightly)}\]

Update 2: Search square [8, 9], don't find it

Use the posterior from Update 1 as the new prior, and repeat!

After Many Searches: The probability concentrates on unsearched areas and areas where detection is harder.

🌍 Real-World Examples

☔ Example 1: Rain and Traffic

Given Information:

  • P(Rain) = 0.30 (rains 30% of days)
  • P(Traffic | Rain) = 0.60
  • P(Traffic | No Rain) = 0.20

Question: You observe heavy traffic. What's the probability it's raining?

Step 1: Calculate P(Traffic)

\[\begin{align} P(T) &= P(T \mid R) \cdot P(R) + P(T \mid \neg R) \cdot P(\neg R)\\ &= 0.60 \times 0.30 + 0.20 \times 0.70\\ &= 0.18 + 0.14 = 0.32 \end{align}\]

Step 2: Apply Bayes' Rule

\[\begin{align} P(R \mid T) &= \frac{P(T \mid R) \cdot P(R)}{P(T)}\\ &= \frac{0.60 \times 0.30}{0.32}\\ &= \frac{0.18}{0.32}\\ &= 0.5625 = 56.25\% \end{align}\]

So if you see traffic, there's about a 56% chance it's raining!

📧 Example 2: Spam Filtering

Given Information:

  • P(Spam) = 0.40 (40% of emails are spam)
  • P("FREE" appears | Spam) = 0.70
  • P("FREE" appears | Not Spam) = 0.05

Question: An email contains the word "FREE". What's the probability it's spam?

Solution:

\[\begin{align} P(\text{Spam} \mid \text{"FREE"}) &= \frac{P(\text{"FREE"} \mid \text{Spam}) \cdot P(\text{Spam})}{P(\text{"FREE"})}\\ \\ P(\text{"FREE"}) &= 0.70 \times 0.40 + 0.05 \times 0.60\\ &= 0.28 + 0.03 = 0.31\\ \\ P(\text{Spam} \mid \text{"FREE"}) &= \frac{0.70 \times 0.40}{0.31}\\ &= \frac{0.28}{0.31}\\ &= 0.903 = 90.3\% \end{align}\]

Strong evidence for spam! This is how email filters work.

🎓 Example 3: Student Cheating Detection

Scenario: A student gets a perfect score on a difficult test.

Given:

  • P(Cheat) = 0.02 (2% of students cheat)
  • P(Perfect | Cheat) = 0.90 (cheaters usually get perfect scores)
  • P(Perfect | Not Cheat) = 0.10 (10% of honest students get perfect)

Question: Given a perfect score, what's P(Cheat)?

\[\begin{align} P(C \mid P) &= \frac{P(P \mid C) \cdot P(C)}{P(P \mid C) \cdot P(C) + P(P \mid \neg C) \cdot P(\neg C)}\\ &= \frac{0.90 \times 0.02}{0.90 \times 0.02 + 0.10 \times 0.98}\\ &= \frac{0.018}{0.018 + 0.098}\\ &= \frac{0.018}{0.116}\\ &= 0.155 = 15.5\% \end{align}\]

Only 15.5% - most perfect scores are from honest, skilled students!

🏭 Example 4: Quality Control

A factory has 3 machines:

  • Machine A: 60% of production, 1% defect rate
  • Machine B: 30% of production, 3% defect rate
  • Machine C: 10% of production, 5% defect rate

Question: A defective item is found. Which machine likely made it?

For Machine A:

\[\begin{align} P(A \mid D) &= \frac{P(D \mid A) \cdot P(A)}{P(D)}\\ \\ P(D) &= 0.01 \times 0.60 + 0.03 \times 0.30 + 0.05 \times 0.10\\ &= 0.006 + 0.009 + 0.005 = 0.020\\ \\ P(A \mid D) &= \frac{0.01 \times 0.60}{0.020} = \frac{0.006}{0.020} = 0.30 = 30\% \end{align}\]

For Machine B:

\[P(B \mid D) = \frac{0.03 \times 0.30}{0.020} = \frac{0.009}{0.020} = 0.45 = 45\%\]

For Machine C:

\[P(C \mid D) = \frac{0.05 \times 0.10}{0.020} = \frac{0.005}{0.020} = 0.25 = 25\%\]

Conclusion: Most likely from Machine B (45%), even though it only makes 30% of items!

🎓 Summary: Key Takeaways

\[\boxed{P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}}\]
  • Prior P(A): Initial belief before evidence
  • Likelihood P(B|A): Probability of evidence given hypothesis
  • Posterior P(A|B): Updated belief after evidence
  • Evidence P(B): Normalizing constant

Remember:

  • P(A|B) ≠ P(B|A) in general
  • Base rates (priors) matter enormously
  • You can chain Bayes' updates sequentially
  • Posterior ∝ Likelihood × Prior
