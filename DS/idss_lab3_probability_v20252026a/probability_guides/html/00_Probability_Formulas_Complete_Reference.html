1. Axioms of Probability

The axioms of probability are the fundamental rules that all probability calculations must follow. These are the building blocks of probability theory.

Axiom 1: Boundedness

\[0 \leq P(A) \leq 1\]

What it means: The probability of any event must be between 0 and 1 (inclusive).

  • \(P(A) = 0\) means the event is impossible
  • \(P(A) = 1\) means the event is certain
  • \(P(A) = 0.5\) means the event has a 50% chance

Example: Rolling a Die

  • \(P(\text{rolling a 3}) = \frac{1}{6} \approx 0.167\) ✓ (between 0 and 1)
  • \(P(\text{rolling less than 7}) = 1\) ✓ (certain event)
  • \(P(\text{rolling a 0}) = 0\) ✓ (impossible event)
  • \(P(\text{rolling a 3}) = 1.5\) ✗ (violates axiom!)

Axiom 2: Unitarity (Normalization)

\[\sum_{x} P(x) = 1\]

What it means: The probabilities of all possible outcomes must add up to 1 (100%).

Example: Coin Flip

For a fair coin:

\[P(\text{Heads}) + P(\text{Tails}) = 0.5 + 0.5 = 1\]

For a die:

\[P(1) + P(2) + P(3) + P(4) + P(5) + P(6) = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = 1\]

Axiom 3: Sum Rule

\[P(A \vee B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \wedge B)\]

What it means: The probability that either A or B happens equals the probability of A plus the probability of B, minus the probability that both happen (to avoid double-counting).

Example: Drawing Cards

What's the probability of drawing a King OR a Heart from a standard deck?

  • \(P(\text{King}) = \frac{4}{52}\) (4 kings in deck)
  • \(P(\text{Heart}) = \frac{13}{52}\) (13 hearts in deck)
  • \(P(\text{King AND Heart}) = \frac{1}{52}\) (only King of Hearts)
\[P(\text{King OR Heart}) = \frac{4}{52} + \frac{13}{52} - \frac{1}{52} = \frac{16}{52} \approx 0.308\]

If we didn't subtract the overlap, we'd count the King of Hearts twice!

2. Integration to Unity

This extends the unitarity axiom to both discrete and continuous distributions.

Discrete Case

\[\sum_{i} f_X(x_i) = 1\]

The sum of all probability mass function values equals 1.

Continuous Case

\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x) \, dx = 1\]

The area under the entire probability density function curve equals 1.

Example: Discrete - Die Roll

\[\sum_{i=1}^{6} P(X=i) = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = 1\]

Example: Continuous - Uniform Distribution

For a uniform distribution on [0, 1]:

\[f_X(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } 0 \leq x \leq 1 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\]

Verify integration to unity:

\[\int_{0}^{1} 1 \, dx = [x]_0^1 = 1 - 0 = 1 \, \checkmark\]

3. Expectation

The expectation (or expected value) is the average value you would expect if you repeated an experiment infinitely many times.

Discrete Expectation

\[E[X] = \sum_{x} f_X(x) \cdot x\]

Multiply each value by its probability, then sum them all.

Example: Expected Value of Die Roll

\[\begin{align} E[X] &= 1 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 3 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 4 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 5 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 6 \cdot \frac{1}{6} \\ &= \frac{1+2+3+4+5+6}{6} = \frac{21}{6} = 3.5 \end{align}\]

On average, a die roll gives 3.5 (even though you can never actually roll 3.5!).

Continuous Expectation

\[E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \cdot f_X(x) \, dx\]

Integrate the value times the probability density over all possible values.

Example: Expected Value of Uniform[0, 1]

\[E[X] = \int_{0}^{1} x \cdot 1 \, dx = \left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} - 0 = 0.5\]

The average value from a uniform distribution on [0, 1] is 0.5 (the midpoint).

Expectation of Functions: \(E[g(X)]\)

Discrete:

\[E[g(X)] = \sum_{x} f_X(x) \cdot g(x)\]

Continuous:

\[E[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x) \cdot g(x) \, dx\]

Example: Expected Value of \(X^2\) for a Die

Let \(g(X) = X^2\)

\[\begin{align} E[X^2] &= 1^2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 2^2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 3^2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 4^2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 5^2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 6^2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} \\ &= \frac{1 + 4 + 9 + 16 + 25 + 36}{6} = \frac{91}{6} \approx 15.17 \end{align}\]

🔑 Key Connection

The expectation operator is linear:

\[E[aX + b] = aE[X] + b\]

This property makes many calculations much easier!

4. Odds and Logit

Odds and logit are alternative ways to express probabilities, commonly used in statistics and machine learning.

Odds

\[\text{odds} = \frac{p}{1-p}\]

What it means: The ratio of the probability that something happens to the probability it doesn't happen.

Example: Converting Probability to Odds

  • If \(p = 0.75\) (75% chance):
    \[\text{odds} = \frac{0.75}{1-0.75} = \frac{0.75}{0.25} = 3\]
    This is read as "3 to 1 odds" - three times more likely to happen than not.
  • If \(p = 0.5\) (50% chance):
    \[\text{odds} = \frac{0.5}{0.5} = 1\]
    Even odds (1 to 1).
  • If \(p = 0.2\) (20% chance):
    \[\text{odds} = \frac{0.2}{0.8} = 0.25\]
    Or "1 to 4 odds" - four times more likely NOT to happen.

Logit (Log-Odds)

\[\text{logit}(p) = \log\left(\frac{p}{1-p}\right)\]

What it means: The logarithm of the odds. This transforms probabilities from [0,1] to (-∞, +∞), which is useful for regression models.

Example: Logit Transformation

Probability \(p\) Odds Logit (log odds) Interpretation
0.5 1 0 Even odds
0.73 2.7 1 Moderately likely
0.88 7.3 2 Very likely
0.27 0.37 -1 Moderately unlikely

🔑 Why Use Logit?

  • Logistic Regression: The logit is the link function in logistic regression
  • Unbounded range: Converts [0,1] to (-∞, +∞), easier for linear models
  • Symmetric: logit(p) = -logit(1-p)

5. Conditional Probability

Conditional probability answers: "What's the probability of A, given that B has already occurred?"

Conditional Probability Formula

\[P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \wedge B)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(A, B)}{P(B)}\]

Read as: "Probability of A given B"

What it means: Among all cases where B happens, what fraction also has A happening?

Example: Medical Testing

Consider a disease test:

  • 1% of population has the disease: \(P(\text{Disease}) = 0.01\)
  • Test is positive for 95% of diseased people: \(P(\text{Positive}|\text{Disease}) = 0.95\)
  • Test is positive for 10% of healthy people: \(P(\text{Positive}|\text{Healthy}) = 0.10\)

Question: If you test positive, what's the probability you have the disease?

First, find \(P(\text{Positive AND Disease})\):

\[P(\text{Positive}, \text{Disease}) = P(\text{Positive}|\text{Disease}) \cdot P(\text{Disease}) = 0.95 \times 0.01 = 0.0095\]

Find \(P(\text{Positive})\) total:

\[\begin{align} P(\text{Positive}) &= P(\text{Positive}|\text{Disease}) \cdot P(\text{Disease}) \\ &\quad + P(\text{Positive}|\text{Healthy}) \cdot P(\text{Healthy}) \\ &= 0.95 \times 0.01 + 0.10 \times 0.99 \\ &= 0.0095 + 0.099 = 0.1085 \end{align}\]

Now apply conditional probability:

\[P(\text{Disease}|\text{Positive}) = \frac{P(\text{Positive}, \text{Disease})}{P(\text{Positive})} = \frac{0.0095}{0.1085} \approx 0.088\]

Result: Only 8.8% chance of having the disease even with a positive test! This is because the disease is rare.

🔑 Multiplication Rule

Rearranging the conditional probability formula gives:

\[P(A, B) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B) = P(B|A) \cdot P(A)\]

This is essential for calculating joint probabilities!

6. Marginal Probability

Marginal probability is the probability of an event regardless of the outcome of another variable. We "marginalize out" or "sum out" the other variable.

Discrete Marginalization

\[P(X) = \sum_{y} P(X, Y)\]

Sum over all possible values of Y to get the probability of X alone.

Continuous Marginalization

\[P(X) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P(X, Y) \, dy\]

Integrate over all possible values of Y to get the probability of X alone.

Example: Weather and Traffic

Joint probability table of Weather and Traffic:

Weather \ Traffic Light Heavy Marginal P(Weather)
Sunny 0.35 0.15 0.50
Rainy 0.10 0.40 0.50
Marginal P(Traffic) 0.45 0.55 1.00

To find \(P(\text{Sunny})\), sum across the row:

\[P(\text{Sunny}) = P(\text{Sunny, Light}) + P(\text{Sunny, Heavy}) = 0.35 + 0.15 = 0.50\]

To find \(P(\text{Heavy})\), sum down the column:

\[P(\text{Heavy}) = P(\text{Sunny, Heavy}) + P(\text{Rainy, Heavy}) = 0.15 + 0.40 = 0.55\]

🔑 Connection to Conditional Probability

We can also write marginalization using conditional probabilities:

\[P(X) = \sum_{y} P(X|Y=y) \cdot P(Y=y)\]

This is called the Law of Total Probability!

7. Bayes Rule

Bayes' Rule is one of the most important formulas in probability. It allows us to reverse conditional probabilities.

Bayes' Rule

\[P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}\]

Components:

  • \(P(A|B)\) = Posterior: probability of A given evidence B
  • \(P(B|A)\) = Likelihood: probability of seeing B if A is true
  • \(P(A)\) = Prior: probability of A before seeing B
  • \(P(B)\) = Evidence: total probability of seeing B

🔗 How Bayes Rule Connects to Conditional Probability

Bayes' Rule comes directly from the conditional probability formula!

We know:

\[P(A|B) = \frac{P(A,B)}{P(B)} \quad \text{and} \quad P(B|A) = \frac{P(A,B)}{P(A)}\]

From the second equation: \(P(A,B) = P(B|A) \cdot P(A)\)

Substitute into the first equation:

\[P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}\]

That's Bayes' Rule! ✨

Example: The Medical Test (Revisited)

Using our earlier medical test example with Bayes' Rule:

  • Prior: \(P(\text{Disease}) = 0.01\)
  • Likelihood: \(P(\text{Positive}|\text{Disease}) = 0.95\)
  • Evidence: \(P(\text{Positive}) = 0.1085\) (calculated before)
\[\begin{align} P(\text{Disease}|\text{Positive}) &= \frac{P(\text{Positive}|\text{Disease}) \cdot P(\text{Disease})}{P(\text{Positive})} \\ &= \frac{0.95 \times 0.01}{0.1085} \\ &= \frac{0.0095}{0.1085} \approx 0.088 \end{align}\]

Same answer as before, but now using Bayes' Rule directly!

Example: Spam Email Filter

An email contains the word "winner". Is it spam?

  • \(P(\text{Spam}) = 0.3\) (30% of emails are spam)
  • \(P(\text{"winner"}|\text{Spam}) = 0.4\) (40% of spam contains "winner")
  • \(P(\text{"winner"}|\text{Not Spam}) = 0.05\) (5% of legitimate emails contain "winner")

First, calculate \(P(\text{"winner"})\) using marginalization:

\[\begin{align} P(\text{"winner"}) &= P(\text{"winner"}|\text{Spam}) \cdot P(\text{Spam}) \\ &\quad + P(\text{"winner"}|\text{Not Spam}) \cdot P(\text{Not Spam}) \\ &= 0.4 \times 0.3 + 0.05 \times 0.7 \\ &= 0.12 + 0.035 = 0.155 \end{align}\]

Now apply Bayes' Rule:

\[\begin{align} P(\text{Spam}|\text{"winner"}) &= \frac{P(\text{"winner"}|\text{Spam}) \cdot P(\text{Spam})}{P(\text{"winner"})} \\ &= \frac{0.4 \times 0.3}{0.155} \\ &= \frac{0.12}{0.155} \approx 0.774 \end{align}\]

Result: 77.4% chance the email is spam!

8. Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)

The CDF tells us the probability that a random variable is less than or equal to a specific value.

Cumulative Distribution Function

\[F_X(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f_X(t) \, dt\]

What it means: The CDF accumulates probability from -∞ up to x.

🔑 Key Properties of CDF

  • \(F_X(-\infty) = 0\) (no probability below -∞)
  • \(F_X(+\infty) = 1\) (all probability by +∞)
  • \(F_X(x)\) is non-decreasing (can only go up or stay flat)
  • The PDF is the derivative of the CDF: \(f_X(x) = \frac{d}{dx}F_X(x)\)

Example: Uniform Distribution CDF

For uniform distribution on [0, 1] with \(f_X(x) = 1\):

\[F_X(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } x < 0 \\ \int_{0}^{x} 1 \, dt = x & \text{if } 0 \leq x \leq 1 \\ 1 & \text{if } x > 1 \end{cases}\]

So:

  • \(P(X \leq 0.5) = F_X(0.5) = 0.5\)
  • \(P(X \leq 0.75) = F_X(0.75) = 0.75\)
  • \(P(0.25 < X \leq 0.75) = F_X(0.75) - F_X(0.25) = 0.75 - 0.25 = 0.5\)

Example: Exponential Distribution CDF

For exponential distribution with rate \(\lambda\) and PDF \(f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\):

\[\begin{align} F_X(x) &= \int_{0}^{x} \lambda e^{-\lambda t} \, dt \\ &= \left[-e^{-\lambda t}\right]_0^x \\ &= -e^{-\lambda x} - (-e^0) \\ &= 1 - e^{-\lambda x} \end{align}\]

For \(\lambda = 2\):

  • \(P(X \leq 1) = 1 - e^{-2} \approx 0.865\)
  • \(P(X > 1) = 1 - P(X \leq 1) = e^{-2} \approx 0.135\)

9. Normal Distribution

The normal (Gaussian) distribution is the most important continuous distribution in statistics!

Normal Distribution PDF

\[f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\]

Where:

  • \(\mu\) = mean (center of the distribution)
  • \(\sigma\) = standard deviation (spread of the distribution)
  • \(\sigma^2\) = variance
  • The normalizing constant \(\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\) ensures the area under the curve equals 1

🔑 Why This Formula?

  • \(e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\): The exponential of a squared term creates the bell shape
  • \((x-\mu)^2\): Measures squared distance from the mean
  • \(\sigma^2\): Controls the width (larger \(\sigma\) = wider bell)
  • \(\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\): Normalizes so total probability = 1

Example: IQ Scores

IQ scores follow \(N(100, 15^2)\) - mean 100, standard deviation 15.

The PDF at \(x = 100\):

\[f_X(100) = \frac{1}{15\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(100-100)^2}{2 \times 15^2}} = \frac{1}{15\sqrt{2\pi}} \approx 0.0266\]

The PDF at \(x = 115\) (one standard deviation above mean):

\[f_X(115) = \frac{1}{15\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(115-100)^2}{2 \times 15^2}} = \frac{1}{15\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-0.5} \approx 0.0161\]

🔗 Standard Normal Distribution

When \(\mu = 0\) and \(\sigma = 1\), we get the standard normal \(N(0,1)\):

\[f_Z(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{z^2}{2}}\]

Any normal can be converted to standard normal using:

\[Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\]

This is called standardization or z-score transformation.

🔑 The 68-95-99.7 Rule

For any normal distribution:

  • 68% of data falls within 1 standard deviation of the mean: \([\mu - \sigma, \mu + \sigma]\)
  • 95% of data falls within 2 standard deviations: \([\mu - 2\sigma, \mu + 2\sigma]\)
  • 99.7% of data falls within 3 standard deviations: \([\mu - 3\sigma, \mu + 3\sigma]\)

10. Mean and Variance (Sample Statistics)

When we have data samples, we estimate population parameters using sample statistics.

Sample Mean

\[\hat{\mu} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i\]

The average of all data points. The "hat" (\(\hat{\mu}\)) indicates this is an estimate of the true population mean.

Example: Test Scores

Five students scored: 78, 85, 92, 88, 77

\[\hat{\mu} = \frac{78 + 85 + 92 + 88 + 77}{5} = \frac{420}{5} = 84\]

The average test score is 84.

Sample Variance

\[\hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (x_i - \hat{\mu})^2\]

The average squared deviation from the mean. Measures how spread out the data is.

Note: Sometimes we use \(\frac{1}{N-1}\) instead of \(\frac{1}{N}\) for an unbiased estimate (Bessel's correction).

Example: Test Scores Variance

Using the same test scores with \(\hat{\mu} = 84\):

\[\begin{align} \hat{\sigma}^2 &= \frac{1}{5}\left[(78-84)^2 + (85-84)^2 + (92-84)^2 + (88-84)^2 + (77-84)^2\right] \\ &= \frac{1}{5}\left[36 + 1 + 64 + 16 + 49\right] \\ &= \frac{166}{5} = 33.2 \end{align}\]

Standard deviation: \(\hat{\sigma} = \sqrt{33.2} \approx 5.76\)

🔗 Connection to Expectation

Sample statistics estimate population parameters:

  • Sample mean \(\hat{\mu}\) estimates population mean \(\mu = E[X]\)
  • Sample variance \(\hat{\sigma}^2\) estimates population variance \(\sigma^2 = E[(X-\mu)^2]\)

In fact, variance can be written as:

\[\sigma^2 = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2\]

This connects variance to expectations!

11. How Everything Connects

Now let's see the big picture of how all these formulas relate to each other!

🔗 The Foundation: Axioms → Everything Else

  1. Axioms of Probability are the fundamental rules
  2. From axioms, we derive conditional probability
  3. From conditional probability, we derive Bayes' Rule
  4. From conditional probability, we get marginalization
  5. All distributions must satisfy integration to unity (axiom 2)

🔗 Probability → Distributions → Statistics

\[\text{Axioms} \rightarrow \text{Probability Functions} \rightarrow \text{Distributions} \rightarrow \text{Statistics}\]
  • Axioms tell us rules for probabilities
  • PMF/PDF describe how probability is distributed
  • CDF accumulates probability
  • Expectation summarizes the distribution's center
  • Variance summarizes the distribution's spread
  • Sample statistics estimate these from data

🔗 The Bayes' Rule Chain

Here's how multiple concepts connect in Bayes' Rule:

\[P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{\sum_a P(B|A=a) \cdot P(A=a)}\]

This single formula uses:

  1. Conditional probability: \(P(A|B)\) and \(P(B|A)\)
  2. Joint probability: \(P(B|A) \cdot P(A) = P(A,B)\)
  3. Marginalization: The denominator sums over all \(a\)
  4. Law of total probability: \(P(B) = \sum_a P(B|A=a)P(A=a)\)

🔗 PDF/PMF → CDF → Probabilities

These are different views of the same distribution:

  • PDF/PMF: Point probabilities (discrete) or probability density (continuous)
  • CDF: Cumulative probability up to a point
    \[F_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f_X(t) \, dt\]
  • Probability intervals: Difference of CDFs
    \[P(a < X \leq b) = F_X(b) - F_X(a)\]

🔗 Expectation Connects Everything

Expectation is the bridge between probability and statistics:

Concept Formula with Expectation
Mean \(\mu = E[X]\)
Variance \(\sigma^2 = E[(X-\mu)^2] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2\)
Covariance \(\text{Cov}(X,Y) = E[(X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y)]\)
Moment Generating Function \(M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]\)

🔗 Normal Distribution: The Central Connection

The normal distribution connects to almost everything:

  • Central Limit Theorem: Sums of random variables → normal distribution
  • Maximum Likelihood: For normal data, MLE gives sample mean and variance
  • Standardization: \(Z = \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\) converts to \(N(0,1)\)
  • Error functions: CDF of normal uses error function (erf)
  • Bayesian inference: Normal prior + normal likelihood = normal posterior

🔗 The Complete Picture: A Real Example

Let's trace through a complete analysis using many formulas:

Scenario: A factory produces widgets. Quality depends on machine (A or B).

  1. Prior knowledge (axioms):
    • \(P(\text{Machine A}) = 0.6\), \(P(\text{Machine B}) = 0.4\)
    • These sum to 1 ✓ (unitarity)
  2. Quality distributions (normal distribution):
    • Machine A: \(N(100, 5^2)\)
    • Machine B: \(N(95, 3^2)\)
  3. Expectation:
    \[E[\text{Quality}] = E[\text{Quality}|A] \cdot P(A) + E[\text{Quality}|B] \cdot P(B)\] \[= 100 \times 0.6 + 95 \times 0.4 = 98\]
  4. Observation: A widget has quality score 97. Which machine made it?
  5. Bayes' Rule:
    \[P(A|\text{quality}=97) = \frac{P(\text{quality}=97|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(\text{quality}=97)}\]
  6. Marginalization for denominator:
    \[\begin{align} P(\text{quality}=97) &= P(\text{quality}=97|A) \cdot P(A) \\ &\quad + P(\text{quality}=97|B) \cdot P(B) \end{align}\]
  7. Result: We can now calculate which machine most likely produced this widget!

🔑 The Learning Path

To master probability, study in this order:

  1. Axioms - The foundation
  2. Conditional Probability - Updates probabilities with information
  3. Bayes' Rule - Reverses conditional probabilities
  4. Distributions - PMF, PDF, CDF
  5. Expectation - Summary statistics
  6. Common Distributions - Normal, exponential, etc.
  7. Sample Statistics - Estimating from data
