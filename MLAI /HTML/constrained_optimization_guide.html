🌟 What is Constrained Optimization?

Imagine you want to buy the most delicious pizza possible, but you only have $20 in your wallet. The "delicious pizza" is what you're trying to maximize, and "$20 budget" is your constraint. Constrained optimization helps you find the best pizza you can afford!

The Basic Problem

In Simple Terms:
We want to find the best value (maximum or minimum) of something, but we have rules (constraints) we must follow.
Mathematical Form:

Find the best value of: \( f(\mathbf{x}) \)
While following the rule: \( g(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \) or \( g(\mathbf{x}) \geq 0 \)
🔤 Notation Guide:
  • \( \mathbf{x} \) = The variables we control (like choosing pizza toppings)
  • \( f(\mathbf{x}) \) = What we want to maximize or minimize (deliciousness)
  • \( g(\mathbf{x}) \) = The constraint/rule we must follow (budget limit)
  • \( \lambda \) (lambda) = A special helper number called "Lagrange multiplier"
  • \( \nabla \) (del/nabla) = Shows the direction of steepest increase

⚖️ Equality Constraints: When You Must Be Exactly On Target

You're walking on a mountain and want to reach the highest point, but you MUST stay exactly on a marked trail. You can't leave the trail even a little bit. This is an equality constraint: \( g(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \)

Understanding the Setup

Problem Statement:

Maximize: \( f(\mathbf{x}) \)
Subject to: \( g(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \)

Where \( \mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_D)^T \) is a vector with \( D \) dimensions

🎯 The Secret Trick: The Lagrangian

The Magic Formula:
Instead of solving the constrained problem directly, we create a new function called the Lagrangian:
\[ L(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \lambda g(\mathbf{x}) \]
Why does this work?
Think of \( \lambda \) as a "penalty multiplier". It penalizes solutions that don't satisfy the constraint. When we find where this Lagrangian is stationary (gradient = 0), we automatically get a solution that satisfies the constraint!

📐 Geometric Intuition (The "Why" Behind It)

Visualization: Equality Constraint Problem
🔑 Key Insight:
At the optimal point \( \mathbf{x}^* \), both gradients \( \nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) \) and \( \nabla g(\mathbf{x}^*) \) point in the same direction (or opposite directions). They are parallel!

Why? If they weren't parallel, you could move slightly along the constraint surface to increase \( f \). That would mean you haven't found the maximum yet!

📝 Step-by-Step Solution Process

Step 1: Write the Lagrangian
Combine your objective function and constraint:
\[ L(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \lambda g(\mathbf{x}) \]
Step 2: Take All Partial Derivatives
Find the gradient with respect to all variables:
\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial x_1} = 0, \quad \frac{\partial L}{\partial x_2} = 0, \quad \ldots, \quad \frac{\partial L}{\partial \lambda} = 0 \]
Step 3: Solve the System of Equations
You now have \( D + 1 \) equations (D for \( x_i \), 1 for \( \lambda \)).
Solve them to find \( \mathbf{x}^* \) and \( \lambda^* \).
Step 4: Verify the Solution
Check that your solution actually satisfies the constraint \( g(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0 \)

🎯 Complete Worked Example

Example: Maximize a Circle Subject to a Line
Problem:
Maximize: \( f(x_1, x_2) = 1 - x_1^2 - x_2^2 \) (inverted paraboloid)
Subject to: \( g(x_1, x_2) = x_1 + x_2 - 1 = 0 \) (a line)
Visualization of the Example
Form the Lagrangian:
\[ L(x_1, x_2, \lambda) = 1 - x_1^2 - x_2^2 + \lambda(x_1 + x_2 - 1) \]
Take Partial Derivatives:
\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial x_1} = -2x_1 + \lambda = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x_1 = \frac{\lambda}{2} \] \[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial x_2} = -2x_2 + \lambda = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x_2 = \frac{\lambda}{2} \] \[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial \lambda} = x_1 + x_2 - 1 = 0 \]
Solve for the Variables:
From equations 1 and 2: \( x_1 = x_2 = \frac{\lambda}{2} \)

Substitute into equation 3:
\[ \frac{\lambda}{2} + \frac{\lambda}{2} - 1 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda = 1 \]
Therefore:
\[ x_1^* = \frac{1}{2}, \quad x_2^* = \frac{1}{2}, \quad \lambda^* = 1 \]
Interpret the Result:
The maximum value of \( f(x_1, x_2) \) on the line \( x_1 + x_2 = 1 \) occurs at the point \( (\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}) \).
The maximum value is: \( f(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}) = 1 - \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{2} \)

📊 Inequality Constraints: When You Have a Region to Work With

Now imagine you're on a mountain and can walk anywhere in a fenced area (not just a single path). The fence says "you must stay inside here" - that's \( g(\mathbf{x}) \geq 0 \). The highest point might be inside the area (inactive constraint) or right on the fence (active constraint).

Two Different Scenarios

Inequality Constraint: Two Cases

📍 Case 1: Inactive Constraint

When the constraint is INACTIVE:
The optimal point \( \mathbf{x}^* \) is in the interior where \( g(\mathbf{x}^*) > 0 \)

What happens:
  • The constraint doesn't affect our solution
  • We just maximize \( f(\mathbf{x}) \) normally: \( \nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0 \)
  • The Lagrange multiplier \( \lambda = 0 \)
Think of it this way: If the fence is far away and the peak is in the middle of your area, the fence doesn't matter. You just climb to the peak!

🎯 Case 2: Active Constraint

When the constraint is ACTIVE:
The optimal point \( \mathbf{x}^* \) is on the boundary where \( g(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0 \)

What happens:
  • Similar to equality constraint
  • BUT: The sign of \( \lambda \) matters now!
  • We MUST have \( \lambda > 0 \)
Why must λ > 0?

If \( \lambda < 0 \), the gradient \( \nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) \) would point INTO the feasible region \( g(\mathbf{x}) > 0 \). This means we could increase \( f \) by moving away from the boundary into the interior. But then the boundary wouldn't be optimal!

Therefore, for the boundary to be optimal, we need \( \lambda > 0 \) so that \( \nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) \) points OUT of the feasible region (toward higher values of \( f \) that we can't reach).

🔗 The Combined Solution: Complementary Slackness

The Beautiful Unifying Condition:
\[ \lambda g(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \]
This is called Complementary Slackness. It means:
  • Either \( \lambda = 0 \) (constraint inactive) OR
  • \( g(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \) (constraint active)
  • At least one must be zero!
Complete Conditions for: Maximize \( f(\mathbf{x}) \) subject to \( g(\mathbf{x}) \geq 0 \)

\[ L(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \lambda g(\mathbf{x}) \]
Subject to:
\[ g(\mathbf{x}) \geq 0 \quad \text{(feasibility)} \] \[ \lambda \geq 0 \quad \text{(non-negativity)} \] \[ \lambda g(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \quad \text{(complementary slackness)} \]

🎯 KKT Conditions: The Complete Framework

What are KKT Conditions?
KKT stands for Karush-Kuhn-Tucker. These are the "golden rules" that any optimal solution must follow. Think of them as a checklist to verify if you've found the best solution!

The Four KKT Conditions

1. Stationarity
The gradient of the Lagrangian must be zero:
\[ \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} L(\mathbf{x}^*, \lambda^*) = 0 \]
Meaning: We're at a stationary point (like the top of a hill)
2. Primal Feasibility
The solution must satisfy the constraint:
\[ g(\mathbf{x}^*) \geq 0 \]
Meaning: We're inside (or on) the allowed region
3. Dual Feasibility
The Lagrange multiplier must be non-negative:
\[ \lambda^* \geq 0 \]
Meaning: The constraint either doesn't matter (\(\lambda = 0\)) or pushes us in the right direction (\(\lambda > 0\))
4. Complementary Slackness
Either the constraint is active OR the multiplier is zero:
\[ \lambda^* g(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0 \]
Meaning: We can't have both a non-zero multiplier AND an inactive constraint

⚠️ For Minimization Problems:
When minimizing, the constraint typically uses \( \leq \) instead of \( \geq \). The Lagrangian stays the same form, but the interpretation flips!

🔄 Primal and Dual Problems

Imagine two people looking at the same mountain from opposite sides. One sees it as "climb up" (primal), the other sees it as "don't fall down" (dual). They're looking at the same problem from different perspectives!

What are Primal and Dual?

Primal Problem:
The original problem - maximize \( f(\mathbf{x}) \) with respect to \( \mathbf{x} \)
\[ \mathbf{x}^* = \arg\max_{\mathbf{x}} L(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) \]
Dual Problem:
The "flipped" problem - find the best multiplier \( \lambda \)
\[ d(\lambda) = L(\mathbf{x}^*, \lambda) = \max_{\mathbf{x}} L(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) \] \[ \lambda^* = \arg\min_{\lambda} d(\lambda) \]
Why do we care about the dual?
  • The dual is ALWAYS convex (easier to solve) even if the primal isn't!
  • Sometimes the dual has fewer variables than the primal
  • Many algorithms (like SVM) work better in the dual formulation
  • The dual gives us bounds on the primal solution

🔢 Multiple Constraints: Real-World Complexity

Real life isn't simple! You might want to buy the best laptop but have MULTIPLE constraints: budget ≤ $1000, weight ≤ 3 pounds, battery life ≥ 8 hours, screen size ≥ 15 inches, etc. Multiple constraints are the norm in practical problems!

General Form with Multiple Constraints

Problem: Maximize \( f(\mathbf{x}) \) subject to:
  • J Equality Constraints: \( g_j(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \) for \( j = 1, \ldots, J \)
  • K Inequality Constraints: \( h_k(\mathbf{x}) \geq 0 \) for \( k = 1, \ldots, K \)
Extended Lagrangian: \[ L(\mathbf{x}, \{\lambda_j\}, \{\mu_k\}) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \sum_{j=1}^{J} \lambda_j g_j(\mathbf{x}) + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \mu_k h_k(\mathbf{x}) \]
What This Means:
  • Each equality constraint gets its own multiplier \( \lambda_j \) (any sign)
  • Each inequality constraint gets its own multiplier \( \mu_k \) (must be ≥ 0)
  • We have more equations to solve, but same principle!

🎯 Real-World Example: Portfolio Optimization

Practical Example: Investing Your Money
Scenario: You have $100,000 to invest in stocks. You want to maximize expected return.

Objective:
Maximize: \( f(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i r_i \)
where \( w_i \) = fraction invested in stock \( i \), and \( r_i \) = expected return of stock \( i \)
Constraints:
  1. Equality: Must invest all money: \( \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i = 1 \)
  2. Inequality: No short selling: \( w_i \geq 0 \) for all \( i \)
  3. Inequality: Risk limit: \( \sigma^2(\mathbf{w}) \leq \sigma_{max}^2 \)
  4. Inequality: Diversification: \( w_i \leq 0.3 \) for all \( i \) (max 30% in any stock)

Lagrangian:
\[ L = \sum_i w_i r_i + \lambda\left(1 - \sum_i w_i\right) + \sum_i \mu_i w_i + \nu(\sigma_{max}^2 - \sigma^2) + \sum_i \eta_i(0.3 - w_i) \]

Solution Approach:
  • Take derivatives with respect to all \( w_i \)
  • Apply KKT conditions for each constraint
  • Solve the system to find optimal portfolio weights

📝 Summary: Everything You Need to Remember

🎯 The Big Ideas

  1. Lagrangian: Combines objective + constraints into one function
  2. Lagrange Multipliers: Special numbers that enforce constraints
  3. Equality: Use when you must be exactly on a surface
  4. Inequality: Use when you have a region (active vs inactive)
  5. KKT Conditions: The "checklist" for optimal solutions
  6. Complementary Slackness: Either constraint is active OR multiplier is zero

🔑 Quick Reference Formulas

Equality Constraint:
\[ L(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \lambda g(\mathbf{x}) \] Solve: \( \nabla L = 0 \)
Inequality Constraint:
\[ L(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \lambda g(\mathbf{x}) \] Conditions: \( g(\mathbf{x}) \geq 0, \quad \lambda \geq 0, \quad \lambda g(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \)
