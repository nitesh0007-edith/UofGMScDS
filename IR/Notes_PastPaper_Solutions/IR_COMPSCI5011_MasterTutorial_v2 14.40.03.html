⚡ What is Information Retrieval?

IR is the science of finding relevant information in unstructured data (text, images, video). It is distinct from database retrieval in three ways:

Modern IR: The RAG Connection

IR is at the heart of modern AI systems through Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). When you ask ChatGPT or any LLM a question that requires up-to-date or specific knowledge, an IR system first retrieves relevant passages, then the LLM generates an answer grounded in those passages. Understanding IR is therefore fundamental to understanding modern GenAI pipelines.

Topic 01

IR System Architecture

Every IR system has three core phases: text processing (turning raw text into searchable tokens), indexing (building efficient data structures), and retrieval (finding and ranking matching documents).

1. Text Processing Pipeline

Raw text must be normalised before indexing. The standard pipeline is:

Pipeline
Raw Text → Tokenisation → Stop Word Removal → Stemming / Lemmatisation → Index Terms

Tokenisation

Split text into individual tokens (words). Challenges include: punctuation, hyphens (e-mail vs email), case folding (Apple → apple), and languages without spaces (Chinese, Japanese).

Stop Word Removal

Remove very common, low-information words: the, of, and, to, is, was, in…. These appear in virtually every document, so they contribute nothing to discrimination. Trade-off: removing them can hurt phrase queries like "to be or not to be".

Stemming vs. Lemmatisation

Porter StemmerRule-based suffix strippingcomputing → computOverstemming: "universe" and "university" → "univers"
LemmatisationDictionary lookupran → run, better → goodSlower; requires lexicon

2. The Inverted Index

The inverted index is the core data structure of every IR system. It maps from terms → documents, the reverse of a book index (which maps documents → words).

Structure

Term (df, cf) → Posting List [docID: tf, docID: tf, ...]
australia (df=3, cf=4) → [(Doc1,2), (Doc4,1)] champion (df=1, cf=1) → [(Doc1,1)] france (df=2, cf=2) → [(Doc2,1), (Doc3,1)]

df = document frequency (number of docs containing the term). cf = collection frequency (total occurrences across all docs). tf = term frequency (occurrences in one specific doc).

3. Best-Match Retrieval Algorithm

Given a query, we score documents using the accumulated weights of matching terms:

Algorithm (TAAT)
For each document D: Score(D) = 0
For each query term t:
  Look up posting list for t
  For each (docID, tf) in posting list:
    Score(docID) += weight(t, docID)
Return documents sorted by Score descending

4. Positional Inverted Index

A standard inverted index stores (docID, tf) per term. A positional index additionally stores the exact positions of each term occurrence within each document. This enables phrase queries and proximity matching.

X → [0, <5>]   [1, <3,10>]   [2, <4>]   [9, <3,12>] Y → [1, <4,11>]   [2, <8>]   [9, <4,10>]

Format: [docID, <pos1, pos2, ...>]. So X appears in doc 0 at position 5, in doc 1 at positions 3 and 10, etc.

Bigram Paired Posting Lists

For a bigram "X Y", we need documents where X is immediately followed by Y (Y's position = X's position + 1). We can pre-compute and store this as a paired posting list for efficiency.

The #uwN Operator (Unordered Window)

The #uwN operator counts windows of size N where both query terms appear in any order. A window of size N starting at position p contains tokens at positions p, p+1, ..., p+N-1.

Topic 02

Term Weighting

Not all words are equally informative. Term weighting assigns a numeric importance score to each (term, document) pair. The gold standard is TF-IDF.

Zipf's Law

Word frequency in natural language follows a power law: a small number of words (the, of, and…) occur extremely often, while the vast majority are rare.

Zipf's Law
\[ r \cdot f = k \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad r \cdot P(w_r | C) \approx 0.1 \text{ (English)} \]

where \(r\) = rank of word by frequency, \(f\) = frequency, \(k\) = constant

🎯 Retrieval Implication
  • Very frequent words (stopwords, top ranks) → poor discriminators → low IDF → should be removed or down-weighted
  • Very rare words (hapax legomena — appear once) → too rare to describe content well
  • Medium-frequency words → most useful for retrieval
PAST PAPER 2025 – Q2a

Zipf's Law Calculation

Q: If Zipf's law holds with \(r \cdot P(w_r|C) = 0.1\), what proportion of word occurrences are removed if we ignore the 4 most frequent words?

1
Find probability of each top-4 word:
\(P(w_1|C) = 0.1/1 = 0.100\),   \(P(w_2|C) = 0.1/2 = 0.050\)
\(P(w_3|C) = 0.1/3 \approx 0.0333\),   \(P(w_4|C) = 0.1/4 = 0.025\)
2
Sum these probabilities:
\(0.100 + 0.050 + 0.0333 + 0.025 = 0.2083\)
✔ ANSWER
Removing the 4 most frequent words eliminates approximately 20.83% of all word occurrences from the collection.

Heaps' Law (Vocabulary Growth)

Heaps' Law
\[ V = K \cdot n^{\beta} \]

\(V\) = vocabulary size, \(n\) = number of tokens in corpus, \(K \approx 10\text{–}100\), \(\beta \approx 0.5\)

Vocabulary grows sublinearly — each new document adds fewer new words than the last. Useful for estimating index size before building it.

PAST PAPER 2024 – Q2b

Heaps' Law: Estimate Vocabulary Size

A collection has 10⁶ tokens total. The first 64 tokens gave V=40 terms; the first 2500 tokens gave V=250 terms. Use β=0.5 to estimate full vocabulary (√2500=50).

1
Find K using one data point: V = K·n^0.5
At n=64: 40 = K·√64 = K·8 → K = 40/8 = 5
Check at n=2500: V = 5·√2500 = 5·50 = 250 ✅ (consistent!)
2
Estimate full collection vocabulary:
V = 5 · √(10⁶) = 5 · 1000 = 5000 unique terms
✔ ANSWER
Estimated vocabulary size = 5000 terms for the million-token collection.

Term Frequency (TF)

Raw TF vs. Log TF
\[ \text{Raw TF: } w(t,d) = \text{tf}(t,d) \] \[ \text{Log TF: } w(t,d) = \begin{cases} 1 + \log(\text{tf}(t,d)) & \text{if } \text{tf}(t,d) > 0 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Why log TF? Ten occurrences of a term in a document is not 10× more evidence than one occurrence — relevance saturates. Log dampens this effect.

Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)

IDF Formula
\[ \text{idf}(t) = \log \frac{N}{\text{df}(t)} \]

\(N\) = total documents in collection, \(\text{df}(t)\) = number of documents containing term \(t\)

Intuition: A term appearing in only 1 out of 1000 documents is a much stronger signal of relevance than one appearing in 999 out of 1000. IDF quantifies this rarity.

TF-IDF

TF-IDF Weight
\[ w(t,d) = \text{tf}(t,d) \times \log \frac{N}{\text{df}(t)} \] \[ \text{With log TF: } w(t,d) = (1 + \log \text{tf}(t,d)) \times \log \frac{N}{\text{df}(t)} \]

How IDF Changes in Different Scenarios

This conceptual question appeared in the 2022 exam for 4 marks. Understand the formula \(\text{idf}(t) = \log(N/df(t))\) and what changes N vs df(t).

PAST PAPER 2022 – Q3a

IDF Changes in 3 Scenarios

①
Adding word w to one document (that didn't contain it):
df(w) increases by 1. N unchanged.
idf = log(N/(df+1)) < log(N/df) → IDF decreases.
The word is now in more docs, so it's slightly less discriminating.
②
Making each document twice as long (concatenate with itself):
df(w) unchanged — if a doc contained w before, it still does. N unchanged.
idf = log(N/df) → IDF stays the same.
IDF is about how many documents contain the term, not how often. Doubling doesn't change which docs contain w.
③
Adding new documents that do NOT contain w:
N increases by k. df(w) unchanged.
idf = log((N+k)/df) > log(N/df) → IDF increases.
Collection grew but w appears in the same number of docs → relatively rarer → more discriminating.
Topic 03

Vector Space Model

The Vector Space Model (VSM) represents every document and query as a vector in a high-dimensional space where each dimension corresponds to a unique term. Documents that are semantically similar will point in the same direction.

🔑 Core Intuition

If document D contains terms "machine" and "learning" frequently, its vector points in a direction "towards" those terms. A query about machine learning will have a vector pointing in the same direction → high similarity → ranked high.

Cosine Similarity

We measure the angle between vectors, not their magnitudes. This normalises for document length (a 10-page and 1-page document about the same topic should score similarly).

Cosine Similarity
\[ \cos(q, d) = \frac{\vec{q} \cdot \vec{d}}{|\vec{q}||\vec{d}|} = \frac{\sum_{i} w_{q_i} \cdot w_{d_i}}{\sqrt{\sum_i w_{q_i}^2} \cdot \sqrt{\sum_i w_{d_i}^2}} \]

Range: 0 (orthogonal = unrelated) to 1 (identical direction = same topic)

Similarity Measures Compared

MeasureFormulaNotes
Dot Product\(\sum_i w_{q_i} \cdot w_{d_i}\)No length normalisation; biases long docs
Cosine\(\frac{\vec{q}\cdot\vec{d}}{|\vec{q}||\vec{d}|}\)Standard choice; normalised
Dice\(\frac{2|X \cap Y|}{|X|+|Y|}\)Normalises by total non-zero entries

Topic 04

Evaluation Methodology

How do we know if one IR system is better than another? We use test collections (documents + queries + relevance judgments) and standard metrics.

The Cranfield Paradigm (TREC)

A test collection has three components: (1) a corpus of documents, (2) a set of queries/topics, (3) relevance judgements (qrels) — which docs are relevant to which queries. TREC (Text REtrieval Conference, NIST) established this methodology in 1992.

Core Metrics

Precision & Recall
\[ \text{Precision} = \frac{|Relevant \cap Retrieved|}{|Retrieved|} \quad \text{(of what I found, how much is correct?)} \] \[ \text{Recall} = \frac{|Relevant \cap Retrieved|}{|Relevant|} \quad \text{(of what exists, how much did I find?)} \] \[ F_1 = \frac{2 \cdot P \cdot R}{P + R} \quad \text{(harmonic mean)} \]
Precision at Rank K   (P@K)
\[ P@k = \frac{\text{Number of relevant documents in top } k}{k} \]

Popular in web search (P@10 = precision of first page). Does NOT consider position within top k.

Average Precision (AP)
\[ AP = \frac{1}{|R|} \sum_{k=1}^{n} P(k) \cdot \text{rel}(k) \]

\(|R|\) = total relevant documents, \(\text{rel}(k)\) = 1 if doc at rank k is relevant, else 0. Non-retrieved relevant docs contribute 0.

Mean Average Precision (MAP)
\[ MAP = \frac{1}{|Q|} \sum_{q \in Q} AP(q) \]

Averages AP over all queries. Single number summarising system effectiveness. Most widely used TREC metric.

Discounted Cumulative Gain (DCG) & NDCG — for graded relevance
\[ DCG@k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{2^{rel(i)} - 1}{\log_2(i+1)} \] \[ NDCG@k = \frac{DCG@k}{IDCG@k} \]

IDCG = DCG of perfect (ideal) ranking. NDCG ∈ [0,1]. Handles graded relevance (0,1,2,3) and discounts lower positions.

Recall-Precision Graphs & Interpolated Precision

A recall-precision graph plots precision vs recall as you go deeper into a ranked list. Because precision fluctuates, we compute interpolated precision at standard recall levels (0, 0.1, 0.2, ..., 1.0).

Interpolated Precision at Recall r
\[ P_{interp}(r) = \max_{r' \geq r} P(r') \]

The interpolated precision at recall level r is the maximum precision achieved at any recall level ≥ r. This smooths out the jagged curve.

Rocchio Algorithm

The classic relevance feedback method. Geometrically: move the query vector toward relevant documents and away from non-relevant ones.

Rocchio Formula
\[ \vec{q}_{new} = \alpha \vec{q}_{orig} + \frac{\beta}{|D_R|}\sum_{d \in D_R} \vec{d} - \frac{\gamma}{|D_{NR}|}\sum_{d \in D_{NR}} \vec{d} \]

Typical: α=1, β=0.75, γ=0.15  |  \(D_R\) = relevant docs, \(D_{NR}\) = non-relevant docs

Pseudo-Relevance Feedback

Run the query → assume top-k retrieved docs are relevant → apply Rocchio → re-run expanded query. Fully automatic but risky: if the first retrieval is bad, feedback amplifies errors ("query drift").

Why Positive Feedback > Negative Feedback

PAST PAPER 2022 – Q3b

Explain Why Positive Feedback is More Useful

✔ MODEL ANSWER (4 marks)

Positive feedback (marking documents as relevant) directly tells the system what the user is looking for. In Rocchio, the positive centroid term \(\frac{\beta}{|D_R|}\sum_{d \in D_R}\vec{d}\) pulls the query toward relevant documents — this has a clear, targeted direction. Each positive example enriches the query with new discriminating terms.

Negative feedback is less useful because: (1) the non-relevant space is vast and diffuse — there are far more ways to be non-relevant than relevant; (2) moving the query away from non-relevant documents has no clear beneficial direction; (3) it can push the query into unintended regions. For example, in a search for "jaguar the animal", marking a document about "Jaguar cars" as non-relevant might push the query away from all car-related terms — but also might push it away from terms shared by both meanings (like "speed", "power").

In Rocchio, \(\gamma\) (weight on negative feedback) is typically much smaller than \(\beta\) (0.15 vs 0.75) precisely because positive feedback is more informative.

Topic 06

BM25 & Language Models

Probability Ranking Principle

Robertson (1977): "If a system ranks documents in decreasing order of probability of relevance, the overall effectiveness will be the best obtainable." This is the theoretical motivation for probabilistic models.

4 Key Components of Advanced Retrieval Models

This question appeared in 2025 Q3a (4 marks): "List four key components shared by most advanced retrieval models such as BM25 or PL2."

PAST PAPER 2025 – Q3a

4 Components of Advanced Models

✔ MODEL ANSWER
  1. Term Frequency (TF) component: Captures how often a term appears in the document. More occurrences = stronger evidence of relevance. BM25 uses a saturating TF; PL2 models TF via a Poisson distribution.
  2. Document Frequency / IDF component: Captures how rare/informative the term is across the collection. Common terms (high df) are down-weighted; rare terms are up-weighted.
  3. Document Length Normalisation: Longer documents are penalised to avoid biasing towards verbosity. A long document may simply repeat terms many times without being more topically relevant. BM25 uses the b parameter; PL2 uses a normalisation factor based on average document length.
  4. Query Term Weighting: Some models apply weights to query terms (e.g., IDF of the query term, TF of query term for long queries). This recognises that not all query terms are equally important.

Divergence From Randomness (DFR / PL2)

DFR is a family of models (including PL2, used by the Terrier system) based on the idea: "a term is informative if its frequency in a document diverges from what would be expected by a random process."

DFR Framework
\[ w(t,D) = -\log_2(Prob_1) \times (1 - Prob_2) \]

Prob₁ = probability of observing term frequency by chance (e.g., Poisson) | Prob₂ = risk of the term being a good descriptor (normalisation). PL2 uses Poisson for Prob₁.

BM25 (Okapi BM25)

The most important classical retrieval model. Used by almost every production search system as a baseline. It extends the Binary Independence Model with TF saturation and document length normalisation.

BM25 Formula
\[ BM25(D, Q) = \sum_{t \in Q} IDF(t) \cdot \frac{tf(t,D) \cdot (k_1 + 1)}{tf(t,D) + k_1 \left(1 - b + b \cdot \frac{|D|}{avgdl}\right)} \] \[ IDF(t) = \log \frac{N - df(t) + 0.5}{df(t) + 0.5} \]

Typical: k₁ = 1.2, b = 0.75

Understanding Each Parameter

ParameterEffectExtreme Values
k₁ (TF saturation)Controls how fast TF contribution saturatesk₁=0 → IDF only; k₁→∞ → raw TF×IDF
b (length norm)Controls penalty for long documentsb=0 → no length penalty; b=1 → full normalisation
🔑 TF Saturation Intuition

A document mentioning "python" 100 times is not 100× more relevant than one mentioning it once. BM25's TF term approaches an asymptote as tf grows, controlled by k₁. This is one of BM25's key improvements over raw TF-IDF.

Language Models for IR

Key idea: Instead of asking "how relevant is D to Q?", ask "if document D were the source of language, how likely is it that query Q was generated from that language?" Rank by \(P(Q|D)\).

Query Likelihood
\[ P(Q|D) = \prod_{t \in Q} P(t|D) \] \[ \hat{P}_{MLE}(t|D) = \frac{tf(t,D)}{|D|} \quad \text{(maximum likelihood estimate)} \]

Problem: If any query term has tf=0 in D, the whole product becomes 0! Solution: smoothing.

Smoothing Methods
Jelinek-Mercer (linear interpolation): \[ P(t|D) = \lambda \cdot \frac{tf(t,D)}{|D|} + (1-\lambda) \cdot P(t|C) \]
Dirichlet Prior: \[ P(t|D) = \frac{tf(t,D) + \mu \cdot P(t|C)}{|D| + \mu} \]

\(P(t|C) = cf(t)/|C|\) = collection language model. Dirichlet μ ≈ 2000. Short docs get more smoothing (pulled more toward collection average).

BIM: Binary Independence Model

Documents are binary vectors (term present/absent). Terms are assumed independent. When no relevance info is available, assume p_i = 0.5 and estimate s_i = df_i/N, which reduces to IDF weighting.

BIM — Retrieval Status Value (RSV)
\[ RSV(D) = \sum_{i: d_i=1} \log \frac{p_i(1-s_i)}{s_i(1-p_i)} \]

\(p_i\) = P(term present | relevant), \(s_i\) = P(term present | non-relevant). Sum only over query terms present in document (\(d_i=1\)).

BIM with Relevance Feedback (Full Estimation)

When we know which documents are relevant, we can estimate p_i and s_i properly instead of using p_i=0.5.

BIM — Estimating Probabilities from Relevance Judgements
\[ p_i = \frac{r_i + 0.5}{R + 1} \quad s_i = \frac{df_i - r_i + 0.5}{N - R + 1} \]

R = total relevant docs in collection | r_i = number of relevant docs containing term i | df_i = total docs containing term i | N = total docs. The +0.5 and +1 are Laplace smoothing to avoid zero probabilities.

Topic 07

Learning to Rank

Modern search engines have hundreds of signals (BM25 score, PageRank, title match, recency…). Learning to Rank (LTR) uses machine learning to combine these features optimally, learning from past user behaviour and relevance judgements.

The Cascaded Ranking Pipeline

Typical Production Pipeline
Billions of docs → Boolean filter → Thousands → BM25 → ~100 docs → LTR re-ranking → ~10 results → Present to user

Feature Types

CategoryExamples
Query-dependentBM25 score, TF-IDF, field match (title/body/URL)
Query-independentPageRank, document length, URL depth, spam score
Query featuresQuery length, query type, number of terms
User/interactionClick-through rate, session data

Three Approaches to LTR

1. Pointwise

Each (query, document) pair is treated independently. Predict a relevance score or label. Uses standard regression/classification. Problem: ignores relative ordering between documents — a model could score all docs 0.99 and still have a terrible ranking.

2. Pairwise

Given two documents d_i and d_j for the same query, learn to predict which should rank higher. Reduces ranking to binary classification of pairs. Algorithms: RankSVM, RankNet, LambdaMART.

Much better than pointwise for ranking, but still doesn't directly optimise MAP or NDCG.

3. Listwise

Considers the entire ranked list. Directly optimises a ranking metric like NDCG. Algorithms: ListNet, AdaRank, LambdaRank.

Most theoretically sound but hardest to implement and optimise.

LambdaMART — The Industry Standard

Combines LambdaRank (gradient-based relevance) with MART (Multiple Additive Regression Trees = gradient boosted trees). Used by Bing, Yahoo, and most major search engines. Implicitly optimises NDCG through lambda gradients without directly computing it (making training tractable).

💡 Linear LTR Limitation (2024 Q3a)

A linear LTR model (e.g., score = w₁·BM25 + w₂·PageRank + w₃·Proximity) assumes a linear combination of features determines relevance. This is problematic because features can interact non-linearly — a page with very high PageRank and moderate BM25 might be more relevant than the linear model predicts. Tree-based models (LambdaMART) handle such interactions naturally.

2025 Q3b(ii) — E-Commerce LTR

Ranking Products by Revenue

CEO wants to promote higher-margin brands when multiple products are equally relevant.

✔ SOLUTION
Add a query-independent feature to the LTR feature vector: "profit margin" or "revenue contribution" of each product/brand. During training, the learning algorithm will learn the weight for this feature alongside relevance features. The LTR model will then naturally trade off relevance and profitability. Alternatively, add a business objective component to the loss function (a bi-criteria optimisation).

PageRank

Invented by Brin and Page (1998). Models a "random surfer" following hyperlinks. PageRank = the probability that this surfer is at your page at any random moment.

PageRank Formula
\[ PR(A) = (1-d) + d \sum_{T \xrightarrow{} A} \frac{PR(T)}{C(T)} \]

\(d\) = damping factor (typically 0.85) | \(C(T)\) = number of outlinks from page T | \((1-d)\) = probability of teleportation (random jump)

3-Page Graph Iteration

Links: A→B, A→C, B→C, C→A. Damping d=0.85. N=3 pages.

Init
PR(A) = PR(B) = PR(C) = 1/3 ≈ 0.333
Iter 1
PR(A) = 0.15 + 0.85 × [PR(C)/1]   (only C links to A)
= 0.15 + 0.85 × 0.333 = 0.15 + 0.283 = 0.433

PR(B) = 0.15 + 0.85 × [PR(A)/2]   (A links to B and C, so A's PR split)
= 0.15 + 0.85 × 0.167 = 0.15 + 0.142 = 0.292

= 0.15 + 0.85 × (0.167 + 0.333) = 0.15 + 0.85 × 0.5 = 0.15 + 0.425 = 0.575

Continue iterating until convergence (~50–100 iterations in practice).
Sum of all PageRanks ≈ N = 3 (conservation of probability).
Higher PR = more authoritative page.

Matrix-Based Alternative for HITS

✔ METHOD
Let A be the adjacency matrix (A[i][j]=1 if i→j). Then:
Authority scores: \(\vec{a} = A^T A \vec{a}\) — solve as principal eigenvector of \(A^T A\)

Topic 09

Neural Information Retrieval

Traditional IR relies on lexical matching (exact term overlap). Neural IR enables semantic matching — understanding that "car" and "automobile" are related, or that "Apple" in context might mean the company, not the fruit.

Model Taxonomy

1. Sparse Lexical (Traditional)

TF-IDF, BM25. Encoder = term frequency vector. Combiner = dot product. Can use inverted index. O(|d|) encoding, O(1) combination.

2. Learned Sparse Models

Use neural networks (BERT) to predict term importance weights instead of using raw TF. Examples: DeepCT (better weights, same inverted index), SPLADE (can expand to terms not in the document).

3. Dense Bi-Encoders

Encode query and document into dense vectors independently. Score = dot product of dense vectors. Documents pre-encoded offline. At query time, find nearest neighbours using FAISS (Approximate Nearest Neighbour).

Bi-Encoder Score
\[ \text{score}(q, d) = \Phi_Q(q) \cdot \Phi_D(d) \]

DPR, ANCE, ColBERT are examples. Fast retrieval but lower quality than cross-encoders.

4. Cross-Encoders

Process query and document together through a single model. Full token-level interaction. Most effective but very expensive — cannot pre-compute document representations.

Cross-Encoder Score
\[ \text{score}(q, d) = \text{BERT}([CLS] \; q \; [SEP] \; d \; [SEP]) \]

Cost: O((|q|+|d|)²). Used only for re-ranking small candidate sets (top 100–1000).

5. Late Interaction (ColBERT)

Encode query and document separately (like bi-encoder) but keep token-level representations. MaxSim: for each query token, find its maximum similarity to any document token.

ColBERT MaxSim
\[ \text{score}(q,d) = \sum_{i \in q} \max_{j \in d} \text{sim}(\vec{q}_i, \vec{d}_j) \]

Between bi-encoder (fast) and cross-encoder (accurate) in both speed and quality.

PAST PAPER 2024 – Q3c(i)

Why is Recall@1000 p-value NaN for reranker?

✔ ANSWER
The reranker pipeline is: first_stage >> get_text >> MonoT5(). The reranker only re-orders the top 1000 documents retrieved by first_stage — it cannot add new documents to the result set. Therefore, Recall@1000 is identical for both first_stage and reranker on every query. The paired t-test has zero variance in the differences, producing an undefined (NaN) test statistic. It's statistically meaningless to compare them on this metric.
PAST PAPER 2022 – Q3c

Limitation of BM25 Re-ranking Strategy

Neural re-ranking over BM25's top-k has a critical limitation.

✔ ANSWER
Key limitation: If a relevant document is not retrieved in BM25's top-k, the neural re-ranker never sees it — it cannot recover it. This is the recall ceiling problem: the re-ranker is bounded by first-stage recall.

Solution: Use dense retrieval (bi-encoder) as the first stage instead of or alongside BM25. Dense retrieval uses semantic matching and can retrieve relevant documents that share no lexical overlap with the query. Hybrid retrieval (BM25 + dense) further improves first-stage recall by combining lexical and semantic signals.

Dense Retrieval and Long Documents

PAST PAPER 2023 – Q3d (7 marks)

Why Dense Retrieval Struggles with Long Documents

Problem
Dense bi-encoders compress an entire document into a single fixed-size vector (e.g., 768 dimensions from BERT's [CLS] token). For a long document (thousands of tokens), this single vector cannot capture all the information — early content dominates the representation, and important content buried later in the document is lost. BERT itself also has a token limit (~512 tokens), so long documents cannot even be processed in one pass.
Solution
Split documents into short passages (e.g., 100-word chunks with overlap). Encode each passage separately. At query time, retrieve relevant passages and aggregate their scores to get a document-level score.
✔ Two Passage Aggregation Methods
  1. MaxPassage: The document's relevance score = the score of its most relevant passage. Intuition: "A document is as good as its best piece of evidence." This works well when relevance is concentrated in one part of the document. However, for a long document where relevant content is spread throughout, this may miss documents that are consistently moderately relevant — it rewards concentrated relevance over distributed relevance.
  2. SumPassage (or AveragePassage): The document's score = sum (or average) of all passage scores. Intuition: "Multiple relevant passages provide stronger evidence than one." This better handles long documents with relevant content spread through them — each relevant passage contributes to the final score. However, it can be biased towards longer documents that happen to have many passages.

For a document with relevant content spread throughout: SumPassage is generally better — it accumulates evidence across all relevant passages rather than being limited to a single highest-scoring one. MaxPassage would find only the single best passage and might miss the cumulative evidence.

Topic 10

IR Infrastructure

Users expect sub-second results. A 0.5-second delay measurably hurts business metrics. At web scale, 1% increase in response time can require 1000 additional servers. Efficiency is not optional.

Query Evaluation Strategies

TAAT (Term-At-A-Time)

  • Process one term's entire posting list before the next
  • Accumulate scores in a document-score array (one entry per doc)
  • Memory: O(N) accumulators (one per document in corpus)
  • Advantage: Sequential, cache-friendly posting list access
  • Disadvantage: Large memory footprint

DAAT (Document-At-A-Time)

  • Process all terms for one document before moving to next
  • Traverse posting lists in parallel (sorted by doc ID)
  • Memory: O(K) for top-K heap + O(|terms|) for current pointers
  • Advantage: Small memory footprint
  • Disadvantage: Requires all posting lists sorted by docID
PAST PAPER 2025 – Q2d

TAAT vs DAAT — Exact Steps

Query with 2 terms:
term1 → [id=2, tf=2], [id=5, tf=1], [id=6, tf=2]
term2 → [id=2, tf=4], [id=3, tf=5], [id=5, tf=5]

TAAT
Step 1: Process term1's entire list → Score[2] += w(2), Score[5] += w(1), Score[6] += w(2)
Step 2: Process term2's entire list → Score[2] += w(4), Score[3] += w(5), Score[5] += w(5)
Memory: Accumulator array of size N (one per doc). For K results, sort top K.
DAAT
Process docs in docID order:
DocID=2: Present in term1 (tf=2) and term2 (tf=4) → compute total score → add to top-K heap
DocID=3: Only in term2 (tf=5) → compute score → check heap
DocID=5: In term1 (tf=1) and term2 (tf=5) → compute score → check heap
DocID=6: Only in term1 (tf=2) → compute score → check heap
Memory: Only K-element heap + 2 posting list pointers (one per term).
✔ KEY DIFFERENCE
TAAT needs O(N) memory (all docs). DAAT needs O(K) memory (just top K). For large corpora, DAAT is far more practical. Both produce identical results.

Dynamic Pruning: MaxScore & WAND

Why score documents that can't possibly make the top-K? Skip them entirely.

PAST PAPER 2023 – Q3b (4 marks)

Why Upper Bounds Facilitate Efficiency

"Explain why recording the upper bound contribution of a weighting model on a given posting list facilitates techniques that improve search efficiency."

✔ MODEL ANSWER

For each term t, we precompute UB(t) = the maximum possible score contribution of term t to any document — i.e., the highest tf×idf or BM25 partial score that any single document in t's posting list could receive.

During query processing, we maintain a threshold θ = the score of the Kth-best document seen so far (the lowest score in the top-K heap). For a candidate document D, the maximum score it could possibly achieve is the sum of UB(t) for all remaining unprocessed terms. If this sum plus D's accumulated score so far is still less than θ, then D cannot enter the top-K regardless of the remaining terms — so we skip it entirely without evaluating it.

Concrete example: Query has terms t1, t2, t3 with UB(t1)=5, UB(t2)=3, UB(t3)=1. Our current θ=7. Processing t1: document D scores 2 from t1. Maximum possible from t2+t3 = 3+1=4. Total max possible = 2+4=6 < θ=7 → skip D. We never need to look up t2 or t3 for D.

This is the MaxScore algorithm (Turtle & Flood, 1995). WAND extends this with pivot-based skipping to efficiently advance posting list pointers to the next possible candidate, skipping entire blocks at once.

WANDPivot-based: uses UBs to advance ALL posting list pointers simultaneously to next viable candidate.Aggressive — skips entire blocks of posting list entries

A/B Testing

Randomly split users into Control (A) and Treatment (B). Measure metrics (CTR, abandonment, session length). Run statistical test (t-test, p < 0.05).

📝 Important

Run A/A tests first as a sanity check — two identical systems should show no significant difference. Demonstrates the measurement apparatus is working correctly. A/B testing demonstrates causality (unlike correlation-based offline evaluation).

Team-Draft Interleaving — Step by Step

The most commonly used interleaving method. Combines two ranking lists fairly:

Team-Draft Interleaving Algorithm

How It Works

System A ranking: [d1, d2, d3, d4, d5]. System B ranking: [d2, d1, d4, d3, d5].

1
Flip a coin to decide who picks first. Say A goes first.
2
A picks its top document not yet in list → d1. Interleaved: [d1]. Team A = {d1}.
B picks its top not yet in list → d2 (d2 is B's top, not yet selected). Interleaved: [d1, d2]. Team B = {d2}.
A picks → d2 already taken, so A picks d3 (A's next). Team A = {d1, d3}. Interleaved: [d1, d2, d3].
B picks → d1 taken, B picks d4. Team B = {d2, d4}. Interleaved: [d1, d2, d3, d4].
Continue until list is full.
3
User sees the interleaved list. They click on e.g. d1 and d4.
d1 ∈ Team A → A gets a click. d4 ∈ Team B → B gets a click. Score: A=1, B=1 → tie for this query.
✔ Why Interleaving is More Sensitive than A/B
Each user sees both systems simultaneously — it's a within-subject comparison. A/B requires different users to see different systems (between-subject). Within-subject designs have lower variance because user preferences cancel out, meaning you need far fewer queries to detect a significant difference (typically 100–1000× fewer than A/B).

A/B Testing

  • ✅ Demonstrates causality
  • ✅ Can test any UI change, not just ranking
  • ✅ Measures absolute metrics (CTR, revenue)
  • ❌ Needs large user base
  • ❌ Slow (users only see one system)

Interleaving

  • ✅ ~100–1000× more sensitive
  • ✅ Each user sees both systems (within-subject)
  • ✅ Unbiased by fair interleaving
  • ❌ Only compares rankings (not UI)
  • ❌ No absolute metrics — only relative preference

📝 Click Model Details

Cascade Model: User examines results top-down. At rank i, clicks with P(click_i) = P(relevant_i) × P(examine_i). After a click, P(stop) = some probability. Below the clicked document, P(examine) drops.

Position-Based Model (PBM): Separates examination from relevance. P(click) = P(examine_i) × P(relevant | document). P(examine_i) depends only on rank position — debiasing means estimating P(relevant) separately from P(examine_i).

Design Experiments to Verify Hybrid Improvement

"Propose two experiments to verify that the hybrid engine improves over using only sparse or only dense as first-stage."

✔ MODEL ANSWER
# Define the three pipelines
sparse_pipeline = lexical >> reranker
dense_pipeline  = dense   >> reranker
hybrid_pipeline = (lexical + dense) >> reranker

# Experiment 1: Compare hybrid vs sparse-only
pt.Experiment(
    [sparse_pipeline, hybrid_pipeline],
    topics,
    qrels,
    eval_metrics=["ndcg_cut_10", "map", "recall_1000"],
    names=["BM25+Reranker", "Hybrid+Reranker"],
    baseline=0   # compare vs sparse_pipeline
)

# Experiment 2: Compare hybrid vs dense-only
pt.Experiment(
    [dense_pipeline, hybrid_pipeline],
    topics,
    qrels,
    eval_metrics=["ndcg_cut_10", "map"],
    names=["Dense+Reranker", "Hybrid+Reranker"],
    baseline=0
)

Justification of choices:

  • Why NDCG@10? Users see top-10 results; NDCG@10 measures quality at that cut-off and handles graded relevance. Most directly reflects user experience.
  • Why MAP? Measures quality across the full ranked list — important to verify the hybrid doesn't just improve top-10 while degrading mid-list results.
  • Why Recall@1000? Verifies the hybrid retrieves more relevant documents overall at first stage (before re-ranking), which is a prerequisite for better final results.
  • Why baseline=0? Enables automatic significance testing (paired t-test) comparing each system against the baseline. Significant p-value (<0.05) confirms the improvement is real, not noise.
  • Two experiments: Test both failure modes — (1) hybrid vs sparse tests whether dense retrieval adds value beyond BM25, (2) hybrid vs dense tests whether lexical matching adds value beyond semantic retrieval.
PAST PAPER 2025 – Q3b(i–iii)

E-Commerce Search Problems

(i) "i-phone" tokenisation problem

✔ ANSWER
Problem: The Porter stemmer and standard tokeniser treat "i-phone" as two tokens: "i" and "phone". "i" is likely a stopword or low-IDF term. So the user's query becomes just "phone" — losing the "iPhone" brand signal entirely. Documents containing "iPhone" (without hyphen) won't match.

Solution: Modify the tokeniser to handle hyphenated terms — either (a) treat "i-phone" as a single token "iphone", or (b) index both the hyphenated form and the merged form simultaneously. This keeps lexical search intact without needing dense retrieval. Another option: add "iphone" as a synonym/query expansion at retrieval time.

(ii) Ranking higher-margin products

✔ ANSWER
Add a query-independent feature "profit_margin_score" to the LTR feature vector. At training time, the LTR model (e.g., LambdaMART) learns a weight for this feature alongside relevance-based features (BM25, title match, etc.). This creates an automatic trade-off between relevance and profitability — the model promotes high-margin products when relevance is similar across candidates. An alternative is to add a multiplicative business boost factor post-ranking.

(iii) Hybrid search advantages

✔ ANSWER
Lexical advantage: Exact match on product codes, brand names (e.g., "Nike Air Max 270"), SKUs and model numbers where semantic similarity is irrelevant — users want exactly what they typed.

Dense advantage: Semantic matching handles vocabulary mismatch — a user searching "trainers" should also find results for "sneakers", "running shoes", "athletic footwear" even if those exact words don't appear in the query.

Combining both: Covers the failure modes of each — lexical retrieval misses semantic synonyms; dense retrieval can miss exact-match brand/SKU queries where there's no semantic signal in the query. Hybrid achieves higher recall at the first stage, giving the re-ranker more good candidates to work with.

Common IR Model Assumptions (Asked Twice!)

PAST PAPER 2023 Q3a & 2025 Q2c

4 IR Model Assumptions that Don't Hold

✔ MODEL ANSWER (4 marks)
  1. Term independence (Bag of Words): VSM and BIM assume terms occur independently. In reality "New York" as a phrase has a very different meaning to "New" and "York" separately. A document about New York City would be retrieved for queries about new things in York, England.
  2. Document independence: Most models score each document independently. But users care about diversity — retrieving 10 nearly identical documents is worse than 10 diverse relevant ones.
  3. Static relevance: Models assume relevance is a fixed property. In reality, a news article about COVID-19 written in 2020 may be highly relevant then but irrelevant (or misleading) in 2026 as medical understanding evolved.
  4. Topical relevance = user relevance: IR systems optimise for topical match. But users also care about novelty (already-seen documents are not useful), credibility (a medical claim needs a reputable source), and readability (a technically correct but impenetrable paper may be useless to a lay user).
