01 — The single most important habit

Concept + Context = Marks

Almost every mark is awarded in pairs: one for naming the concept, one for linking it to the scenario in front of you. Miss either half and you halve the score.

✓ Full marks · 2 / 2

"Mainframes are a single point of failure 1, meaning if the mainframe breaks the satellite processing stops entirely 1."

✗ Half-marks at best · 0–1 / 2

"Mainframes are a single point of failure." — concept only, no link. Or worse: "If the system breaks everything stops" — link only, no concept name. Zero marks.

Memory hook · CCM

"Concept · Connect · Mark."

Three beats. Every paragraph you write should hit all three. Concept (name the textbook idea). Connect (the satellite images / trades / videos in the scenario). Mark (mentally tick the 2-mark pair).

Warning · MCQs are worth zero

Multiple-choice options in this paper score 0 marks for the choice itself. All marks are in the "explain your answer" follow-up. If you pick the right letter and skip the explanation, you score nothing.

Q1 · A — appears every year

Big Data dimensions, applied

You'll be given a scenario and asked which dimensions make it a Big Data problem. Six dimensions exist; not all six apply to every scenario. Pick the 2–4 most relevant and quantify them with numbers from the question.

DimensionWhat it meansScenario triggers
VolumeLarge amounts of dataTB/PB, millions of records, high-res images, large files
VelocitySpeed required for processingReal-time, alerts, live, latency, "within X seconds"
VarietyMultiple data types / formatsVideo + audio + text; structured + unstructured
VeracityData quality, error, trustworthinessPoor quality, sensor errors, missing values, cleaning needed
VariabilityWorkload changes over timePeaks, seasonal, "scales up/down", "20% active but may rise"
ComplexityMulti-stage processing & dependenciesEntity hierarchies, dependent pipeline stages

Memory hook · the 6 V's

Volume · Velocity · Variety · Veracity · Variability · Complexity

Five start with V, one cheats. Picture five soldiers in a "V" formation with a sixth — Complexity — running late, tripping over wires. If you can't recite all six in 10 seconds, you'll waste exam time.

How to write a 2-mark dimension answer

Pattern: [Dimension] 1: [calculate / quote numbers from the scenario] + [why this makes it a Big Data problem] 1.

2023 · satellites Volume — full-marks model

Volume 1: up to 500 MB/s of images arrive each second (100 satellites × 20% active × 10 MB each = 200 MB/s normal; 500 MB/s peak), which constitutes a large volume that cannot be processed on a single machine 1.

Velocity 1: imagery must be analysed for disaster alerting in real time; delays would mean missing the alerting window 1.

Variability 1: only 20% of satellites are active at any one time, but the fleet doubles in two years — load will rise sharply and unpredictably 1.

Trap · 2025

The 2025 paper asked "is tracking these goods a Big Data problem?" — and the correct answer was NO, it isn't. Read the numbers. If volume is small and velocity isn't urgent, say so and explain which V's fail.

Q1 · B — paired with dimensions

Vertical vs Horizontal scaling

A company is offered one giant mainframe instead of a cluster. Explain why that's a bad idea. Eight marks = four points × two marks.

The four canonical reasons mainframes lose

  1. Scaling limit. Mainframes have a maximum compute & storage capacity 1. The company plans to double operations — they will outgrow it 1.
  2. Cost. Mainframes cost far more per FLOP than commodity hardware 1. For embarrassingly parallel image processing, many cheap nodes win on £/throughput 1.
  3. Single point of failure. One machine = one outage 1. For disaster alerting / live trading, downtime is unacceptable 1.
  4. Future-proofing. You cannot scale a mainframe incrementally 1. Horizontal scaling lets you add nodes as the fleet grows 1.

Memory hook · SCSF

"Scaling, Cost, SPoF, Future."

SCSF = "Small Clusters Scale Faster". Four reasons, in order of increasing severity. Recite them as a checklist.

The horizontal side — what you trade for that

Horizontal scaling brings its own pain: load balancing, communication overheads, fault tolerance, orchestration, and programmer complexity. That's the next question.

Q1 · C — distributed challenges

Parallelism challenges

"Discuss the challenges of building this multi-stage pipeline in a distributed way." Eight marks = four challenges × two marks. Each: name the concept + link to scenario.

① Data splitting & load balancing

The dataset must be divided so all machines stay busy. Uneven splits cause stragglers. "The video frames must be chunked equally so all encoders finish at the same time."

② Communication / network overhead

Intermediate data must move between stages over the network — bandwidth-bound. "Topic-tagged paragraphs from every mapper must reach the right reducer."

③ Data explosion / duplication

Each input item produces many outputs, ballooning the intermediate state. "Every article emits ~10 paragraphs, multiplying data 10× before reduce."

④ Fault tolerance

With hundreds of machines, failure is the norm. Lost data must be recomputed without restarting the whole job.

⑤ Synchronisation

Stage N+1 cannot start until Stage N finishes for the relevant key. This creates idle gaps in the pipeline.

⑥ Heterogeneous hardware

Different nodes process at different speeds. The slowest dictates throughput unless you handle stragglers via speculative execution.

Memory hook · "SCFS-HD"

Splitting · Communication · Fault · Sync · Heterogeneous · Duplication

Imagine a SCFS-HD network drive cracking under load. Six headaches of going wide.

Q1 · D — pick a side

Batch vs Streaming

"Which is better, and why?" Four marks: state your choice + two contrasted points × two marks each. You must contrast against the alternative — don't just praise streaming, say why batch would fail here.

Argue FOR Batch when…

  • Data needs to be complete before processing.
  • Processing can wait (end-of-day reports).
  • Parallelisable by chunks (video segments).
  • Simpler error handling on complete data.

Argue FOR Streaming when…

  • Low-latency, real-time output is required.
  • Data arrives continuously, must be acted on.
  • Avoids storing the raw firehose (process & discard).
  • Pipeline stages can overlap (CPU + GPU in parallel).
2025 · WhiskyAI ML Why streaming beats batch for online training

Streaming is the better solution because:

(1) Stream processing defines computation as a pipeline where items at different stages can be processed in parallel 1. The next batch of training data can be prepared on the CPU while the GPU is still learning from the current batch 1.

(2) This avoids the idle-resource problem 1, whereas batch processing would force the CPU and GPU to take turns — one always idle while the other works 1.

Why streaming is hard (separate question, asked 2025)

  • No equal-sized blocks → load balancing is harder.
  • Variable velocity → input rate spikes unpredictably.
  • End-to-end latency is critical — a human is waiting.
  • Back-pressure cascade. Full input buffers stop upstream stages, which fills their buffers, which... eventually crashes the job.
  • Stateful routing. Specific items must hit the specific instance that holds their state.

Q1 · F — system selection

CAP · BASE · ACID

A distributed system can guarantee only two of three properties. Which two you choose is a design decision — and the exam tests whether you can match the right pair to the right use case.

C — Consistency

Every read sees the latest write. All nodes agree on the same value at the same time.

A — Availability

Every request gets a (non-error) response, even if the data might be stale.

P — Partition tolerance

The system keeps working when the network splits nodes into isolated groups.

CombinationPropertiesReal systems
CAConsistency + AvailabilityTraditional RDBMS (Oracle, MySQL). Can't survive a network partition.
CPConsistency + Partition toleranceHBase, MongoDB. Will refuse requests during a partition to stay correct.
APAvailability + Partition toleranceCassandra, CouchDB. Returns possibly-stale data rather than fail.

ACID — traditional DBs

Atomicity · Consistency · Isolation · Durability. Strong guarantees, limited scalability. Banking, accounting, anything where wrong-answer-now is worse than no-answer-now.

BASE — Big Data DBs

Basically Available · Soft state · Eventually consistent. Trade strict consistency for availability and scale. Chat apps, social feeds, IoT.

Memory hook · "Pick your pain"

Partitions will happen — so you only really pick between C and A.

If the scenario is banking, pick CP (HBase). If it's a chat app, pick AP (Cassandra). Once you internalise this, CAP questions are free marks.

Q2 · A — appears every single year

Designing a MapReduce solution

The hardest question type. You're given a multi-stage processing task and must map it onto Map/Reduce. To do this in your head, ask three questions in this order.

The MapReduce design formula

  1. What needs to be GROUPED? That becomes your key.
  2. What can be done per-record independently? That's your Map.
  3. What needs all records for a group? That's your Reduce.

Then for each operation, write 4 things: operation type · what it does · input <key, value> · output <key, value>. Each = 1 mark.

Memory hook · GKM

"Group → Key. Per-record → Map. All-of-group → Reduce."

Recite this before you write a single line. The hardest part of MapReduce design is deciding the key — once the key is right, everything follows.

Worked example — News Summarizer (2023, 9 marks)

Scenario: 4 stages — content extraction → topic identification → paragraph grouping → summarization.

Full marks · 9/9 Model answer

Map: performs content extraction + topic identification per article 1. Input: <articleID, article> 1. Output: <topic, paragraph> 1.

Reduce: performs summarization across all paragraphs for a topic 1. Input: <topic, List<paragraph>> 1. Output: summary 1.

Why these choices: extraction and topic ID are per-article so they aggregate into a single Map step 1. Emitting topic as the key means the shuffle automatically groups paragraphs by topic 1. Summarization needs all paragraphs on a topic, so it must be a reducer 1.

Worked example — SELECT DISTINCT (2020)

Scenario: Each line of a text file has two integers (weight, volume). Implement SELECT DISTINCT Weight, Volume.

5/5 + combiner bonus Model answer

Map: Parse the two integers per line. Emit key = concat(weight, volume), value = null 3.

Reduce: Output the key regardless of values — duplicates have already been merged by the shuffle 2.

Insight: MapReduce's shuffle-and-sort groups identical keys together for free. By making the pair the key, DISTINCT happens during grouping.

Combiner extension: A combiner acts as a "mini reducer" — emit one <key, null> per unique key. Often no amendments to map/reduce are needed because the operation is already commutative & associative.

Worked example — counting in-links in a graph (2018, 18 marks)

2018 Basic count + top-k variant

Basic:

  • Map: for each edge (src, tgt) emit <tgt, 1>.
  • Combiner: local sum per tgt.
  • Reduce: sum all 1s per tgt, emit <tgt, total>.

Top-k variant: same mappers/combiners. Each reducer maintains an in-memory sorted list initialised in setup(), inserts pairs, trims past position k, emits final list in cleanup(). The driver merges the k-sized outputs from each reducer.

Beyond Map / Reduce / Main — supporting classes (2025)

  • InputFormat — converts raw HDFS blocks into KV pairs. TextInputFormat, KeyValueTextInputFormat, SequenceFileInputFormat, DBInputFormat.
  • OutputFormat — controls how reduce output is written.
  • Partitioner — decides which reducer gets which keys. Default is hash-based. Custom partitioners balance load.
  • Combiner — optional local pre-aggregation. Must be commutative & associative.

Q2 · B — 14-mark question, 2018

The Shuffle & Sort phase

Between Map and Reduce, MapReduce does a complex reorganisation that the exam loves to test in detail. Memorise the eight steps below — each one is worth marks.

  1. Map output is buffered in an in-memory circular buffer (default 100 MB).
  2. When the buffer reaches the threshold (80%), contents are sorted by key, partitioned by reducer, and spilled to disk.
  3. Combiners may run on the spilled data to locally pre-aggregate and shrink transfer volume.
  4. Once the mapper finishes its input, all spill files are merge-sorted into a single sorted, partitioned file.
  5. Mapper notifies the JobTracker via heartbeat that it has completed.
  6. Each reducer contacts completed mappers and transfers only its own partition. This is "the shuffle".
  7. Reducer performs a recursive merge-sort on the incoming data (mix of in-memory and on-disk).
  8. Merged, sorted data is fed to reduce().

Memory hook · "Buffer · Spill · Merge · Pull · Merge · Reduce"

Two merges, two transfers, one reduce.

The mapper merges its spills. The reducer pulls from many mappers and merges those. Then it actually reduces.

Q2 · C — follow-up to MapReduce design

Hadoop overhead costs

"After you designed the solution, what are the overhead costs?" Critically: they are not asking about the cost of your operations. They are asking about Hadoop's inherent inefficiencies.

Read the rubric carefully

The 2023 paper explicitly says: "We do NOT expect you to discuss the cost of the operations themselves... but rather aspects of Hadoop that will result in efficiency overheads."

① Small-file problem

Loading is slow because HDFS isn't optimised for many small files — the NameNode stores metadata per file in RAM. "Each news article is one file."

② Intermediate sort cost

The mapper must sort all output by key before spill. Sorting is O(n log n) per partition.

③ Network shuffle

Map output is transferred across the network from mappers to reducers. Latency-bound.

④ Disk write cost

After the map sort, intermediate data is persisted to disk. Adds I/O overhead unavoidable in Hadoop's design.

Memory hook · "SSND"

Small files · Sort · Network · Disk

Four Hadoop overheads, four letters. Always link each to the scenario: "in this case, the satellite images are…".

2025 variant — "why is this difficult in Hadoop"

The pattern shifts to Hadoop limitations, not just overheads. Add: (1) keyless-transformation problem — Map and Reduce both require keys, awkward for transformations that don't naturally have one. (2) High job startup cost — makes frequent computation (every 10 seconds) impractical.

Q2 · D — paired with overheads

Spark advantages over Hadoop

Right after Hadoop's problems comes "why would Spark be better?" Pick the four advantages that actually apply to the scenario — don't list all four blindly.

  1. No HDFS dependency. Spark doesn't require HDFS for data 1 — initial read costs are lower, can use local/cloud storage 1.
  2. In-memory processing. Spark doesn't write intermediate data to disk 1 — eliminates the I/O overhead of Hadoop's shuffle spill 1.
  3. Better serialisation. Transformation overheads are lower 1 due to more efficient binary formats 1.
  4. Flexible programming model. Operations are defined as a DAG of transformations 1 instead of a rigid Map–Reduce shape 1.

Zero-mark answers

"Chaining maps", "streaming support", "language support" — these are invalid unless the scenario explicitly involves them. Use them only when applicable (e.g. mobile-phone scenarios benefit from low-bandwidth in-memory work; UI startup wants quick interactive jobs).

Q2 · E — 2020, 9 marks

Narrow vs Wide dependencies

Spark groups transformations into stages based on dependency type. The MCQ is worth 0; the explanation is worth 9.

TypeDefinitionBehaviour
NarrowEach parent partition feeds at most one child partitionPipelined within a single stage. No shuffle. Examples: map, filter, union.
WideEach parent partition feeds multiple child partitionsRequires a shuffle across the network. Marks a stage boundary. Examples: groupByKey, reduceByKey, join, repartition.
2020 · 9/9 Full-marks explanation

Stage creation 3: narrow operations are grouped and pipelined into a single stage. Tasks operating on partitions with narrow dependencies are scheduled on the executor that already holds the parent partition. Wide operations require data to be shuffled across nodes and mark stage boundaries in the DAG.

Performance reasoning 6: this replicates what a seasoned MapReduce programmer would do by hand — identify operations needing only local data and execute them pipelined 2. If producing output for partition Z only needs computation on one partition of Y, which only needs one partition of X, all three computations happen in-memory sequentially on the same executor 3. This gives a significant reduction in network overhead for negligible memory overhead 1.

Memory hook · the funnel test

"One parent → one child = narrow. One parent → many children = wide."

Wide is wide because data fans out. Narrow is narrow because it stays in its lane.

Q2 · F — 2023

YARN & containers

YARN separates cluster resource management from job coordination. It replaces Hadoop v1's JobTracker bottleneck.

Why YARN beats classic MR1

  1. Better resource utilisation 1 — handles variable-size resource requests instead of MR1's fixed task slots 1.
  2. Multi-application support 1 — Spark, HBase, Flink can all run concurrently on the same cluster 1.
  3. Decentralised coordination 1 — each job's Application Master runs on a worker node, removing the central JobTracker bottleneck 1.

Q2 · G — 2023 Practice, 14 marks

The full MapReduce pipeline — 7 steps

Generic shape: Read → Map → Sort → Write → Shuffle/Read → Reduce → Write. In the exam, describe each step in scenario-specific nouns (frames, blocks, key-frames — not "data").

  1. Read (HDFS → Map). Input is read from HDFS as a series of blocks; each block = a batch of records.
  2. Map. The user function runs per record, emitting <key, value> pairs.
  3. Sort. Map output is sorted by key (in the in-memory buffer, then on spill).
  4. Write (Map → Disk). Sorted output is written to the local DataNode.
  5. Read (Disk → Reduce). Reducers fetch their partition from each mapper and merge.
  6. Reduce. Reads all values for a key and produces the output value(s).
  7. Write (Reduce → HDFS). Reducer output is written to HDFS and replicated.

Memory hook · "Read, Map, Sort, Write, Read, Reduce, Write"

R-M-S-W-R-R-W. Two reads, three writes, one map, one reduce.

Q2 · H — 2020 synthesis question

Can reducers start before mappers finish?

3/3 Complete answer

Reducers cannot start processing before all mappers finish 1, because each reducer needs to merge output from potentially all mappers before it can call reduce() on a key.

However, reducers can start transferring data while mappers are still running — this is the slowstart mechanism 1.

This overlaps CPU-intensive map computation with I/O-intensive shuffle transfers, improving overall cluster utilisation 1.

Q2 · I — new in 2025

Stateful distributed processing

If a scenario involves periodic synchronisation (e.g. ML training updating shared weights), name it as stateful and explain why that's hard.

6 marks Answer template

This training process requires global state shared across all tasks — the application is stateful 2.

Stateful distributed processing is difficult because:

  1. Synchronisation adds an intermediate step where cluster machines wait idle while state is collected 2.
  2. Each task stores additional state (e.g. neural network weights) which, over time, risks out-of-memory errors 2.
  3. (alt) Network transfer of state between executors adds significant cost.
  4. (alt) Error recovery is complex — a worker failure loses its state, forcing re-execution of the entire batch since the last checkpoint.

Q3 · A — HDFS fundamentals

HDFS — the architecture

Two roles. The NameNode keeps the map. The DataNodes keep the data. Clients talk to the NameNode once for location info, then go straight to the DataNodes for I/O.

NameNode (Master)

Stores all metadata in RAM: file→block mappings, block→DataNode locations, directory structure, permissions.

Recovery files on disk: Checkpoint (snapshot) + Journal (write-ahead log of changes since checkpoint).

Single point of failure until Hadoop v3.

DataNode (Worker)

Stores actual data blocks (default 128 MB). Each block = 2 native files: contents + metadata (checksum, size).

Sends a heartbeat to the NameNode every 3 s with block reports <block_id, timestamp, length>.

Why 128 MB blocks? 2019 · 8 marks

Disk seek time ≈ 10 ms. At ~100 MB/s transfer, reading 128 MB takes ~1.28 s — making the seek only ~1% of total transfer time. Smaller blocks would let seek overhead dominate. HDFS is designed for batch processing of large files, not random access to small ones.

Hardware sizing — NameNode vs DataNode

  • NameNode: needs high RAM (holds entire filesystem image). Disk is less important.
  • DataNode: needs high disk capacity, moderate RAM for caching.

Memory hook · "Brain vs Belly"

NameNode is the brain (RAM-heavy). DataNode is the belly (disk-heavy).

HDFS vs NFS / RAID — the comparison

AspectNFS / RAIDHDFS

Q3 · B — 2018 & 2020

HDFS — the read process

Two-phase. Client gets locations from NameNode. Then client reads directly from the nearest DataNode. The NameNode is never on the data path.

  1. Client contacts NameNode with the file pathname.
  2. NameNode returns block IDs and DataNode locations, sorted by network distance (rack awareness).
  3. Client contacts the closest DataNode directly.
  4. DataNode streams the block in 64 KB packets, each with a checksum.
  5. Client verifies checksums; data is now in client memory.

Error handling

  • Checksum failure → report to NameNode, try the next DataNode.
  • Network failure → mark the faulty node, try the next replica.
  • Retries happen a configurable number of times before giving up.
2020 · 8/8 Why this design is chosen

The client contacts the NameNode only for block locations and caches them; all I/O then goes directly to DataNodes 3. This removes the NameNode from the data path, letting the system scale despite having a single NameNode.

Requirement: NameNode must maintain very low latency, which requires all metadata to fit in RAM. This imposes either high RAM requirements on the NameNode or an upper limit on filesystem size 2.

Why other options are wrong: proxying data through the NameNode makes it a bottleneck 1. Requiring all DataNodes to know all block locations creates enormous network overhead for block creation/migration 1 and forces NameNode-class RAM on every DataNode 1.

Q3 · C — pipelined replication

HDFS — the write process (pipelining)

  1. Client asks NameNode to choose DataNodes for the first block's replicas.
  2. DataNodes form a sequential pipeline: DN1 → DN2 → DN3.
  3. Client sends 64 KB packets through the pipeline. Each node forwards while receiving.
  4. Multiple packets can be in flight at once (true pipelining).
  5. Per-packet ACKs flow back. hFlush() periodically writes to the journal for crash safety.
  6. Each DataNode separately reports its received block to the NameNode.

Default replica placement policy

  • Replica 1: same node as writer (or a random node in the writer's rack).
  • Replica 2: random node in a different rack.
  • Replica 3: different node in the same remote rack as Replica 2.

Trade-off: balances reliability (cross-rack replica survives a rack failure) with write bandwidth (only one cross-rack network transfer needed). Read bandwidth benefits from multiple locations.

Q3 · D — 2023 Practice, 15 marks

Replication factor — effects on the cluster

Write throughput

Slightly reduced. HDFS uses pipelined replication, so adding N replicas costs ~N × network latency on top of the first write — negligible on 1 Gbps+ links.

Read latency

Potentially reduced. HDFS reads from the closest replica — more replicas = more candidate locations = higher chance of a nearby copy.

Resource usage

More disk across DataNodes. More NameNode RAM to track replica locations. More bandwidth for block reports.

Q3 · E — 2018, 2020, 2025

LSM trees — the engine behind HBase & Cassandra

Log-Structured Merge trees are write-optimised on-disk indexes. Used by both HBase RegionServers and Cassandra nodes. The whole design is a trade: fast writes, acceptable reads, expensive compaction.

The five components

  • Write-Ahead Log (WAL) — append-only persistent log. Every write goes here first. Sequential = fast. Provides crash recovery.
  • MemTable — in-memory sorted store of recent writes. Checked first on reads (it's a cache).
  • SSTables (Sorted String Tables) — immutable on-disk files, created when the MemTable is flushed. Sorted by key.
  • Index — in-memory mapping of keys → which SSTables might contain them. Uses Bloom filters for O(1) lookup.
  • Compactor — background process that merges SSTables, applies tombstones (deletes), reclaims space.

Write process

  1. Append record to the WAL (sequential disk write — fast).
  2. Insert record into the MemTable (in-memory sorted structure).
  3. When the MemTable fills, sort it, write it as a new SSTable, update the index, clear WAL.
  4. The Compactor periodically merges SSTables in the background.

Read process

  1. Check the MemTable first — most recent data, fastest.
  2. If not found, query the Index via Bloom filter to find candidate SSTables in O(1).
  3. Load and scan only the candidate SSTables (sequential block reads).

Memory hook · "Log · Memory · Spill · Merge"

Write goes Log first, Memory second, Spill third, Merge eventually.

LSM = "logs that eventually become sorted".

True/False statements you'll be asked

StatementT/FWhy
"Lower read latency as more items are added"FALSEMore SSTables = more files to scan. Caching helps only for skewed workloads.
"High write throughput"TRUEMemTable buffering + append-only WAL = sequential disk I/O only.
"Compaction reduces retrievable items"FALSECompaction removes only non-visible data (old versions, tombstones). Retrievable items unchanged.
"Only works with master-workers architecture"FALSELSM is per-node. HBase (master-workers) and Cassandra (peer-to-peer) both use it.

The examiner has stated: if you write the wrong T/F but the reasoning is correct, you won't be penalised.

Q3 · F — 2025

Bloom filters

A space-efficient probabilistic structure that answers "is X possibly in this set?" with two outcomes: possibly yes, or definitely no. Used by LSM-based stores to skip irrelevant SSTables.

How they work

  1. Bit array of m bits, all zero. Define k hash functions.
  2. To add an item: hash it with all k functions, set those k bits to 1.
  3. To query: hash with all k. If all bits are 1 → possibly in set. If any bit is 0 → definitely not.

Key formulas

False-positive rate   p = (1 − e−kn/m)k
Optimal hash count   k = (m/n) · ln 2
Optimal array size   m = − (n · ln p) / (ln 2)²
2025 · 3 marks "Fewer large files reduce lookup time" — T/F

The statement is FALSE 1.

HBase uses Bloom filters for SSTable lookup 1. Bloom filters are O(k) where k is the number of hash functions — these can run in parallel, giving effectively constant-time lookup regardless of the number of SSTables 1.

Q3 · G — heavy in earlier exams

HBase / BigTable

Distributed, sparse, sorted, persistent, multidimensional map. Built on HDFS. CP in CAP. Has a master (HMaster) — and so a single point of failure.

The six key properties

  • Sparse — rows can have missing columns.
  • Distributed — data spread across machines.
  • Persistent — always on persistent storage.
  • Multidimensional — indexed by RowKey + Column Qualifier + Timestamp.
  • Sorted — lexicographically by RowKey.
  • Map — RowKey → record.

Memory hook · "SDPMSM"

"Sparse, Distributed, Persistent, Multi-dim, Sorted, Map."

The data model

  • Row — group of cells sharing a RowKey.
  • Column Qualifier — a column inside a row. Unlimited per row.
  • Column Family — group of columns stored together on disk in their own HDFS directory /hbase/table/CF/.
  • Cell — intersection of (RowKey, Column Qualifier). Can have multiple versioned timestamps.
  • Region — contiguous block of rows. When too large, it splits.

Unique cell identifier (2019): the tuple {RowKey, Column Qualifier, Timestamp}. This is the composite key used in on-disk storage.

On-disk storage format 2018 · 5 marks

Data is stored as KV pairs. Key = <RowKey, ColumnFamily, ColumnQualifier, Timestamp> + size metadata. Value = column value + size. Entries sharing a RowKey are stored contiguously, sorted by the composite key. Each Column Family lives in its own HDFS directory — which is why HBase is technically column-oriented (despite often being called a "fat column store").

Is HBase a row store or column store? 2020 · 4 marks

4/4 Full answer

HBase is essentially a row store, although often called a "fat column store" 1.

Cells are sorted first by RowKey and then by column identifier — this is a row-major format 1.

Columns are grouped into Column Families, each stored in a separate file, so queries on one CF don't touch others 1.

For it to be a true column store, each column would need its own Column Family, or every query would only ever access a single CF 1.

HBase architecture

  • HMaster — assigns regions to RegionServers, handles table create/delete, region splits, failures.
  • RegionServer — handles data requests. Holds 4 LSM-tree structures: WAL, MemStore (writes), BlockCache (reads), HFiles (SSTables on HDFS).
  • ZooKeeper — communication bridge; tracks state of HMaster & RegionServers, triggers recovery on failure.

Data lookup procedure 2016 · 10 marks

  1. Client asks ZooKeeper for the location of the META table.
  2. Client reads the META table to find which RegionServer holds the target row's region.
  3. Client contacts that RegionServer directly.
  4. RegionServer checks MemStore first (fastest, most recent).
  5. If not in MemStore, checks BlockCache.
  6. If not cached, uses Bloom filters to find candidate HFiles.
  7. Reads relevant HFiles from HDFS, returns result.

Trap question · 2023 Practice

"Can HBase replace HDFS for storing student files?" → NO. HBase is a column store, not a file store. It cannot store arbitrary documents. The examiner has confirmed this is a deliberate AI-catcher.

Q3 · H — Cassandra

Cassandra

Peer-to-peer, masterless, distributed key-value store. Combines BigTable's data model with Dynamo's distribution. AP in CAP. BASE consistency.

Architecture

Cassandra is a distributed hash table. Nodes are arranged in a logical ring. Each node contains a Commit Log (WAL), MemTables, SSTables, an Index, and a Coordinator for inter-node communication.

Gossip protocol: every second, each node sends a token with its own state + state of known nodes to 3 other nodes. Eventually, all nodes have global knowledge — no central coordinator needed.

Partitioning

  • RandomPartitioner — uniform hash, simple, good for point queries, no load-balancing guarantees.
  • OrderPreservingPartitioner — if x ≤ y then hash(x) ≤ hash(y). Enables range queries but can cluster data (hot spots).
  • Cassandra combines both: order-preserving hash for key ordering, consistent hashing for assigning to nodes.

Replication & arcs

Data is assigned to a node based on the partition key hash. If no exact match, the next node clockwise takes it. With replication factor N, data is copied to the next N nodes clockwise — these form an arc responsible for that key. Nodes within an arc monitor each other; on failure, the arc extends to the next available node.

Virtual nodes 2023

One physical node is represented as multiple virtual nodes around the ring. A machine with 8 cores gets more virtual nodes than one with 4. This balances load across heterogeneous machines and smooths hash-imbalance.

5 marks Are virtual nodes needed for 50 nodes?

With 50 nodes, probably not 1 — 50 unique locations already provide good fault tolerance and balance 1. Exception: if the hashing scheme causes data to cluster on a subset of nodes, virtual nodes would help 1.

Q3 · I — 2020, 2023

Consistent hashing

A hash space arranged as a ring. Each node owns the keys in the arc clockwise before it. Adding or removing a node only affects keys in its arc — not the whole space.

The combination with order-preserving hashing

Cassandra layers an order-preserving hash on top: input key Y maps to ID A such that key Z > Y maps to ID B > A. Combined: input key → order-preserving hash → position on ring → consistent hashing → assigned to node. Adjacent keys land on adjacent (or the same) nodes — enabling range queries without losing scalability.

Finger tables (Chord)

Instead of O(n) hops to find a key, each node stores log₂(N) finger entries pointing to specific nodes. Lookups complete in O(log N) hops without any global knowledge.

True/False statements (with order-preserving hash)

StatementT/FWhy
An item is always hashed to the same node regardless of node changesFALSEIf a node is added/removed in the arc containing the item, placement changes. Changes outside that arc don't affect it.
Storage load is balanced across nodesFALSEOrder-preserving hash preserves input distribution — if input values aren't uniform, load won't be.
Range queries perform wellTRUEThat's the whole point of order-preserving hashing.

Q3 · J — the 2020 trap question

Quorum & strong consistency

The quorum rule

R + W > N   AND   2W > N
where N = replication factor, R = read replicas contacted, W = write replicas contacted

This guarantees at least one node overlaps between any read and write set — so every read sees the latest write.

2019 · 7 marks RF=5, want read-your-own-write with minimum write latency

Choose QUORUM / QUORUM — R=3, W=3, N=5. R+W = 6 > 5 ✓.

ANY/ONE and ONE/ONE both fail: with a single-copy write, the read replica may not yet have the latest data.

2020 trap · the answer is "NONE OF THE ABOVE"

You'd expect R + W > N to guarantee strong consistency. But quorum assumes writes always succeed — all W replicas update fully before any read. Without an atomic-write protocol (2PC, Paxos, Raft), a partial write failure breaks the guarantee.

Key insight: quorum provides strong consistency only when combined with atomic-write protocols.

Q3 · K — 2025

Kubernetes

Open-source container orchestration system (Google → CNCF). Automates deployment, scaling, and management of containerised applications.

Master plane

  • API server — entry point.
  • etcd — distributed key-value store of cluster state.
  • Scheduler — allocates pods to nodes.
  • Controller manager — runs maintenance & recovery loops.

Worker plane

  • Kubelet — ensures the pods on this node are running.

If a question asks you to count/sum/aggregate and you use groupByKey, expect to lose marks.

Final stretch

Exam-day strategy

  1. Read the guidance first. It tells you exactly how many points to make and how marks are split. "Four points expected, 2 marks each" → write exactly four bullets.
  2. Count your points. If a question is worth 8 marks and asks for points, write four bullets. If it asks to discuss, write four sentences.
  3. Every point = concept + scenario link. Never write a concept without the link.
  4. Do the math. If numbers are in the scenario, calculate them. 100 satellites × 10 MB × 20% active = 200 MB/s.
  5. Use scenario-specific nouns. Not "the data" — "the satellite images" or "the trade records".
  6. For MCQs: spend 90% of your time on the explanation, not the letter.
  7. Time management: 60 marks in 90 minutes = 1.5 minutes per mark. A 6-mark question gets at most 9 minutes.
  8. If stuck, write something. Concept-only sentences earn partial marks. Blank earns nothing.
