(a) Describe how BBC video encoding can be characterised as a Big Data problem using the 5V dimensions. 6 marks

BBC Video Encoding and the 5Vs of Big Data

The BBC produces thousands of hours of video content annually — live broadcasts, on-demand shows, news segments, documentaries — all of which must be encoded into multiple formats (SD, HD, 4K) and codecs (H.264, H.265, VP9, AV1) for streaming across devices. This is a textbook Big Data challenge when viewed through the lens of the 5V framework.

DimensionHow It Applies to BBC Video Encoding
Volume The raw data is enormous. A single hour of uncompressed 4K video is approximately 300GB. The BBC's archive contains over 15 million items spanning decades. When you factor in multiple encoded versions per title, you're looking at petabytes of data that must be stored and processed. Even daily intake of new content runs into terabytes.
Velocity Live broadcasts (news, sports events like Wimbledon, the World Cup) demand real-time or near-real-time encoding. The raw feed must be encoded and pushed to CDNs within seconds of capture. Meanwhile, new on-demand content is uploaded continuously by production teams across BBC Studios worldwide.
Variety The data is highly heterogeneous. Each piece of content exists in multiple resolutions (480p, 720p, 1080p, 4K), multiple codecs (H.264 for older devices, H.265 for modern ones, VP9 for web), multiple audio tracks (English, Welsh, Gaelic, audio description), subtitles in various languages, and rich metadata (programme info, scene descriptions, rights data).
Veracity Encoding must be accurate and trustworthy. A single encoding artefact — colour banding, audio/video desync, dropped frames — renders content unwatchable. The BBC must verify each encoded version against quality benchmarks. Corrupted metadata could mean the wrong content is served, or rights-restricted content is shown in the wrong region.
Value Properly encoded video is directly tied to revenue and audience engagement. Efficient encoding using modern codecs (AV1) can reduce file sizes by 30–50%, saving millions in CDN and storage costs. Poor encoding quality drives viewers to competitors. Adaptive bitrate streaming (enabled by multi-format encoding) provides smooth playback that retains subscribers.
Real-World Comparison: Netflix

Netflix encodes each title into approximately 120 different versions — combinations of resolution, bitrate, and codec. For their catalogue of ~17,000 titles, that means over 2 million encoded files. When a new season of a flagship show drops at midnight globally, all 120 versions per episode must be ready simultaneously — that is Volume meeting Velocity at scale. Each version is a different format (Variety), each must be pixel-perfect (Veracity), and the entire effort exists to keep 230 million subscribers paying (Value).

(b) Describe the differences between batch and stream processing. 4 marks

Batch vs Stream Processing

These are the two fundamental paradigms for processing data at scale, and they differ in almost every dimension — data model, latency, completeness, and use cases.

Batch Processing

Operates on complete, bounded datasets. The entire dataset is available before processing begins. Achieves high throughput by processing large volumes at once, but incurs higher latency — results are available minutes to hours after the data arrives.

Example: Hadoop MapReduce processes yesterday's web server logs overnight to produce a daily traffic report. The report is comprehensive and accurate, but it reflects yesterday's state, not right now.

Use cases: Monthly financial reports, training machine learning models, historical trend analysis, ETL pipelines, building search indexes.

Stream Processing

Operates on unbounded, continuous data — records arrive indefinitely and are processed as they appear. Achieves very low latency (milliseconds to seconds) but processes fewer records per unit time than batch.

Example: Apache Spark Streaming or Flink analyses credit card transactions as they happen, flagging suspicious patterns within milliseconds. You catch fraud as it occurs, not the next morning.

Use cases: Fraud detection, live dashboards, real-time recommendations, IoT sensor monitoring, alerting systems.

The Fundamental Trade-off

Latency vs Completeness. Batch gives you complete, accurate results but hours later. Stream gives you instant results but they may be incomplete or require later correction. Modern systems like Apache Beam and the Lambda Architecture attempt to combine both — stream for speed, batch for accuracy.

Everyday Analogy

Batch is like a newspaper: comprehensive coverage of yesterday's events, delivered this morning. You get the full picture, but it's already old. Stream is like a live Twitter feed: instant updates as events unfold, but you might see incomplete information, rumours, or corrections later. Most news organisations now use both — the live feed for breaking news, the newspaper for analysis.

(c) Design a MapReduce solution for BBC video encoding/processing. 10 marks

MapReduce Design for Video Encoding

Video encoding is an inherently parallelisable problem — each video (or segment of a video) can be encoded independently. This maps naturally to the MapReduce paradigm, though several design decisions require careful thought.

Mapper Design

  1. Input: Each mapper receives one video file or one segment of a large video as its input split. The key-value pair is (video_filename, raw_video_bytes). We need a custom InputFormat because video files cannot be split at arbitrary byte offsets — you must split at scene boundaries or GOP (Group of Pictures) boundaries to avoid corrupting the video stream.
  2. Processing: The mapper runs the actual encoding algorithm — transcoding the raw video from its source format to the target format and resolution. For example, converting a 4K H.264 master to a 720p H.265 stream. This is the most CPU-intensive step and dominates the total job time.
  3. Output: The mapper emits (video_id + segment_number, encoded_segment_bytes). The composite key ensures segments can be reassembled in the correct order during the reduce phase.

Reducer Design

  1. Grouping: The framework groups all encoded segments by video_id. The secondary sort on segment_number ensures segments arrive in order.
  2. Assembly: The reducer concatenates ordered segments into a complete encoded video file. It writes the final output to HDFS.
  3. Validation: The reducer performs quality checks — verifying frame counts, checking audio/video sync, validating container format integrity. Failed validations can trigger re-encoding of specific segments.
// Pseudo-code for the MapReduce Video Encoder class VideoMapper extends Mapper<Text, BytesWritable, CompositeKey, BytesWritable> { void map(Text filename, BytesWritable rawVideo, Context ctx) { EncodedSegment segment = FFmpegEncoder.encode(rawVideo, TARGET_FORMAT); CompositeKey key = new CompositeKey(filename, segmentNumber); ctx.write(key, new BytesWritable(segment.bytes)); } } class VideoReducer extends Reducer<CompositeKey, BytesWritable, Text, BytesWritable> { void reduce(CompositeKey key, Iterable<BytesWritable> segments, Context ctx) { ByteArrayOutputStream finalVideo = new ByteArrayOutputStream(); for (BytesWritable seg : segments) { // segments arrive in order finalVideo.write(seg.getBytes()); } if (QualityChecker.validate(finalVideo)) { ctx.write(key.getVideoId(), new BytesWritable(finalVideo.toByteArray())); } } } // Custom InputFormat: splits by video file (or GOP boundaries for large files) class VideoInputFormat extends FileInputFormat<Text, BytesWritable> { // Override isSplitable() to split only at scene/GOP boundaries // Override createRecordReader() to return whole video segments }

Key Design Considerations

  • Data locality is critical: Raw 4K video files are massive (300GB+ per hour). Moving them across the network is prohibitively expensive. The scheduler must assign map tasks to nodes where the raw video blocks are already stored in HDFS. This is the core advantage of "moving computation to data."
  • Multiple output formats: If the BBC needs each video in 10 formats, you can either run 10 separate MR jobs (simple but reads raw data 10 times) or use Hadoop's MultipleOutputs class to produce all formats in a single mapper pass (complex but reads once).
  • CPU-bound workload: Video encoding is computationally expensive, unlike typical MR tasks that are I/O-bound. You may need to configure fewer map tasks per node than usual, or use nodes with more CPU cores and less disk.
  • Speculative execution: Since encoding is CPU-intensive, some tasks may run slower. Hadoop's speculative execution can launch backup copies of slow tasks on idle nodes. However, this doubles CPU usage, so it should be tuned carefully.
  • Combiner is not applicable: Unlike word count, video segments cannot be "combined" — they are unique binary data, not aggregatable values.
Real-World Implementation

YouTube processes over 500 hours of video uploaded every minute. Their encoding pipeline is essentially a massive distributed MapReduce-like system: each uploaded video is split into segments, encoded in parallel across thousands of machines into multiple resolutions and codecs, then reassembled. The reason you can watch a just-uploaded video in 360p quickly (fast low-res encode) but 4K takes longer (slow high-res encode) is exactly this parallel pipeline at work.

(a) What are the advantages of Spark over Hadoop MapReduce? 6 marks

Spark's Advantages Over Hadoop MapReduce

While Hadoop MapReduce pioneered large-scale distributed data processing, it has fundamental architectural limitations that Spark was specifically designed to overcome. The improvements are not incremental — for many workloads, Spark is 10x to 100x faster.

FeatureHadoop MapReduceApache Spark
Data Persistence Writes intermediate results to HDFS (disk) after every stage Keeps data in memory (RDDs). Only spills to disk when memory is full
Execution Model Rigid 2-stage: Map → Reduce. Multi-step jobs require chaining multiple MR jobs DAG (Directed Acyclic Graph) of arbitrary transformations — map, filter, join, group, etc. in one job
Optimisation No cross-stage optimisation. Each MR job is independent Lazy evaluation builds full plan first; Catalyst optimizer eliminates redundant operations and minimises shuffles
API Breadth Only MapReduce. Need separate tools for SQL, streaming, ML, graph Unified API: Spark SQL, Spark Streaming, MLlib, GraphX — all in one framework
Iterative Algorithms Reads data from disk on every iteration. 100 iterations = 100 disk reads Cache data in memory. 100 iterations = 1 disk read + 99 memory reads. Massive speedup for ML
Ease of Use Verbose Java code. Even word count is ~60 lines Concise APIs in Scala, Python, Java, R. Word count is ~5 lines
In-Memory Processing: The Core Advantage

Consider a video analytics pipeline that reads a dataset, filters it, joins it with metadata, and computes statistics. In Hadoop, each of these 4 steps is a separate MR job that writes to and reads from HDFS. That is 8 disk I/O operations. In Spark, the data is read once into memory, and all 4 transformations happen in-memory — just 1 disk read. Since disk is ~100x slower than RAM, the speedup is dramatic.

The Kitchen Analogy

Hadoop MapReduce is like a cook who must put every ingredient back in the fridge after each step: chop onions, refrigerate; take them out, sauté, refrigerate; take them out, add to sauce, refrigerate. Spark keeps all ingredients on the counter (in memory) throughout the entire recipe. The constant trips to the fridge (disk) are what make Hadoop slow for multi-step pipelines.

(b) Explain how task re-execution works in Spark vs Hadoop MapReduce for fault recovery. 6 marks

Task Re-execution & Fault Recovery

Both systems must handle failures gracefully — in a cluster of thousands of nodes, hardware failures are not exceptions but daily occurrences. However, they use fundamentally different strategies for recovery.

Hadoop MapReduce

Strategy: Re-read from stable storage.

When a map or reduce task fails, the Application Master detects the failure and re-schedules the task on a different (healthy) node. The re-executed task reads its input from HDFS from scratch — since HDFS data is replicated (RF=3), it is always available even if the original node is dead.

By default, Hadoop retries a failed task up to 4 times before marking the entire job as failed. It can also use speculative execution — proactively launching duplicate copies of slow tasks on other nodes, using whichever finishes first.

Strength: Simple and robust. HDFS guarantees data availability.

Weakness: Re-reading from disk is slow, especially for large intermediate datasets.

Apache Spark

Strategy: RDD lineage-based recomputation.

Spark's RDDs (Resilient Distributed Datasets) maintain a lineage graph — the complete sequence of transformations that created each RDD from its parent. If a partition of an RDD is lost (because the node holding it in memory crashed), Spark does not re-run the entire job. Instead, it recomputes only the lost partition by replaying the transformations from the nearest available ancestor.

If the ancestor is cached in memory on another node, recomputation is fast. If the ancestor is the original HDFS input, Spark re-reads just that partition from disk.

Strength: Fine-grained recovery. Only lost partitions are recomputed.

Weakness: Long lineage chains make recomputation expensive.

Spark Checkpointing: Solving the Long Lineage Problem

For jobs with very long chains of transformations (e.g., iterative ML algorithms running 1000 iterations), the lineage can become extremely deep. If a failure occurs, replaying 1000 transformations is expensive. Spark addresses this with checkpointing — periodically saving an RDD's contents to HDFS, which truncates the lineage. Recovery then starts from the most recent checkpoint rather than the original input.

The Photography Analogy

Imagine a photographer editing a photo with 20 steps (crop, brightness, contrast, filter, etc.). Hadoop's approach: if the edited photo is lost, re-take the entire photo from scratch and start over. Spark's approach: the photographer remembers every editing step (lineage). They find the original raw image (or a saved intermediate version) and replay only the steps needed. Checkpointing: the photographer saves the photo after every 5 edits, so at worst they only replay 5 steps, not all 20.

Key distinction: Hadoop stores actual data redundantly (HDFS replication). Spark stores the recipe to recreate the data (lineage). This is why Spark uses less disk space but may need more computation time during recovery.

(c) Describe the YARN architecture and its components. 8 marks

YARN Architecture (Yet Another Resource Negotiator)

YARN is the cluster resource management layer of the Hadoop ecosystem. It was introduced in Hadoop 2.0 to address a critical limitation: Hadoop 1.0's JobTracker combined resource management with application scheduling in a single component, creating a scalability bottleneck and limiting the cluster to running only MapReduce jobs.

The Four Core Components

  1. Resource Manager (RM) — The Global Scheduler: A single master daemon that runs on a dedicated node. It has a complete view of the cluster's total capacity (CPU cores, memory, disk across all nodes) and decides which applications get which resources. It has two sub-components: the Scheduler (allocates containers based on policies like capacity or fair scheduling) and the Application Manager (accepts job submissions and launches the first container for each application). Critically, the RM does NOT monitor task progress — it only manages resources.
  2. Node Manager (NM) — The Per-Node Agent: One NM runs on every worker node in the cluster. It is responsible for launching and monitoring containers on its node, tracking resource usage (CPU, memory), and reporting health status back to the RM via heartbeats. If a container exceeds its allocated memory, the NM kills it. Think of the NM as a floor manager in a building — they manage the rooms on their floor and report to the central office.
  3. Application Master (AM) — The Per-Application Coordinator: One AM is created for each submitted application. It runs inside a container on a worker node (not on the RM node). The AM negotiates containers with the RM, distributes tasks to those containers, monitors task progress, and handles task failures (retries). For MapReduce: the AM manages map and reduce tasks. For Spark: the AM manages executor processes. Each AM is independent — a crash in one application's AM does not affect any other application.
  4. Container — The Unit of Resource Allocation: A container is an abstract bundle of resources: a specific amount of CPU cores and memory on a specific node. Applications run inside containers. The RM allocates containers; the NM launches them; the AM uses them. A single node can run many containers simultaneously, each isolated from the others.
How They Work Together

Step 1: Client submits an application to the RM. Step 2: RM allocates a container on a worker node and launches the AM inside it. Step 3: AM assesses the job requirements and requests additional containers from the RM. Step 4: RM grants containers on various nodes. Step 5: AM contacts the NMs on those nodes to launch tasks in the granted containers. Step 6: Tasks run, report progress to AM, and AM reports completion to RM.

Advantages Over Hadoop 1.0's JobTracker

FeatureHadoop 1.0 (JobTracker)YARN
Multi-tenancy Only MapReduce could run Spark, Flink, MR, Tez, Storm — all on the SAME cluster simultaneously
Resource Flexibility Fixed map slots and reduce slots — unused reduce slots couldn't be used for maps Generic containers — any resource can be used for any purpose
Scalability JobTracker managed both resources AND application logic — bottleneck at ~4,000 nodes RM only manages resources; AMs handle application logic — scales to 10,000+ nodes
Failure Isolation JobTracker failure killed ALL running jobs One AM crashing only affects its own application; all others continue
The Hotel Analogy

YARN is like a hotel management system. The Resource Manager is the front desk — they know which rooms are available and assign them to events. The Node Manager is the floor housekeeper on each floor — they prepare rooms, monitor cleanliness, and report issues. The Application Master is the event coordinator for each wedding or conference — they request rooms from the front desk and coordinate activities within their allocated rooms. Each event (application) has its own coordinator, so a chaotic wedding doesn't disrupt the quiet conference next door (failure isolation).

Question 3 — Storage Systems 20 marks
(a) Compare RAID 1 and RAID 6 — their mechanisms, trade-offs, and use cases. 6 marks

RAID 1 vs RAID 6

RAID (Redundant Array of Independent Disks) provides fault tolerance at the hardware level by spreading or duplicating data across multiple physical disks. RAID 1 and RAID 6 take very different approaches to achieving this redundancy.

RAID 1 — Mirroring

Mechanism: Every write is duplicated identically to a second disk. The two disks are exact mirror copies of each other at all times.

Storage efficiency: 50% — you lose half your total disk capacity to redundancy. With 2 x 1TB disks, you get 1TB usable.

Read performance: Excellent — the controller can read from either disk, effectively doubling read throughput.

Write performance: Good — writes go to both disks simultaneously (parallel), so there is minimal overhead.

Fault tolerance: Survives 1 disk failure.

Recovery: Simple — copy the surviving disk to a replacement. No computation needed.

RAID 6 — Double Parity

Mechanism: Data is striped across multiple disks with two independent parity blocks distributed across all disks. Parity is calculated using different algorithms (e.g., P + Q parity using Reed-Solomon coding).

Storage efficiency: Much better at scale — with N disks, you lose only 2 disks to parity. With 6 x 1TB disks, you get 4TB usable (67%). With 10 disks: 80%.

Read performance: Good — data is striped, so reads can be parallelised across disks.

Write performance: Slower — every write requires calculating and writing two parity blocks (write penalty). Small random writes are especially expensive.

Fault tolerance: Survives 2 simultaneous disk failures.

Recovery: Complex — must recalculate lost data from parity. CPU-intensive and time-consuming.

PropertyRAID 1RAID 6
Redundancy methodFull mirror (copy)Double parity (calculation)
Min. disks needed24
Disks tolerated failing12
Storage efficiency (6 disks)50%67%
Write overheadLow (parallel mirror)High (2 parity calculations)
Best forOS drives, small critical dataLarge storage arrays, archival
Everyday Analogy

RAID 1 is like photocopying every important document — dead simple, but you use twice the paper. RAID 6 is like using a mathematical error-correcting code: you add two "check" pages to every set of documents, and if any two pages are destroyed, you can reconstruct them from the check pages. It requires more math, but you use far less paper overall — especially when you have many documents.

(b) Explain how HBase/HDFS handles storage differently from RAID, and the trade-offs involved. 6 marks

HBase Storage via HDFS vs RAID

HBase does not use RAID at all. Instead, it relies entirely on HDFS software-level replication running on commodity hardware — a fundamentally different philosophy from traditional enterprise storage.

The Key Philosophical Difference

RAID protects against disk failures within a single machine using specialised hardware controllers. HDFS protects against disk failures, node failures, and entire rack failures using software replication across many cheap machines. RAID says "make each machine reliable." HDFS says "assume every machine will fail, and plan for it."

PropertyRAID (Traditional)HDFS Replication (HBase)
Protection levelDisk failure onlyDisk + node + rack failure
HardwareExpensive RAID controllers, enterprise disksCommodity servers, cheap JBOD (Just a Bunch Of Disks)
Replication mechanismHardware-level parity or mirroringSoftware-level full copies across network
Default redundancyRAID 6: 2 parity disks (~1.5x overhead)RF=3: three full copies (3x overhead)
Rack awarenessNot applicable (single machine)Yes — replicas placed on different racks to survive rack-level failures
Recovery speedLimited by single machine's I/OParallel recovery from many nodes simultaneously
ScalabilityLimited by single server capacityScales horizontally to thousands of nodes

HDFS Rack-Aware Replica Placement: With the default replication factor of 3, HDFS places the first replica on the local node, the second on a different node in a different rack, and the third on another node in that same remote rack. This means even if an entire rack loses power (its switch fails, or there is a fire), two copies of every block survive on the other rack.

The Storage Cost Trade-off: HDFS uses 3x storage (vs RAID 6's ~1.5x), which seems wasteful. However, HDFS provides much stronger fault tolerance, uses dramatically cheaper hardware (no RAID controllers), and enables parallel reads from multiple replicas for better performance. At the scale of Google, Facebook, or the BBC, the savings on hardware costs far outweigh the extra disk usage.

The Insurance Analogy

RAID is like having a spare tyre in your car — it protects against one tyre going flat, but if the whole car is stolen or flooded, you've lost everything. HDFS replication is like parking three identical cars in three different cities. You survive car theft, flood, earthquake — virtually any localised disaster. It costs 3x as much, but your "data" is far safer. And because the cars are cheap models (commodity hardware), the total cost may still be less than one luxury car with fancy insurance (enterprise RAID).

In practice: HBase nodes use JBOD (Just a Bunch Of Disks) — each disk operates independently with no RAID controller. If one disk fails, only the blocks on that disk are lost, and HDFS automatically re-replicates them from copies on other nodes. Using RAID on HDFS nodes would be redundant and wasteful — you'd be protecting data that's already protected by software replication.

(c) Explain Cassandra's virtual nodes (vnodes), how they work, and their benefits. 8 marks

Cassandra Virtual Nodes (Vnodes)

Cassandra uses consistent hashing to distribute data across nodes. Each row key is hashed to a position on a circular hash ring, and each node is responsible for a range of that ring. Virtual nodes (vnodes) fundamentally change how those ranges are assigned.

Traditional Approach (Without Vnodes)

Each physical node owns one large, contiguous range on the hash ring. For example, with 4 nodes, each owns 25% of the ring as a single block.

Problems with single ranges: (1) Adding a new node means one existing node must split its range and transfer a massive chunk of data — a slow, network-intensive operation. (2) If one node fails, all its data must be rebuilt from its immediate neighbours, putting enormous load on just 1-2 nodes. (3) If one node has better hardware, there is no way to give it proportionally more data.

Virtual Nodes Approach

With vnodes, each physical node owns many small, non-contiguous ranges on the hash ring. By default, Cassandra assigns 256 vnodes per physical node. Each vnode is a small token range — a tiny slice of the ring — and a physical node's 256 vnodes are scattered randomly around the ring, interleaved with other nodes' vnodes.

Traditional (4 nodes, 1 range each): Ring: [====Node A====][====Node B====][====Node C====][====Node D====] Vnodes (4 nodes, 8 vnodes each = 32 total): Ring: [A][C][B][D][A][B][D][C][A][D][C][B][A][C][B][D][A][B]... ^-- Node A's vnodes are scattered throughout the ring

Benefits of Vnodes

  1. Faster, smoother rebalancing when adding/removing nodes: When a new node joins, it takes over some vnodes from many existing nodes (not just one). Each existing node loses only a small fraction of its data, so the transfer is distributed across the entire cluster. Similarly, removing a node distributes its small ranges across many nodes. This is dramatically faster than moving one giant range.
  2. Faster rebuild after failure: When a node dies, its 256 vnodes are scattered across the ring, meaning its data is replicated on many different nodes. All of those nodes can participate in the rebuild simultaneously, in parallel. Traditional approach: 1-2 neighbours rebuild one huge range (serial bottleneck). Vnodes: potentially dozens of nodes each rebuild a small piece (massively parallel).
  3. Better data distribution and hotspot mitigation: With many small ranges, the law of large numbers helps average out any natural skew in the hash distribution. If some keys happen to cluster in one part of the ring, vnodes ensure that range is shared by many physical nodes rather than burdening a single one.
  4. Heterogeneous hardware support: A more powerful server can be assigned more vnodes (e.g., 512 instead of 256), so it owns proportionally more of the ring and stores more data. A weaker server gets fewer vnodes. This is impossible with the traditional one-range-per-node approach — each node owns exactly one equal slice regardless of its capacity.
  5. Simplified operations: With vnodes, operators no longer need to manually calculate and assign token ranges when adding nodes. Cassandra automatically distributes vnodes across the cluster, making cluster management much simpler.
The Postman Analogy

Traditional approach: Each postman covers one entire ZIP code — a large, contiguous area. If a postman is sick, their entire ZIP code has no delivery, and one neighbouring postman must cover the whole extra area alone. Adding a new postman means redrawing ZIP code boundaries and transferring all the mail for a huge area.

Vnodes approach: Each postman covers 256 individual streets scattered randomly across the city. If a postman is sick, their scattered streets are easily covered by many neighbouring postmen, each picking up just a few extra streets. Adding a new postman simply means reassigning a few streets from each existing postman — barely noticeable to any one person. And a postman with a bike gets 100 streets, while one with a van gets 400 — naturally handling heterogeneous capacity.

Quick Summary

Vnodes transform the consistent hash ring from "few large ranges per node" to "many small scattered ranges per node." This enables parallel rebuilds, smooth rebalancing, hotspot mitigation, and heterogeneous hardware support — all critical for operating large-scale Cassandra clusters in production.

(a) What are the effects of increasing the HDFS replication factor from 3 to 5? 5 marks

Increasing Replication Factor: RF=3 to RF=5

Changing the replication factor from 3 to 5 has wide-ranging implications across performance, storage, fault tolerance, and network usage. It is not a simple "more is better" decision — there are significant costs.

DimensionRF=3 (Default)RF=5 (Increased)Impact
Write Latency Pipeline of 3 nodes Pipeline of 5 nodes Slower — write acknowledged only when all 5 nodes confirm. The slowest node in the pipeline determines latency, and the probability of encountering a slow node increases with pipeline length.
Storage Cost 3x raw data size 5x raw data size 67% more storage needed. A 1PB dataset now uses 5PB instead of 3PB — an enormous cost increase at scale.
Read Performance Choose from 3 replicas Choose from 5 replicas Potentially faster — more replicas means better chance of finding one on the same rack or even the same node as the reader (improved data locality).
Fault Tolerance Survives 2 node failures Survives 4 node failures Much stronger — the system can lose 4 nodes simultaneously without losing any data.
NameNode Memory 3 block-location mappings per block 5 block-location mappings per block 67% more metadata in NameNode's RAM — can push large clusters toward NN memory limits.
When Would You Use RF=5?

In practice, RF=5 is used for extremely critical data that absolutely cannot be lost — for example, financial transaction logs at a bank, or the master copy of a genome database. The BBC might use RF=5 for their most valuable archived content (irreplaceable historical footage) while keeping RF=3 for daily operational data. It is a conscious trade-off: spending more on storage and accepting slower writes in exchange for near-bulletproof durability.

(b) What are the effects of decreasing the HDFS replication factor from 3 to 1? 5 marks

Decreasing Replication Factor: RF=3 to RF=1

Reducing to a single copy is the most dramatic change you can make, and it fundamentally breaks HDFS's core reliability guarantee.

DimensionRF=3 (Default)RF=1 (Single Copy)Impact
Write Latency Pipeline of 3 nodes No pipeline — write to 1 node Much faster — no network overhead, no waiting for 2 more nodes to confirm.
Storage Cost 3x raw data size 1x raw data size 3x less storage — massive savings. A 1PB dataset uses exactly 1PB instead of 3PB.
Read Performance Choose from 3 replicas Only 1 copy — no choice All reads for a block go to the single DataNode holding it. This creates hotspots — popular blocks cause heavy load on specific nodes with no way to distribute reads.
Fault Tolerance Survives 2 node failures Survives 0 node failures CRITICAL: Any single disk or node failure causes permanent, irrecoverable data loss. There is no backup. The data is gone forever.
Data Locality 3 locations to schedule tasks near data 1 location only Scheduler has no flexibility — tasks must run on or near that one node, or data must travel over the network.

NEVER use RF=1 for production data. In a cluster of 1000 nodes, with ~2% annual disk failure rates, you will lose data regularly. The only acceptable use case for RF=1 is truly temporary, disposable, or easily re-creatable data — such as intermediate results of a MapReduce job that can be recomputed, or a cache that can be rebuilt from an external source.

The Backup Analogy

Think of your university dissertation. RF=3: You have it on your laptop, a USB drive, and Google Drive. Your laptop gets stolen? No problem — two copies survive. RF=1: You have it only on your laptop. Laptop stolen, hard drive crashes, coffee spill — your dissertation is gone. Permanently. Every student who has lost an essay this way understands viscerally why RF=1 is unacceptable for important data.

(c) Discuss the network and resource implications of changing the replication factor. 5 marks

Network & Resource Implications

The replication factor does not just affect storage — it has cascading effects on network bandwidth, NameNode memory, write latency, and read patterns. Understanding these implications is essential for capacity planning.

  1. Network bandwidth during writes: Each additional replica means one more hop in the write pipeline. Going from RF=3 to RF=5 adds 2 more network transfers per block write. Since HDFS blocks are 128MB by default, that is 256MB of additional network traffic per block. At cluster scale with thousands of concurrent writes, this can saturate inter-rack network links.
  2. Cross-rack traffic: HDFS's rack-aware placement strategy places replicas on different racks for fault tolerance. With RF=3, one replica is local and two cross the rack switch. With RF=5, three or four replicas cross rack boundaries. Cross-rack bandwidth is typically the scarcest resource in a data centre (rack-local switches have much more bandwidth than the aggregation layer), so higher RF disproportionately strains this bottleneck.
  3. NameNode memory pressure: The NameNode stores a mapping of every block to every DataNode that holds a replica. With RF=3, each block has 3 mappings in the NN's memory. With RF=5, each block has 5 mappings — a 67% increase. For a cluster storing 100 million blocks, this means an additional ~200 million mapping entries. Since the NN keeps all metadata in RAM, this can push the NN toward its memory ceiling, potentially requiring more expensive hardware.
  4. Write latency and tail latency: In a write pipeline, the client waits for an acknowledgement from the last node in the chain. The write latency is determined by the slowest node. With more nodes in the pipeline, the probability of encountering a slow node (due to garbage collection, disk contention, or network congestion) increases. This is the "tail latency" problem — the more nodes involved, the higher the chance of a slow outlier.
  5. Read benefit — improved locality: Higher RF is not all cost. More replicas distributed across more nodes and racks mean that when a MapReduce or Spark task needs to read a block, there are more locations where it can find the data nearby. This improves data locality scheduling — more tasks can be node-local or rack-local, reducing network traffic during reads. This is especially valuable for read-heavy workloads.
The Cost-Benefit Equation

Higher RF = more network cost during writes + more storage + more NN memory, BUT less network cost during reads + better fault tolerance + better data locality. The optimal RF depends on your workload's read/write ratio, the criticality of the data, and your cluster's network capacity.

Practical Perspective

Consider it like backup copies of your thesis. RF=1: One copy on your laptop. Fast to save (one write), uses no extra space, but any failure = permanent loss. RF=3: Laptop + USB + cloud. Takes longer to save everywhere, uses 3x space, but you survive almost any disaster. RF=5: Add two more backups (a friend's computer, a university server). Even safer, but every save now takes longer and you're managing 5 copies. The right number depends on how irreplaceable the data is and how much effort you're willing to invest in redundancy.

Key Takeaway

Replication factor is not a "set and forget" parameter — it is a deliberate engineering trade-off between durability, performance, cost, and network capacity. Most production HDFS clusters use RF=3 as the default, increase to RF=5 only for mission-critical data, and never use RF=1 except for disposable temporary data. The network is usually the binding constraint.
