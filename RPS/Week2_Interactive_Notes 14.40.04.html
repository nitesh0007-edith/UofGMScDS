Reading Research Papers

Core Principle

Reading research papers is a skill that improves with practice. Don't expect to understand everything on the first read!

Why Read Research Papers?

  • Become familiar with new research areas
  • Understand the state of the art
  • Identify research gaps and opportunities
  • Learn research methodologies and techniques
  • Build your reference library
  • Support your own research with evidence

The Three-Pass Approach

First Pass: The Quick Scan (5-10 minutes)

Goal: Get the gist of the paper

  • Read title, abstract, and introduction
  • Read section headings
  • Glance at figures and tables
  • Read the conclusion
  • Decision: Is this paper relevant? Should I continue reading?

Second Pass: Detailed Reading (1 hour)

Goal: Grasp the paper's content

  • Read the paper carefully, but skip detailed proofs
  • Note key points and mark relevant references
  • Examine figures, diagrams, and graphs carefully
  • Identify strengths and weaknesses
  • Make notes in your research journal

Third Pass: Deep Understanding (4-5 hours)

Goal: Virtually re-implement the paper

  • Attempt to re-create the work
  • Identify and challenge assumptions
  • Think about how you would present the ideas
  • Note ideas for future work
  • Write a detailed summary

Pro Tips for Reading Papers

  • Don't read linearly: Start with abstract, intro, conclusion, then fill in details
  • Take notes: Always write summaries in your own words
  • Ask questions: What problem does this solve? How? What are limitations?
  • Follow references: Build your reading list from cited works
  • Be critical: Not everything published is perfect

Understanding Paper Structure

Section Purpose What to Look For
Abstract Brief summary of entire paper Problem, approach, key results, contributions
Introduction Context and motivation Research question, why it matters, paper structure
Related Work Position in literature How this work differs, what it builds on
Method How the research was done Approach, algorithms, experimental design
Results What was found Data, analysis, statistical significance
Discussion Interpretation of results Implications, limitations, future work
Conclusion Summary of contributions Key takeaways, impact

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Don't: Try to understand everything immediately
  • Don't: Accept claims without evidence
  • Don't: Skip the methodology section
  • Don't: Ignore figures and tables
  • Don't: Forget to summarize after reading

The 5 Levels of Paper Summarization

Progressive Depth Framework

Different purposes require different levels of summary. Understanding these levels helps you choose the right approach for your needs.

Level Title Description When to Use
Level 1 Annotated Bibliography Brief summary (2-3 sentences): what the paper is about, relevance, and your opinion Building a reference library, quick screening
Level 2 Summary Longer, detailed summary (1-2 paragraphs) of the paper's content Personal notes, sharing with colleagues
Level 3 Critique Critical analysis: strengths, weaknesses, contributions, limitations Deep understanding, evaluating for your work
Level 4 Peer Review Similar to Level 3, plus recommendation to an editor (accept/reject) Reviewing for journals/conferences
Level 5 Literature Survey Covers several papers with integrated narrative and reflection Writing literature reviews, research proposals

Level 1: Annotated Bibliography

Example

Paper: "TensorFlow: A System for Large-Scale Machine Learning"

Annotation: This paper presents TensorFlow, Google's open-source machine learning framework for distributed computing. The system uses dataflow graphs and supports heterogeneous devices. Relevant for understanding modern ML infrastructure. Well-written but assumes familiarity with ML concepts.

Level 2: Summary

Template

  • Problem: What problem does the paper address?
  • Approach: How do the authors approach the problem?
  • Results: What are the main findings?
  • Contributions: What are the key contributions?
  • Relevance: Why did you read it? How does it relate to your work?

Level 3: Critique

Critical Analysis Framework

Think beyond what the paper says - evaluate HOW and WHY

Questions to Ask:

  • Validity: Are the methods sound? Are conclusions supported by data?
  • Significance: Does this work matter? Is it novel?
  • Strengths: What does the paper do well?
  • Weaknesses: What are the limitations?
  • Assumptions: What assumptions are made? Are they reasonable?
  • Comparisons: How does this compare to other approaches?
  • Future Work: What questions remain unanswered?

Level 5: Literature Survey

Key Characteristics

  • Multiple papers: Synthesizes findings from many sources
  • Integrated narrative: Not just a list, but a story
  • Critical reflection: Identifies trends, gaps, contradictions
  • Organization: Structured by themes, chronology, or methodology
  • Synthesis: Presents the "bigger picture" of the research area

Best Practices for Summarization

  • Always use your own words - avoid copying text
  • Be consistent - use the same format for all summaries
  • Be selective - focus on what's most relevant
  • Stay organized - use reference managers (Mendeley, Zotero)
  • Update regularly - revisit and refine your summaries

Literature Review

What is a Literature Review?

A narrative summary of published works on a focused topic, presenting a critical analysis with an underlying story.

Purpose of Literature Reviews

Context Purpose
New Project Become familiar with a new topic, generate ideas, ensure problem hasn't been solved
Writing Papers/Dissertations Provide context, show how your approach differs, demonstrate novelty
Research Contribution Help others understand a field, identify trends, present new perspectives

Critical Analysis in Literature Reviews

A literature review is NOT just a list of paper summaries!

Think about the literature as a whole:

  • Identify different approaches to solving problems
  • Identify contradictions in findings or methods
  • Analyze strengths and weaknesses of solutions
  • Synthesize the bigger picture - what's the current state of the art?
  • Reach conclusions - trends, gaps, future directions

Planning Your Literature Review

Step 1: Define Your Aims

Be clear about WHY you're conducting the review:

  • To understand an unfamiliar topic?
  • To critically evaluate a research area?
  • To identify approaches for a research problem?
  • To identify research gaps?
  • To provide background for a paper/dissertation?

Step 2: Define Scope

What will and won't you include?

  • Not too broad: "Machine learning" is too general
  • Not too narrow: Might miss important works
  • Find balance: Specific enough to be useful, broad enough to be comprehensive

Step 3: Structure the Review

How will you organize the discussion?

  • Chronological: Show development over time
  • Thematic: Organize by topics or approaches
  • Methodological: Group by research methods
  • Mixed: Combine approaches as needed

Organizing Your Literature Review

Example Structure: Computer Vision Object Detection

  1. Introduction: Overview of object detection problem
  2. Traditional Approaches (chronological within)
    • Viola-Jones detectors
    • HOG + SVM methods
    • Deformable part models
  3. Deep Learning Era (by approach type)
    • Two-stage detectors (R-CNN family)
    • Single-stage detectors (YOLO, SSD)
    • Transformer-based methods
  4. Discussion: Trends, trade-offs, future directions
  5. Conclusion: State of the art and research gaps

Writing the Literature Review

How Much to Write About Each Paper?

"It varies" - write as little as necessary to say as much as you need to

More detail for papers that:

  • Make particularly important contributions
  • Are directly relevant to your work
  • Describe methods you intend to use
  • Represent seminal works in the field

Less detail for papers that:

  • Are minor extensions of discussed work
  • Are tangentially related
  • Can be grouped with similar approaches

Your Literature Review Should:

  • ✓ Have a clear narrative that tells a story
  • ✓ Critically reflect on papers individually and collectively
  • ✓ Synthesize findings across multiple papers
  • ✓ Use literature to support your arguments
  • ✓ Reach meaningful conclusions
  • ✓ Identify implications for your work (if appropriate)

It Should NOT:

  • ✗ Be just a list of paper summaries
  • ✗ Accept all claims without critical evaluation
  • ✗ Miss important connections between works
  • ✗ Lack a clear organizational structure

Systematic Reviews

A rigorous and systematic type of literature review with:

  • Clearly defined research questions
  • Specified search terms and methods
  • Clear inclusion/exclusion criteria
  • Systematic method for extracting information
  • Often used for meta-analysis

Most common in medicine, but also used in computer science

Searching for Literature

The Challenge

How do you find relevant literature in a sea of millions of papers?

Key Resources

Resource Description Best For
Google Scholar Broad academic search engine Initial searches, finding citations
ACM Digital Library Computing research database CS-specific papers, ACM publications
IEEE Xplore Engineering/technology database Engineering, hardware, systems papers
University Library Institutional access to databases Access to paywalled content
arXiv Preprint repository Latest research, pre-publication

Search Strategy

1. Find a Starting Point

  • Ask supervisors/experts for key papers
  • Use search engines with relevant keywords
  • Look for recent survey/review papers
  • Identify seminal papers in the area

2. Look at References

  • Review the bibliography of starting papers
  • Skim abstracts to screen for relevance
  • Add relevant papers to your list

3. Use Advanced Search Features

  • Related articles: Find similar papers
  • Cited by: Find newer papers that cite this work
  • Same authors: Find other papers by key researchers
  • Filter by date: Find recent work
  • Filter by venue: Focus on top conferences/journals

4. Branch Out and Repeat

  • Follow leads from quality papers
  • Identify new keywords and search terms
  • Build your reading list iteratively

Finding Quality Papers

Quality Indicators

  • Citation count: Highly cited papers often have high impact (but this favors older papers)
  • Publication venue: Top-tier conferences and journals (e.g., ACM, IEEE)
  • Author reputation: Known researchers in the field
  • Clarity and rigor: Well-written with sound methodology

Search Terms

Building Effective Search Queries

Start with key concepts:

  • Identify core terms from paper titles and abstracts
  • Look for keywords listed in papers

Expand with synonyms:

  • "virtual reality" → "VR", "mixed reality", "XR", "augmented reality"
  • "machine learning" → "ML", "statistical learning", "AI"

Use Boolean operators:

  • AND: narrow search ("deep learning" AND "healthcare")
  • OR: broaden search ("VR" OR "virtual reality")
  • NOT: exclude terms ("apple" NOT "fruit")

Advanced Search Tips

ACM & IEEE Digital Libraries

  • Filter by sponsor: SIGCHI (HCI), SIGIR (Information Retrieval), SIGAI (AI)
  • Filter by conference/journal: Target specific venues
  • Filter by date: Focus on recent or historical work
  • Use field-specific search: Title, abstract, keywords, full text

Keep Notes on Your Search Process

Document your search strategy to avoid repeating work:

  • Search terms used
  • Databases searched
  • Advanced search paths taken
  • Papers screened and inclusion decisions
  • Date of searches

Organizing Your Findings

Best Practices

  • Use a reference manager: Mendeley, Zotero, EndNote
  • Collect complete metadata: Authors, title, venue, DOI, etc.
  • Tag and categorize: Use keywords and folders
  • Take notes: Write summaries as you read
  • Track relevance: Note why each paper matters to your work

Common Search Mistakes

  • ❌ Stopping after the first few results
  • ❌ Using only one database or search engine
  • ❌ Not following cited references
  • ❌ Ignoring recent papers
  • ❌ Not keeping track of search strategy
  • ❌ Forgetting to collect complete bibliographic data

Citing Work Properly

Why Citations Matter

Citations serve to:

  • Acknowledge the work of others
  • Provide evidence for claims
  • Enable readers to find source material
  • Demonstrate your engagement with the literature
  • Avoid plagiarism

Citation Formats

Format Description Example Common In
Numeric Numbers in brackets, references numbered Freeman et al. [7] argued... Computing science (IEEE, ACM)
Author-Year Author name and year Freeman et al. (2009) argued... Social sciences, some CS venues

Citation Best Practices

DO:

  • ✓ Always name the source: "Freeman et al. [3] proposed..."
  • ✓ Use "et al." for three or more authors
  • ✓ Place citations before your own commentary
  • ✓ Group multiple citations: [4, 8-10] not [4], [8], [9], [10]
  • ✓ Cite the most relevant, original source
  • ✓ Include page numbers for specific quotes or claims

DON'T:

  • ✗ Use citations as nouns: "In [3], a new method..." (WRONG)
  • ✗ Cite without reading the paper
  • ✗ Over-cite: don't cite common knowledge
  • ✗ Under-cite: always acknowledge others' ideas
  • ✗ Put citations after your own critique of the work

Example Citations

Good Examples:

  • ✓ "Freeman et al. [3] proposed a new method for..."
  • ✓ "Others [3] proposed a new method for..."
  • ✓ "A new method [3] was proposed for..."
  • ✓ "Several approaches have been developed [3-7, 12]..."

Bad Examples:

  • ✗ "In [3], a new method was proposed..."
  • ✗ "Freeman et al. suggested that..., but this has not been validated [1]"

Bibliography/Reference List

Essential Elements

Complete bibliographic entries typically include:

  • Authors: Full names (format varies by style)
  • Year: Publication year
  • Title: Paper/article title
  • Venue: Conference/journal name
  • Publisher: Publishing organization
  • Pages: Page numbers
  • DOI: Digital Object Identifier (when available)

Using Reference Managers

Recommended Tools

Tool Pros Best For
Mendeley Free, PDF management, social features General use, collaboration
Zotero Open source, browser integration Web research, flexibility
EndNote Powerful features, institutional support Advanced users, specific needs
BibTeX LaTeX integration, plain text CS papers, technical writing

Pro Tips

  • Collect metadata early: Save complete citation info when you find papers
  • Double-check DOIs: Ensure they're correct and complete
  • Use citation styles: Let tools format citations automatically
  • Sync across devices: Keep your library accessible everywhere
  • Backup regularly: Don't lose your reference library!

What is "et al." used for?

Latin for "and others"

Used when citing papers with three or more authors

Example: Freeman et al. [3]
Instead of: Freeman, Smith, and Jones [3]

What is a DOI?

Digital Object Identifier

A unique, permanent identifier for published works

Ensures papers can be found even if URLs change

Example: 10.1145/1234567.1234568
