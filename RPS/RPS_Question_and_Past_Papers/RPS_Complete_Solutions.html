1. Exam Style Questions

1a Multiple Choice: Scientific Approach 1 Mark
A scientific approach...
a. compares observations with predictions
b. only considers cultural facts
c. requires gathering lots of information
Answer: (a)

The scientific method is fundamentally about making predictions based on hypotheses and then comparing those predictions with actual observations. This is the core of empirical research.

1b Hypothesis Evaluation 1 Mark
Consider the hypothesis "Mary's machine learning algorithm is better than John's"...
a. Running the experiment more than once will ensure generalisability
b. 'Better' needs to be defined more carefully
c. Testing this hypothesis means running both algorithms with different data
Answer: (b)

A good hypothesis must be specific and measurable. The term "better" is vague and could mean:

  • Higher accuracy
  • Faster execution time
  • Lower memory usage
  • Better generalization to unseen data

Without defining what "better" means, the hypothesis cannot be properly tested or falsified.

1c Ethics in Human Experimentation 1 Mark
Considering ethics in human experimentation...
a. means allowing participants to take as long as they like on an experimental task
b. ensures that any risk to the participants' well-being is reduced as much as possible
c. is important to ensure that your paper will be published
Answer: (b)

Ethics in research is primarily about protecting participants. Key ethical principles include:

  • Minimizing risk to participants' physical and psychological well-being
  • Informed consent - participants understand what they're agreeing to
  • Right to withdraw at any time
  • Confidentiality and data protection
  • Beneficence - maximizing benefits while minimizing harm
1d Nature of Hypotheses 1 Mark
A hypothesis…
a. even if supported by evidence now, may be rejected later
b. is always rephrased as a research question
c. should always be phrased so that it is impossible to prove wrong
Answer: (a)

Scientific knowledge is provisional and falsifiable. Key points:

  • Hypotheses are never "proven" definitively - they are supported or rejected by evidence
  • New evidence or better experimental methods may lead to rejection of previously accepted hypotheses
  • This is a strength of the scientific method, not a weakness
  • Example: Newtonian physics was supported for centuries but was refined by Einstein's relativity
Note on Falsifiability:
Option (c) is wrong because hypotheses should be falsifiable - it must be possible to prove them wrong through experimentation.
1e GDPR Principles 6 Marks
Describe 3 principles of GDPR and give an example of each principle in relation to the University's storage of data about students
Answer

1. Lawfulness, Fairness, and Transparency (2 marks)

Principle: Data must be processed lawfully, fairly, and in a transparent manner. Data subjects must be informed about how their data is being used.

University Example: The University must clearly inform students at enrollment about what personal data is collected (name, address, student ID, academic records), why it's collected (for educational administration, degree certification), and how it will be used. This is typically done through privacy notices and data protection policies provided during registration.

2. Purpose Limitation (2 marks)

Principle: Personal data must be collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a manner incompatible with those purposes.

University Example: Student data collected for academic purposes (e.g., tracking grades, attendance, course enrollment) cannot be used for unrelated purposes such as marketing to external companies or sharing with third parties without explicit consent. If the University collected email addresses for sending course materials, they cannot use the same emails for promoting commercial products.

3. Data Minimization (2 marks)

Principle: Only data that is adequate, relevant, and necessary for the purposes should be collected and processed.

University Example: When enrolling students, the University should only collect information necessary for education delivery and administration (name, contact details, academic qualifications). They should not collect unnecessary information such as political affiliations, religious beliefs, or extensive family background unless directly relevant to specific support services with proper justification.

Other GDPR Principles (for reference):
  • Accuracy: Data must be accurate and kept up to date
  • Storage Limitation: Data kept only as long as necessary
  • Integrity and Confidentiality: Data must be kept secure
  • Accountability: Data controllers must demonstrate compliance
1f Characteristics of Good Research Papers 6 Marks
Discuss 3 characteristics of a good research paper
Answer

1. Clear and Well-Defined Research Question/Hypothesis (2 marks)

A good research paper addresses a specific, focused research question or tests a well-formulated hypothesis. The research objective should be:

  • Clearly stated in the introduction
  • Specific enough to be answerable within the scope of the paper
  • Significant and contributing to the field
  • Testable or investigable using appropriate methods

This gives the paper direction and allows readers to understand exactly what problem is being addressed and why it matters.

2. Rigorous and Reproducible Methodology (2 marks)

The research methods should be:

  • Well-documented: Sufficient detail for others to replicate the study
  • Appropriate: Methods suit the research question
  • Justified: Explanation of why particular methods were chosen
  • Valid and reliable: Proper experimental design, controls, and measurements

Reproducibility is a cornerstone of scientific research - other researchers should be able to follow the methodology and verify the results.

3. Critical Analysis and Sound Conclusions (2 marks)

A good research paper demonstrates:

  • Proper analysis: Results are analyzed using appropriate statistical or qualitative methods
  • Honest interpretation: Results are discussed objectively, including limitations
  • Evidence-based conclusions: Claims are supported by the data presented
  • Contribution to knowledge: Clear articulation of what new insights the research provides
  • Acknowledgment of limitations: Honest discussion of what the study cannot conclude
Other Important Characteristics:
  • Proper literature review: Situates work within existing research
  • Clear writing: Well-organized, grammatically correct, appropriate academic style
  • Proper citations: All sources appropriately referenced
  • Ethical compliance: Research conducted ethically with appropriate approvals
2a MegaMerge vs KiloMerge - Hypothesis 2 Marks
You have developed a new algorithm, called MegaMerge (MM), for joining multiple lists of numbers together then sorting them into ascending order. You believe it does this faster than your existing algorithm called KiloMerge. State this belief as a testable hypothesis.
Answer

Testable Hypothesis:

"The MegaMerge algorithm will complete the merging and sorting of multiple lists in less time than the KiloMerge algorithm when tested on the same input data."

Alternative formulations (also acceptable):

  • "MegaMerge has a lower average execution time than KiloMerge for merging and sorting multiple lists of numbers."
  • "The mean processing time of MegaMerge will be significantly less than the mean processing time of KiloMerge when merging and sorting identical sets of number lists."
Key Elements of This Hypothesis:
  • Comparative: Compares MM vs KM
  • Measurable: "time" or "execution time" is quantifiable
  • Specific: Defines what is being measured (time to merge and sort)
  • Testable: Can run experiments to verify or falsify
  • Directional: Predicts MM will be faster (less time)
2b Good Hypothesis Criteria 3 Marks
Explain how it meets the essential criteria of a good hypothesis
Answer

1. Testable and Falsifiable (1 mark)

The hypothesis can be tested through experimentation by running both algorithms on the same datasets and measuring their execution times. It is also falsifiable - if MegaMerge takes equal or more time than KiloMerge, the hypothesis would be rejected. This is essential because a hypothesis that cannot be proven wrong is not scientific.

2. Specific and Measurable (1 mark)

The hypothesis specifies exactly what will be measured: execution time. This is a concrete, quantifiable dependent variable that can be accurately measured in milliseconds or seconds. It clearly defines the task (merging and sorting lists) and the comparison being made (MM vs KM), leaving no ambiguity about what success looks like.

3. Based on a Logical Foundation (1 mark)

The hypothesis stems from an observation or belief about algorithm performance, which is grounded in the researcher's understanding of the algorithms. It makes a clear prediction (MM is faster) that can be empirically validated. The comparison is fair and meaningful - both algorithms perform the same task, making the comparison of their performance valid and relevant.

Additional Criteria Met:
  • Clear variables: Independent variable = algorithm type (MM vs KM); Dependent variable = execution time
  • Directional: States the expected direction of the result (faster/less time)
  • Relevant: Algorithm efficiency is practically important
2c Increasing Generalizability 4 Marks
Describe 2 ways in which the generalizability of this experiment might be increased
Answer

1. Vary the Input Data Characteristics (2 marks)

Test the algorithms with diverse input datasets that vary in:

  • List sizes: Small (10-100 elements), medium (1,000-10,000), and large lists (100,000+)
  • Number of lists: Test with 2, 5, 10, 50, and 100+ lists to merge
  • Data patterns: Already sorted, reverse sorted, random, partially sorted, duplicates
  • Data types: Integers, floating-point numbers, negative and positive values
  • Value ranges: Small ranges (1-100) and large ranges (1-1,000,000)

Why this increases generalizability: Testing with diverse data ensures the findings apply broadly, not just to one specific type of input. If MM is faster only on small sorted lists but slower on large random lists, this would be discovered and reported, giving a more complete picture of real-world performance.

2. Test Across Different Hardware and Software Environments (2 marks)

Run the experiment on different computing platforms:

  • Different hardware: Various CPUs (Intel, AMD, ARM), different amounts of RAM (4GB, 8GB, 16GB+), different storage types (SSD vs HDD)
  • Different operating systems: Windows, Linux, macOS
  • Different programming language implementations: If possible, implement in Python, Java, C++, etc.
  • Different system loads: Test on idle systems vs systems under load

Why this increases generalizability: Algorithm performance can vary significantly across different environments. Testing across platforms ensures the results are not specific to one particular setup and will be applicable to diverse real-world deployment scenarios where users may have different hardware/software configurations.

Other Valid Approaches:
  • Multiple trials: Run each test many times (e.g., 1000+ iterations) to account for variability
  • Statistical rigor: Use proper statistical tests to ensure results are significant
  • Independent validation: Have other researchers replicate the experiments

Answer

Primary Answer: Execution Time (in milliseconds or seconds)

This measures how long each algorithm takes to complete the merge and sort operation. It is:

  • Quantitative: Numerical measurement

3 App Icon Selection Experiment 15+ Marks
You have been given a set of 100 newly designed app icon designs that are not yet associated with any existing app. You have created an app to help people remember other people's names. Design an experiment to answer the following research question: "Which icon should you use as the icon of your new app"
Complete Experimental Design

Research Question Refinement

Refined Research Question: "Which icon design best represents the app's purpose (helping users remember names) and is most memorable, recognizable, and appealing to potential users?"

Hypothesis

"Icons that incorporate visual elements related to memory, people, or names (such as brain imagery, person silhouettes, or text symbols) will be rated more highly for app relevance, memorability, and appeal than abstract or unrelated icons."

Experimental Design

Phase 1: Icon Screening (Reducing from 100 to 10-15)

Method: Expert Review + Quick User Survey

  • Expert Panel: 3-5 UX/UI designers eliminate clearly inappropriate icons (too complex, unclear, similar to existing apps, poor quality)
  • Quick Survey: 50 participants rate 100 icons on a scale of 1-5 for "first impression appeal"
  • Selection: Choose top 10-15 icons based on combined expert judgment and user ratings

Phase 2: Main Experiment (Testing Shortlisted Icons)

Participants:

  • 100-150 participants representative of target user base
  • Mix of ages (20-60), genders, and tech-savviness levels
  • Recruited through university mailing lists, social media, and participant pools

Independent Variables:

  • Icon design (10-15 different designs)

Dependent Variables:

Type Variable Measurement Method
Quantitative Relevance to app purpose 7-point Likert scale: "How well does this icon represent an app for remembering names?"
Quantitative Visual appeal 7-point Likert scale: "How visually appealing is this icon?"
Quantitative Memorability Recognition test: Show all icons briefly, then ask participants to identify which they saw (measured as % correctly recognized)
Quantitative Download intention 7-point Likert scale: "How likely would you be to download an app with this icon?"
Qualitative Perceived functionality Open question: "What do you think this app does?"
Qualitative Icon associations Open question: "What words or concepts come to mind when you see this icon?"

Experimental Procedure:

  1. Introduction (2 min): Explain purpose, obtain informed consent, assure anonymity
  2. Initial Viewing (3 min): Show all 10-15 icons simultaneously on screen for 30 seconds (for later memory test)
  3. Individual Icon Evaluation (15-20 min):
    • Present each icon individually in randomized order
    • For each icon, participants complete ratings and answer open questions
    • Show icon against both light and dark backgrounds
  4. Memory Test (3 min):
    • Show 20 icons (the 10-15 test icons plus 5-10 distractors)
    • Ask participants to identify which icons they saw earlier
  5. Final Preference (2 min): Ask participants to select their top 3 icons and explain why
  6. Demographics (2 min): Collect age, gender, tech experience, frequency of downloading apps

Control Measures:

  • Randomize icon presentation order for each participant
  • Use same screen size, resolution, and brightness for all tests
  • Present icons at same size (e.g., 512x512 pixels)
  • Conduct experiment in quiet, distraction-free environment
  • Don't tell participants the app's actual purpose until after rating "perceived functionality"

Data Collection and Analysis

Quantitative Analysis:

  • Calculate mean and standard deviation for each Likert scale measure per icon
  • Use repeated-measures ANOVA to test for significant differences between icons
  • Calculate recognition accuracy (% correct identifications) for each icon
  • Create composite score: weighted average of relevance (40%), appeal (30%), memorability (20%), and download intention (10%)
  • Rank icons by composite score

Qualitative Analysis:

  • Code open-ended responses using thematic analysis
  • Identify common themes in perceived functionality (correct vs incorrect interpretations)
  • Analyze word associations to understand what each icon communicates
  • Look for negative associations or confusion

Ethics Considerations

  • Informed Consent: Participants informed about study purpose, duration (~30 min), right to withdraw
  • Data Protection: Anonymize all data, store securely, comply with GDPR
  • No Deception: Brief deception about app purpose (revealed after functionality question) is minimal and justified; full debrief provided
  • Minimal Risk: No physical or psychological risk; participants can take breaks
  • Compensation: Provide reasonable compensation (£10 gift voucher or course credit)
  • Ethics Approval: Obtain approval from university ethics committee before starting

Decision Criteria

Select the icon that:

  1. Scores highest on the weighted composite score
  2. Has >70% participants correctly identify it relates to memory/names/people
  3. Has high recognition rate (>60% in memory test)
  4. Receives minimal negative qualitative feedback
  5. Appeals across different demographic groups (check for age/gender differences)

Limitations and Future Work

  • Limitation: Icons tested in isolation, not in context of app store browsing
  • Future test: A/B testing with actual app store listings to measure real download rates
  • Limitation: Static evaluation doesn't test long-term memorability
  • Future test: Longitudinal study testing recall after 1 week, 1 month
Key Strengths of This Design:
  • Multi-phase approach reduces workload (screening then detailed testing)
  • Combines multiple metrics rather than relying on single measure
  • Includes both quantitative (ratings, recognition) and qualitative (open responses) data
  • Tests actual memory performance, not just self-reported memorability
  • Considers diverse participant demographics
  • Clear decision criteria for selecting final icon

1e Database Copyright Requirements 4 Marks
Describe four requirements for a database to be considered protected by copyright / intellectual property rights.
Answer

1. Substantial Investment (1 mark)

There must be substantial investment in obtaining, verifying, or presenting the contents of the database. This investment can be:

  • Financial resources
  • Human resources (time and effort)
  • Technical resources

The investment must be in the creation of the database itself, not just the data within it.

2. Originality in Selection or Arrangement (1 mark)

The selection or arrangement of the contents must constitute the author's own intellectual creation. This means:

  • The way data is organized shows creativity or intellectual effort
  • Not just a simple, obvious arrangement (e.g., pure alphabetical listing)
  • Reflects authorship through choices made in structure and organization

3. Fixed in Tangible Form (1 mark)

The database must be recorded in some permanent or stable form:

  • Digital storage (hard drives, cloud servers)
  • Physical media (printed, CD-ROM)
  • Must be more than just an idea or concept

4. Systematic or Methodical Arrangement (1 mark)

The database must have:

  • A clear organizational structure
  • Contents arranged systematically or methodically
  • Individual elements accessible through some method (search, indexing, etc.)
  • Not just a random collection of data
Additional Considerations:
  • Sui generis database right (EU): Separate protection for databases showing substantial investment, even without creative selection
  • Duration: Copyright typically lasts 70 years after creator's death; database rights may be shorter
  • Exclusions: Facts themselves are not copyrightable, only the creative arrangement

2 Computing + Physics Students Programming Improvement 9 Marks

A researcher is investigating whether first year Computing Science students who also study Physics show greater improvement in their programming abilities by the end of the year than those who do not.

The researcher has planned a simple experiment to test this belief. First year Computing Science students will complete a multiple-choice programming quiz at the end of the academic year. The researcher will then compare the mean score from each group of students (i.e., Physics vs non-Physics) to see if the mean is higher for Physics students.

Critique this experiment design by explaining three ways in which the proposed experiment may be unable to answer the research question. Propose changes to address any issues you identified.

Answer

Issue 1: No Baseline Measurement (3 marks)

Problem: The research question asks about "improvement" (change over time), but the experiment only measures ability at the END of the year. This cannot show improvement because:

  • We don't know students' programming abilities at the start of the year
  • Physics students might have already been better programmers initially
  • A higher end score doesn't necessarily mean greater improvement
  • Can't determine if observed difference is due to improvement or pre-existing differences

Solution:

  • Conduct a pre-test at the beginning of the year (Week 1) using a similar programming quiz
  • Conduct post-test at the end of the year
  • Calculate improvement score for each student: (post-test score - pre-test score)
  • Compare mean improvement between Physics and non-Physics groups
  • This directly measures what the research question asks: whether Physics students show "greater improvement"

Issue 2: Confounding Variables Not Controlled (3 marks)

Problem: Many factors other than studying Physics could explain differences in programming ability:

  • Prior programming experience: Some students may have coded before university
  • Mathematical ability: Students taking Physics likely have stronger maths, which helps programming
  • Motivation and study time: Physics students might be more motivated or spend more time studying overall
  • Other subjects: What other courses are students taking? Different combinations could affect programming
  • Demographics: Age, gender, educational background may differ between groups

Solution:

  • Collect demographic data: Prior programming experience, maths background, other courses, age, gender
  • Statistical control: Use ANCOVA (Analysis of Covariance) to control for baseline differences in programming ability and mathematical skills
  • Matching: Create matched pairs of students (one Physics, one non-Physics) with similar characteristics
  • Measure and control for:
    • Initial programming knowledge (pre-test)
    • Mathematics grade/ability
    • Hours per week spent on programming coursework
  • Better still: Include these as covariates in statistical analysis to isolate the effect of Physics study

Issue 3: Multiple-Choice Quiz May Not Adequately Measure Programming Ability (3 marks)

Problem: Programming ability is complex and multifaceted; a multiple-choice quiz has serious limitations:

  • Limited validity: MCQs test knowledge recall and code reading, not actual programming skill (writing code, debugging, problem-solving)
  • Guessing: Students can guess answers (25% chance with 4 options), adding noise to data
  • Surface-level: Doesn't test ability to design solutions, write original code, or debug complex problems
  • Doesn't reflect real programming: In practice, programmers write code, not select from options

Solution:

  • Multi-method assessment combining:
    • Practical coding tasks: Give students actual programming problems to solve (e.g., write a function to sort an array)
    • Code writing: Assess their written code for correctness, style, efficiency
    • Debugging task: Give them buggy code to fix
    • MCQ component: Can still include for theoretical knowledge, but shouldn't be sole measure
  • Automated testing: Use automated test suites to objectively assess if their code works correctly
  • Rubric scoring: Develop detailed rubric for assessing code quality (not just correctness, but also style, efficiency, clarity)
  • Multiple indicators: Consider also using:
    • Course grades in programming modules
    • Lab exercise completion rates and scores
    • Self-assessment questionnaires on programming confidence
Additional Issues That Could Be Mentioned:
  • Self-selection bias: Students who choose Physics may systematically differ from those who don't (could use propensity score matching)
  • Sample size: Need sufficient participants in each group for statistical power
  • Attrition: Some students may drop out during the year - need plan for missing data
  • Single time point: Could measure at multiple points (mid-year, end-year) to track improvement trajectory
3 Pair Programming Experiment
You are planning an experiment to investigate if paired programming helps novice Java programmers solve lab exercises during a programming course.

Note: Pair programming is a software development technique in which two programmers work together at a single workstation. One programmer, the driver, writes code while the other, the observer, reviews each line of code as it is typed in.

Part (a): Within-Subject vs Between-Subject Design 6 Marks

Discuss the advantages of both within-subject and between-subject designs for this experiment. Clearly link your identified advantages to the given experimental scenario. Indicate which approach you would choose and justify your choice.
Answer

Within-Subject Design (same participants do both conditions)

Advantages for this scenario:

  • Controls for individual differences (2 marks):
    • Each participant acts as their own control
    • Eliminates variance due to different programming ability levels, learning styles, motivation, prior experience
    • Relevant to scenario: Novice programmers vary widely in ability; this design ensures we're comparing the same person's performance under both conditions
  • More statistical power with fewer participants (1 mark):
    • Needs fewer participants to detect an effect
    • Relevant to scenario: May be limited pool of novice Java programmers in a single course

Disadvantages for this scenario:

  • Order effects: Performance on second condition affected by doing first condition (learning, fatigue, boredom)
  • Practice effects: Participants get better at programming with practice, so second task may show improvement regardless of condition
  • Need counterbalancing: Must vary which condition comes first

Between-Subject Design (different participants in each condition)

Advantages for this scenario:

  • No order/practice effects (1 mark):
    • Each participant only experiences one condition
    • Learning from first condition doesn't contaminate second
    • Relevant to scenario: In programming, skills learned working alone might transfer to pair work (or vice versa), confounding results
  • Simpler to implement (1 mark):
    • No need to create matched pairs of equivalent tasks
    • No counterbalancing required
    • Relevant to scenario: Easier to integrate into actual lab sessions - some students work in pairs, others alone

Disadvantages for this scenario:

  • Individual differences: Groups may differ in programming ability, affecting results
  • Need more participants: Requires larger sample size for same statistical power

Recommended Choice: Between-Subject Design (1 mark)

Justification:

  • Learning transfer is major concern: In a programming course, skills learned while pair programming (communication, code review, collaborative problem-solving) would likely transfer to solo work, making within-subject comparison invalid
  • Practice effects too strong: The same programming concepts practiced in condition 1 would make condition 2 easier, regardless of whether it's paired or solo
  • Practical implementation: Can randomly assign students to paired or solo condition at start of course and maintain throughout, mimicking real learning environment
  • More ecologically valid: Students typically either learn with pair programming or without - switching back and forth is unrealistic

To address between-subject disadvantages:

  • Use random assignment to minimize group differences
  • Measure baseline programming ability (pre-test) and use as covariate in analysis
  • Ensure sufficient sample size (power analysis to determine N needed)

Part (b): Dependent Variables 4 Marks

Identify one quantitative and one qualitative dependent variable for this experiment. Justify your choice of each chosen variable.
Answer

Quantitative Dependent Variable (2 marks)

Variable: Number of lab exercises completed correctly

Justification:

  • Directly measures effectiveness: Completion rate directly indicates whether pair programming "helps" solve exercises, as stated in research question
  • Objective and measurable: Clear, countable metric - code either passes test cases or doesn't
  • Relevant to learning outcome: In a programming course, successful task completion is primary indicator of learning and skill development
  • Easy to compare: Can statistically compare mean completion rates between paired and solo groups
  • Practical: Already assessed in typical lab courses through automated testing

Alternative quantitative variables (also acceptable):

  • Time to complete exercises: Efficiency measure
  • Code quality score: Based on rubric (style, efficiency, documentation)
  • Number of bugs/errors: Code correctness measure
  • Lines of code written: Productivity measure

Qualitative Dependent Variable (2 marks)

Variable: Students' perceived learning experience and understanding

Justification:

  • Captures depth of understanding: Students might complete exercises without truly understanding concepts; qualitative data reveals depth of comprehension
  • Explains the "how" and "why": Quantitative data shows if pair programming works; qualitative explains why it works (or doesn't) - e.g., "My partner explained the logic behind loops in a way that finally clicked"
  • Identifies mechanism: Reveals what aspects of pair programming are beneficial (code review, discussing approaches, catching errors) or problematic (one person dominates, disagreements)
  • Contextual richness: Provides insights that numbers alone can't capture about the social and cognitive processes involved
  • Informs practice: Teachers can learn how to better implement pair programming based on student experiences

Alternative qualitative variables (also acceptable):

1. Within-Subject Design Controls for Individual Differences (2 marks)

Positive aspect: Each participant experiences both conditions (paper and app)

Why this is good:

  • Eliminates participant variability: Fitness levels, workout experience, learning speed vary greatly between people. Within-subject design means each person is their own control
  • More statistical power: Can detect smaller differences between conditions with fewer participants
  • Fair comparison: Not confounded by one group happening to have more experienced exercisers

2. Clear, Measurable Dependent Variable (2 marks)

Positive aspect: Uses completion time as the outcome measure

Why this is good:

  • Objective: Time can be measured precisely and consistently (no subjective judgment needed)
  • Quantifiable: Easy to statistically analyze and compare
  • Relevant: Completion time is a reasonable proxy for "ease" - if app makes workout easier to follow, people should complete it faster
  • Practical: Simple to collect and requires no complex equipment
  • Interpretable: Easy to communicate results (e.g., "app users finished 5 minutes faster on average")

3. Standardized Task Ensures Comparability (2 marks)

Positive aspect: All participants complete the same "fixed workout"

Why this is good:

  • Controls task difficulty: Everyone does identical exercises in same order, so differences in completion time reflect the instruction method, not workout difficulty
  • Eliminates confound: Without standardization, different participants might do different exercises, making comparison meaningless
  • Replicable: Other researchers can use the same fixed workout to verify findings
  • Fair test: Both instruction methods (paper and app) are tested on identical content, so neither has an advantage from easier/harder exercises
Other Potential Positive Aspects:
  • Simple design: Straightforward to implement and understand
  • Practical relevance: Tests real-world scenario (people actually use both paper and digital workout guides)
  • Direct comparison: Same workout twice allows clear before/after comparison

Part (b): Four Negative Aspects + Solutions 12 Marks

Critique this experiment by explaining four negative aspects of its design. Propose changes to address any identified negative issues.
Answer

Issue 1: Strong Order Effects - Always Paper First (3 marks)

Problem:

  • Learning/Practice effect: Participants learn the workout routine during the first trial (paper), then perform it better the second time (app) simply due to familiarity, NOT because the app is better
  • They'll remember exercise sequences, proper form, timing - artificially inflating app performance
  • Fatigue effect: Participants might be tired during second workout (app condition), performing worse regardless of instruction method
  • Can't separate: With this fixed order, we can't tell if observed differences are due to instruction method or order effects
  • Confound: Order is completely confounded with condition

Solution:

  • Counterbalancing: Randomly assign half the participants to Paper→App order, other half to App→Paper order
  • This balances out learning and fatigue effects across both conditions
  • Can statistically test for order effects and control for them in analysis
  • Analysis: Use repeated-measures ANOVA with order as a factor to separate order effects from condition effects

Issue 2: Completion Time May Not Measure "Ease" or Quality (3 marks)

Problem:

  • Speed ≠ Ease: Faster completion doesn't necessarily mean "easier" or better workout:
    • Users might rush through video to keep up, sacrificing form
    • Paper users might take time to read carefully and execute properly
    • Faster could mean skipping rest periods or cutting exercises short
  • Quality not measured: No assessment of whether exercises were performed correctly
  • Ease is subjective: Time doesn't capture perceived difficulty, frustration, or satisfaction
  • Wrong conclusion: Might conclude app is "better" when actually users just rushed through it poorly

Solution:

  • Multiple dependent variables:
    • Completion time: Keep as one measure
    • Exercise quality: Video record participants and have trained assessor rate form/technique using rubric
    • Perceived ease: Post-workout questionnaire (7-point scale): "How easy was it to follow the instructions?"
    • Cognitive load: NASA-TLX to measure mental effort required
    • Comprehension: Ask participants to explain what they're supposed to do before each exercise
  • Define "easier": Clarify what "easier" means - faster AND correct form AND less mental effort AND higher satisfaction

Issue 3: Lacks Control Condition / True Baseline (3 marks)

Problem:

  • No true baseline: Both conditions provide guidance (paper vs. app). We don't know if either is actually helpful compared to no instructions
  • Can't assess magnitude of benefit: Might find app is 2 minutes faster than paper, but maybe both are 20 minutes slower than optimal
  • Missing context: Don't know participants' baseline fitness or workout ability without any guidance
  • Alternative explanation: Maybe experienced exercisers don't need either form of guidance and are slowed down by having to reference instructions

Solution:

  • Add baseline measurement: Before the experiment, have participants perform a different (but similar difficulty) workout without any instructions - just exercise names
  • This measures their baseline ability to complete workouts
  • Three conditions:
    1. Baseline: List of exercise names only
    2. Paper instructions with descriptions/images
    3. App with video demonstrations
  • This shows whether instruction methods actually help, and which helps most
  • Or: Control for experience: Measure prior workout experience and use as covariate in analysis

Issue 4: Single Outcome Measure Prevents Understanding Mechanism (3 marks)

Problem:

  • Black box: Only measuring time doesn't reveal WHY one method might be better
  • No process data: Can't tell if app is better because:
    • Videos clarify exercise form better than images?
    • Timing/pacing is easier to follow?
    • Voice-over instructions free up attention?
    • Motion demonstration is clearer?
  • Limited actionable insights: If paper is better, we don't know what to improve in the app
  • Missed nuances: Maybe app is great for some exercises but confusing for others

Solution:

  • Qualitative data collection:
    • Think-aloud protocol: Ask participants to verbalize thoughts while working out ("I'm confused about hand position," "This video makes it clear")
    • Post-workout interview: "Which exercises were hardest to understand?" "What did you like/dislike about each instruction method?"
  • Process measures:
    • Count how often participants refer back to instructions
    • Note where participants pause or hesitate (indicates confusion)
    • Video analysis: Track incorrect movements to see where instructions failed
  • Exercise-level analysis: Break down time/quality by individual exercise to identify which benefit most from video vs. paper
  • Benefit: Provides explanatory power - not just "what works" but "why it works" and "how to improve"
Other Valid Negative Aspects:
  • No rest period between workouts: Participants doing same workout twice back-to-back may be too fatigued (solution: test on different days or use different workouts for each condition)
  • Participant characteristics not controlled: Prior fitness, smartphone comfort, reading ability could affect results (solution: measure and control for these)
  • Small sample size likely: May not have statistical power (solution: conduct power analysis to determine N needed)
  • Ecological validity: Real users wouldn't do same workout twice in a row (solution: use different but equivalent workouts, or test on different days)

Part (c): Inductive vs Deductive Inference 2 Marks

Describe two reasons why inductive inference would be used to evaluate the hypothesis of this experiment opposed to deductive inference.
Answer

Understanding the Difference:

  • Deductive inference: Logic-based reasoning from general principles to specific conclusions (if premises are true, conclusion must be true). Used in mathematics and formal logic.
  • Inductive inference: Reasoning from specific observations to general conclusions (conclusions are probable, not certain). Used in empirical science.

Reason 1: Dealing with Empirical Data from a Sample (1 mark)

This experiment collects data from a limited sample of participants, not the entire population:

  • Testing maybe 30-50 participants, not all possible people who might use workout apps
  • Inductive reasoning: "These 30 participants completed workouts faster with the app, therefore the app is probably faster for users in general"
  • This is probabilistic, not certain - we infer from sample to population
  • Cannot use deduction: We can't deduce with certainty that all users will benefit; we can only induce that they probably will based on our sample
  • Statistical inference: Uses probability (p-values, confidence intervals) to estimate how likely our sample findings apply to the broader population

Reason 2: Cannot Derive Empirical Facts from Logic Alone (1 mark)

Whether an app is faster than paper instructions is an empirical question that must be answered through observation:

  • Not logically derivable: You can't deduce from first principles whether video or paper instructions are faster - it depends on human psychology, perception, motor learning, etc.
  • Requires observation: Must actually conduct the experiment and observe what happens
  • Inductive approach: Observe specific instances (these participants with this workout) → generalize to broader claim (video instructions generally help with workouts)
  • Example contrast:
    • Deductive (mathematics): "All squares have 4 sides; this shape is a square; therefore it has 4 sides" (logically certain)
    • Inductive (empirical): "These 30 people were faster with the app; therefore the app is probably faster in general" (probable, not certain)
Additional Context:

Why empirical research is always inductive:

  • Measurement error: Our measurements are imperfect
  • Individual variation: Results vary across people
  • Sampling: Can never test everyone
  • Generalization: Want to apply findings beyond our specific experiment

This is why we use statistical significance testing - it's inherently inductive reasoning about probability, not deductive certainty.

  • Unauthorized access (Section 1): Using virus to access data without permission

Key principle: The illegality is in the unauthorized access or damage, not in the virus itself.

  • Distributing virus to infect others' computers (unauthorized access/modification)
  • Using virus to steal data from company systems (unauthorized access)
  • Creating ransomware to extort money (unauthorized impairment + modification)

  • Self-reported data via questionnaires
  • Interviews with researchers
  • Observation notes

  • Quantitative: numerical ratings, time measurements, error counts

3. Analysis Methodology (max 3 marks)

Must be specific with terminology - 'statistical methods' or 'statistical test' is not sufficient

Expected appropriate terms:

  • Independent/repeated-measures (depending on design)
  • Two conditions
  • T-test
  • Reference to null-hypothesis significance testing
  • Test for 'significant difference'
  • Specifying the desired p-value for accepting the hypothesis (e.g., p < 0.05)
  • Stating the null- and alternative-hypothesis

For qualitative data:

  • Open-axial-coding of interview statements
  • Thematic analysis
  • Any other appropriate qualitative analysis technique

4. Ethics Approval (max 3 marks)

Stating Need for Ethics Approval [1 mark]:

Acknowledge that ethics committee approval is required before starting

Informed Consent [1 mark]:

Mentioning obtaining informed consent from participants:

  • Written consent form
  • Explain purpose, procedures, duration
  • Right to withdraw
  • How data will be used

Potential Risks [1 mark]:

Mentioning a potential risk of the experiment to warn participants about:

  • Motion sickness: VR can cause nausea, dizziness, disorientation
  • Physical safety: Risk of tripping or bumping into objects while wearing headset
  • Eye strain: Extended VR use can cause eye fatigue
  • Mitigation: Screen participants for susceptibility, allow breaks, clear play area, have supervisor present

5. Conclusion (max 2 marks)

Indicating how conclusions may be reached from the experiment [1 mark]:

  • Whether using the new locomotion system leads to improved immersion/control
  • If p < 0.05, reject null hypothesis and conclude LocoPro is significantly better
  • Triangulate quantitative and qualitative findings
  • Discuss practical significance alongside statistical significance

A full-marks answer would include:
