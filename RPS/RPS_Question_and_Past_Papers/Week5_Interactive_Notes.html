Qualitative and Quantitative Research Methods

Week 5 Overview

You'll learn about both quantitative and qualitative approaches to research, data collection methods, and analysis techniques.

  • Learn the "5 Ps" framework for data collection
  • Master various data collection methods (surveys, interviews, observations, etc.)

  • Understand mixed-methods research approaches

Why Study Research Methods?

Research methods are essential for conducting rigorous scientific studies in computer science. Whether you're:

  • Evaluating a new system: How do you measure if your new UI is better?
  • Understanding user behavior: What methods reveal how users interact with technology?
  • Testing hypotheses: How do you prove your algorithmic improvement actually works?
  • Writing your dissertation: What methodologies should you employ for your research?

Understanding both quantitative and qualitative methods gives you a complete toolkit for answering research questions.

Quantitative Research Methods

Definition: A method of research that relies on measuring variables using a numerical system, analyzing these measurements using any of a variety of statistical models, and reporting relationships and associations among the studied variables.
Key Feature: Quantitative research data is often standardized and can be easily used to generalize findings.

12 Characteristics of Quantitative Research

1. Numerical Data

Quantitative research focuses on quantifiable data, which can be measured and expressed numerically.

Examples:
  • Survey results
  • Statistical data
  • Experimental measurements
2. Objectivity and Rigor

The research aims to remain objective and free from researcher bias. This is achieved through standardized methods, clear protocols, and statistical tools.

3. Structured Methodology

Quantitative research follows a structured process, including:

  1. Formulation of hypotheses
  2. Data collection
  3. Analysis
  4. Conclusion
4. Use of Statistical Analysis

Data is analyzed using statistical techniques to identify patterns, relationships, and causal effects.

Commonly applied: Descriptive and inferential statistics

5. Large Sample Sizes

Quantitative studies often involve larger sample sizes to ensure the reliability and generalizability of the results.

6. Hypothesis Testing

Researchers often begin with a specific hypothesis or research question and test it using empirical data.

7. Generalizability

The goal is to produce results that can be generalized to a larger population, provided the sample is representative.

8. Controlled Variables

Experiments and studies are designed to control variables and minimize confounding factors to isolate the effects of the variable being studied.

9. Reproducibility

The methods are documented in detail, allowing other researchers to replicate the study to verify results.

10. Deductive Approach

Quantitative research often uses a deductive reasoning process, moving from theory or hypothesis to data collection and analysis to confirm or refute the theory.

11. Focus on Relationships and Patterns

Quantitative research aims to identify trends, relationships, or causation between variables.

12. Use of Numerical Tools and Instruments

It employs tools like surveys, questionnaires, structured interviews, and experiments to collect data systematically.

Qualitative Research Methods

What is Qualitative Research? A method of inquiry focused on understanding human behaviour and the reasons that govern such behaviour. It emphasizes subjective assessments and the exploration of phenomena in their natural settings through methods like interviews, observations, and analysis of texts and artifacts.
Historical Context: Early development of qualitative research is often associated with the field of sociology and anthropology.

Qualitative Research in Computer Science

Although most of scientific research is quantitative, qualitative research methods are common in some areas, such as: HCI, CS Education, and Software Engineering.

Examples:
  • Software Engineering: Investigating software adoption or user behaviour towards a new release
  • HCI: How do users perceive and experience the integration of artificial intelligence in everyday personal technology devices?
Mixed Methods: Combining quantitative and qualitative methods are referred to as "mixed methods" - can provide a comprehensive understanding of a research problem.

Qualitative Research Characteristics

1. Data Collection

Qualitative research often involves collecting data through:

  • Direct observation
  • Interviews
  • Questionnaires
  • Focus groups
  • Participants' diaries
  • Other methods that allow researchers to access the perspectives and experiences of their subjects
2. Subjective Perspective

This approach often seeks to understand how individuals construct their own meaning and experience of the world. It acknowledges that the researcher's own background and perspective can influence the interpretation of the data.

3. Exploratory Nature

It is typically exploratory and is used to gain an understanding of underlying reasons, opinions, and motivations. It provides insights into the problem or helps to develop ideas or hypotheses for potential quantitative research.

4. Contextual Detail

Qualitative research is good at providing rich, detailed descriptions that bring out the nuances of complex situations or phenomena.

5. Analysis

Data analysis in qualitative research is often more interpretative, subjective, and involves identifying patterns and themes in data.

6. Flexibility

Research design in qualitative research is often less structured than in quantitative research, and can evolve as the research progresses.

7. Inductive Approach

In qualitative research, the focus is on exploring and discovering patterns in data, then building theory from the ground up. This is the opposite of the deductive approach used in quantitative research.

8. Small Sample Sizes

Qualitative research often involves smaller sample sizes compared to quantitative research. The emphasis is on depth rather than breadth, providing rich and detailed insights from fewer participants.

9. Natural Settings

Qualitative research often takes place in natural settings, where researchers observe and interact with participants in their everyday environments rather than in controlled laboratory conditions.

10. Researcher as Instrument

The researcher is often the primary instrument for data collection and analysis. Their observations, interpretations, and interactions with participants are central to the research process.

Quantitative vs Qualitative: Detailed Comparison

Important Note: Both approaches are valuable and often complement each other. The choice depends on your research question, objectives, and the nature of the phenomenon you're studying.
Aspect Quantitative Research Qualitative Research
Purpose Test theories, examine relationships between variables, make predictions Explore phenomena, understand experiences, develop theories
Data Type Numerical, standardized Textual, visual, audio - rich descriptions
Sample Size Large samples for statistical power Smaller, purposeful samples for depth
Data Collection Structured instruments (surveys, experiments, tests) Flexible methods (interviews, observations, documents)
Analysis Statistical analysis, mathematical models Thematic analysis, coding, pattern identification
Approach Deductive - starts with theory/hypothesis Inductive - builds theory from data
Objectivity Emphasizes objectivity and removing bias Acknowledges researcher's role and subjectivity
Generalizability High - results can generalize to population Lower - focuses on transferability to similar contexts
Research Questions "How many?", "How much?", "What is the relationship?" "Why?", "How?", "What does it mean?"
Flexibility Structured, predetermined design Flexible, emergent design that can evolve
Reporting Style Numbers, statistics, tables, graphs Narratives, quotes, themes, descriptions
Time Investment Can collect data quickly from many participants Time-intensive data collection and analysis

Quick Check: Which Method?

Question: You want to understand why students prefer one programming language over another for their projects. Which method would be most appropriate?

Answer: Qualitative research would be most appropriate here. The research question asks "why" which requires understanding students' reasoning, experiences, and motivations. Qualitative methods like interviews or focus groups would allow you to explore the depth of their preferences and the reasons behind them.

A quantitative approach could tell you how many students prefer each language, but wouldn't explain the why behind their preferences.

The 5 Ps Framework

What are the 5 Ps? The 5 Ps is a framework for understanding what types of data can be collected in research. It helps researchers think systematically about different aspects they can measure or observe.

Performance

What it measures: How well users complete tasks or achieve goals.

Examples:

  • Task completion time
  • Error rates
  • Accuracy
  • Success rates
  • Learning curves

Preference

What it measures: What users like or choose when given options.

Examples:

  • User rankings
  • Choice between options
  • Favorite features
  • Preferred interfaces
  • Design choices

Perception

What it measures: How users feel about or experience the system.

Examples:

  • Satisfaction ratings
  • Ease of use
  • Enjoyment
  • Frustration levels
  • Confidence

Process

What it measures: How users interact with the system and their behavior.

Examples:

  • Click patterns
  • Navigation paths
  • Interaction sequences
  • Eye tracking data
  • Usage patterns

Product

What it measures: What users create or produce using the system.

Examples:

  • Documents created
  • Code written
  • Designs produced
  • Quality of output
  • Artifacts generated
Using the 5 Ps in Your Research:

When designing a study, consider which of the 5 Ps are most relevant to your research question. You might collect data on one, several, or all five Ps depending on your objectives.

  • For usability studies: Performance, Perception, and Process are often key
  • For comparative studies: Preference becomes crucial
  • For productivity tools: Product and Performance are important

Example Research Study Using the 5 Ps

Study: Evaluating a New Code Editor for Students
  • Performance: Measure how quickly students can complete coding tasks, number of syntax errors
  • Preference: Ask students which editor they prefer (new vs. old) and why
  • Perception: Survey students about ease of use, satisfaction, frustration levels
  • Process: Record which features students use, how they navigate the interface, common workflows
  • Product: Analyze the quality and correctness of the code students produce

Practice: Identify the P

Question: You're studying a new email application and you record which folders users create and how they organize their messages. Which P is this?

Answer: This is Product - the folders and organizational structure are artifacts that users create/produce using the email application.

Note: If you were tracking how they create folders (the steps they take), that would be Process. If you were measuring how quickly they organize emails, that would be Performance.

Data Collection Methods

Key Terminology: Understanding the language of data collection is essential for designing and conducting research.

Important Terminology

Quantitative vs Qualitative Data

Quantitative Data: Numerical data that can be measured and analyzed statistically

  • Numbers, counts, measurements
  • Example: Time taken to complete a task (45 seconds), satisfaction score (7/10)

Qualitative Data: Non-numerical data that describes qualities or characteristics

  • Words, images, observations, descriptions
  • Example: User interview transcripts, observational notes
Open vs Closed Questions

Closed Questions: Have a fixed set of possible responses

  • Yes/No questions
  • Multiple choice
  • Rating scales (1-5)
  • Example: "On a scale of 1-10, how satisfied are you?"
  • Advantage: Easy to analyze, good for quantitative data

Open Questions: Allow free-form responses

  • No predetermined answers
  • Example: "What did you think about the interface?"
  • Advantage: Rich insights, discover unexpected information
  • Challenge: Time-consuming to analyze

Six Main Data Collection Methods

1. Questionnaires (Surveys)

What it is: A set of written questions that participants answer

Best for:

  • Gathering data from large numbers of people
  • Collecting standardized information
  • Quantitative data collection

Advantages:

  • Can reach many participants quickly
  • Cost-effective
  • Standardized responses easy to analyze
  • Participants can complete at their convenience

Disadvantages:

  • No opportunity to ask follow-up questions
  • Risk of misunderstanding questions
  • May have low response rates
  • Limited depth of responses
Example Use Case: Surveying 500 students about their study habits and tool preferences using Google Forms with Likert scale questions and multiple choice.
2. Interviews

What it is: One-on-one conversations with participants to gather in-depth information

Types:

  • Structured: Fixed questions, same order for all participants
  • Semi-structured: Key questions prepared, but flexible to explore interesting topics
  • Unstructured: Informal, conversational approach

Advantages:

  • Rich, detailed data
  • Can ask follow-up questions
  • Can clarify misunderstandings
  • Observe non-verbal cues

Disadvantages:

  • Time-consuming to conduct and analyze
  • Requires skilled interviewer
  • Difficult to generalize findings
  • Potential for interviewer bias
Example Use Case: Conducting 15 semi-structured interviews with developers to understand their experiences with a new IDE feature.
3. Task Sheets / Think-Aloud Protocol

What it is: Participants complete specific tasks while verbalizing their thoughts

How it works:

  • Give participants specific tasks to complete
  • Ask them to "think aloud" - say what they're thinking
  • Record their actions and verbalizations

Advantages:

  • Reveals user's thought process
  • Identifies usability issues in real-time
  • Combines performance data with qualitative insights

Disadvantages:

  • Thinking aloud can affect task performance
  • Not all thoughts are verbalized
  • Some users find it unnatural
Example Use Case: Usability testing where users think aloud while trying to book a flight on a new travel website.
4. Observation

What it is: Watching and recording user behavior in natural or controlled settings

Types:

  • Direct observation: Researcher is present
  • Indirect observation: Video recording for later analysis
  • Participant observation: Researcher participates in the activity

Advantages:

  • Captures actual behavior, not reported behavior
  • Provides contextual understanding
  • Can reveal unconscious actions

Disadvantages:

  • Observer's presence may influence behavior (Hawthorne effect)
  • Time and resource intensive
  • Difficult to record everything
  • Interpretation can be subjective
Example Use Case: Observing how students collaborate in a computer lab to understand their natural workflows and tool usage.
5. Automatic Data Collection

What it is: Using software or sensors to automatically record user behavior and system events

Examples:

  • Web analytics (Google Analytics)
  • Application logging
  • Click tracking and heatmaps
  • Eye tracking
  • Biometric sensors (heart rate, skin conductance)

Advantages:

  • Objective, precise measurements
  • Can collect massive amounts of data
  • No observer bias
  • Captures data unobtrusively

Disadvantages:

  • Privacy and ethical concerns
  • Doesn't capture "why" - only "what"
  • Requires technical setup
  • Can generate overwhelming amounts of data
Example Use Case: Using eye tracking to see where users look on a webpage, combined with click tracking to understand navigation patterns.
6. Artefacts / Document Analysis

What it is: Analyzing existing documents, records, or products created by users

Examples:

  • Code repositories (GitHub commits, pull requests)
  • Design documents
  • Bug reports and issue trackers
  • Forum posts and discussions
  • Student assignments and projects
  • Social media content

Advantages:

  • Non-intrusive - data already exists
  • Can analyze historical data
  • Cost-effective
  • Reflects authentic behavior

Disadvantages:

  • Limited to available documents
  • May lack context
  • Quality and completeness vary
  • Potential privacy/ethical issues
Example Use Case: Analyzing StackOverflow posts to understand common programming challenges students face with a particular library.
Combining Methods:

Often the best research uses multiple data collection methods (triangulation). For example:

  • Questionnaire to identify trends + Interviews to understand why
  • Automatic data collection for behavior + Survey for perceptions
  • Observation during tasks + Think-aloud for insights into thought process

Analyzing Qualitative Data: Coding

What is Coding? Coding is the process of organizing and making sense of qualitative data (like interview transcripts, observations, open-ended survey responses) by identifying patterns, themes, and categories.

Qualitative data analysis often involves coding, a systematic approach to categorizing and interpreting textual data. The most common coding approach follows three stages:

The Three-Stage Coding Process

Stage 1: Open Coding

What it is: Breaking down data into discrete parts and labeling them

Goal: Identify concepts and categories from the data

Process:

  • Read through your data (transcripts, notes, etc.)
  • Assign labels (codes) to meaningful chunks of text
  • Stay close to the data - use participants' own words when possible
  • Ask: "What is this about?" "What is happening here?"
Example:

Interview quote: "I find the IDE really slow when I'm trying to autocomplete. It freezes for a few seconds and I lose my train of thought."

Open codes: "performance issues", "autocomplete lag", "interrupts workflow", "cognitive disruption"

↓

Stage 2: Axial Coding

What it is: Identifying relationships between the categories from open coding

Goal: Connect categories and subcategories, understand how they relate

Process:

  • Look for connections between open codes
  • Group related codes together
  • Identify main categories and subcategories
  • Ask: "How are these codes related?" "What patterns emerge?"
Example:

Grouping codes: "performance issues", "autocomplete lag", "slow compilation", "freezing" → Main category: "System Performance Problems"

"interrupts workflow", "cognitive disruption", "loses focus" → Main category: "Impact on User Experience"

Relationship: System performance problems CAUSE impact on user experience

↓

Stage 3: Selective Coding

What it is: Integrating and refining categories to form a coherent theory or narrative

Goal: Identify the core category that ties everything together

Process:

  • Identify the central phenomenon or core category
  • Relate all major categories to the core category
  • Validate relationships against the data
  • Fill in any gaps in categories
  • Ask: "What is the main story here?" "What is the overarching theme?"
Example:

Core category: "Developer Productivity Barriers in IDE Usage"

This core category encompasses:

  • System Performance Problems (cause)
  • Impact on User Experience (consequence)
  • Workaround Strategies (user response)
  • Tool Abandonment (final outcome)

Best Practices for Coding

Tips for Effective Coding
  • Start broad, then refine: It's okay if your initial codes are messy - you'll refine them
  • Use participants' language: "In vivo codes" (using participants' actual words) can be powerful
  • Be consistent: Apply codes consistently across your data
  • Keep a codebook: Document what each code means
  • Iterate: You'll likely go back and re-code as your understanding develops
  • Check reliability: Have another researcher code some data to check agreement
  • Use software: Tools like NVivo, Atlas.ti, or even Excel can help manage codes
Common Challenges
  • Too many codes: Start with too many rather than too few, then consolidate
  • Overlapping codes: Normal in the early stages - merge or differentiate as you progress
  • Bias: Be aware of your own assumptions affecting how you code
  • Time consuming: Coding takes time - plan accordingly
  • Knowing when to stop: Stop when you reach "saturation" - no new codes emerge

Practice Scenario

Scenario: You're analyzing interview data about students' experiences with online learning. One student says: "The recorded lectures are helpful because I can watch them at my own pace, but I really miss the interaction with classmates."

Question: What are some potential open codes for this quote?

Possible open codes:

  • "flexibility benefits" or "self-paced learning"
  • "recorded lecture advantages"
  • "missing social interaction"
  • "peer interaction valued"
  • "trade-offs in online learning"

In axial coding, you might group "flexibility benefits" and "recorded lecture advantages" under a category like "Asynchronous Learning Benefits", while "missing social interaction" and "peer interaction valued" might form "Social Learning Challenges".

Mixed Methods Research

What is Mixed Methods Research? Mixed methods research combines both quantitative and qualitative approaches in a single study to provide a more comprehensive understanding of a research problem.
Why Use Mixed Methods?

The combination allows you to leverage the strengths of both approaches:

  • Quantitative methods provide breadth, generalizability, and statistical rigor
  • Qualitative methods provide depth, context, and understanding of "why"
  • Together, they can validate findings and provide richer insights

Common Mixed Methods Designs

1. Explanatory Sequential Design

Sequence: QUANTITATIVE → qualitative

How it works:

  1. Collect and analyze quantitative data first
  2. Use quantitative results to inform qualitative data collection
  3. Collect qualitative data to help explain quantitative findings
Example:

First, survey 200 developers about their IDE preferences (quantitative). The survey shows 70% prefer IDE A over IDE B, but you don't know why. Then, conduct interviews with 15 developers to understand the reasons behind their preferences (qualitative).

Best for: When you want to explain or explore quantitative results in more depth

2. Exploratory Sequential Design

Sequence: QUALITATIVE → quantitative

How it works:

  1. Collect and analyze qualitative data first to explore the phenomenon
  2. Use qualitative findings to develop instruments or hypotheses
  3. Test these with a larger sample using quantitative methods
Example:

First, interview 10 students to understand their challenges with learning programming (qualitative). Based on these interviews, identify key themes (e.g., syntax confusion, lack of debugging skills). Then, create a survey based on these themes and distribute it to 500 students to see how widespread these issues are (quantitative).

Best for: When little is known about the phenomenon and you need to develop instruments or test emerging theories

3. Convergent Parallel Design

Sequence: QUANTITATIVE + QUALITATIVE (simultaneously)

How it works:

  1. Collect quantitative and qualitative data at the same time
  2. Analyze both datasets separately
  3. Compare and integrate findings to see if they converge or diverge
Example:

While conducting a usability study, simultaneously collect: (1) Task completion times and error rates (quantitative), and (2) Think-aloud protocol data and observations (qualitative). Compare both to get a complete picture of usability.

Best for: When you want to validate findings through triangulation or get different perspectives on the same issue

4. Embedded Design

Structure: One method is primary, the other is embedded within it

How it works:

  • One method (quantitative or qualitative) plays a dominant role
  • The other method supports or enhances the primary method
  • The secondary method addresses a different question or provides supporting evidence
Example:

Primary: Large-scale experiment testing the effectiveness of a new teaching method (quantitative). Embedded: Brief interviews with a subset of participants to understand their experience and any implementation issues (qualitative support).

Best for: When one approach alone is insufficient, but you want to maintain a clear primary focus

Advantages of Mixed Methods

Strengths of the Mixed Methods Approach:
  • Triangulation: Validate findings by approaching the question from multiple angles
  • Complementarity: Get both breadth and depth of understanding
  • Development: Use one method to inform and improve the other
  • Expansion: Extend the scope and breadth of your research
  • Corroboration: Strengthen conclusions when different methods converge on the same findings

Challenges of Mixed Methods

Common Challenges and Solutions

Challenge 1: Time and Resources

  • Mixed methods requires more time, expertise, and resources
  • Solution: Plan carefully, consider embedded designs, or work in teams

Challenge 2: Integration

  • Combining quantitative and qualitative findings can be complex
  • Solution: Plan integration from the start, be explicit about how methods connect

Challenge 3: Conflicting Results

  • What if quantitative and qualitative findings disagree?
  • Solution: Don't hide divergences - they can reveal important insights about the complexity of the phenomenon

Challenge 4: Expertise Required

  • Researchers need skills in both quantitative and qualitative methods
  • Solution: Collaborate with others, invest in training, or use simpler designs

Example Mixed Methods Study

Research Question: How effective is pair programming for novice programmers, and what factors influence its success?

Mixed Methods Approach (Explanatory Sequential):

Phase 1 - Quantitative:

  • Controlled experiment with 100 students
  • Measure: code quality, task completion time, test scores
  • Compare pair programming vs. solo programming
  • Finding: Pair programming students score 15% higher on average

Phase 2 - Qualitative:

  • Interviews with 20 students from the experiment
  • Questions: What did you like/dislike? What helped you learn? What challenges did you face?
  • Code interview data to identify themes
  • Findings: Success depends on personality compatibility, communication skills, and clear role division

Integration:

The quantitative data shows pair programming is effective, while the qualitative data explains why it works and under what conditions. Together, they provide actionable insights for educators implementing pair programming.

Quick Check: Choose Your Design

Scenario: You want to study student engagement in online courses. You're not sure what factors affect engagement, so you start by interviewing 12 students. Based on themes from interviews, you create a survey and send it to 400 students.

Question: Which mixed methods design is this?

Answer: This is an Exploratory Sequential Design (QUALITATIVE → quantitative).

You started with qualitative data (interviews) to explore and understand the phenomenon, then used those findings to develop a quantitative instrument (survey) to test with a larger sample.
