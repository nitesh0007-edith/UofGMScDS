BCS Code of Conduct

The BCS Code of Conduct sets professional standards for all members of the British Computer Society (BCS), which is the Chartered Institute for IT. It defines the ethical and professional responsibilities of IT professionals.

About BCS

BCS is responsible for setting professional standards in the UK IT industry. All members must abide by this Code of Conduct as a condition of membership, regardless of their grade or role.

The Four Main Duties

The BCS Code of Conduct is organized around four fundamental duties. Each duty contains specific requirements that members must follow.

1. Public Interest
Core Principle

You shall carry out your professional responsibilities with due regard for the public health, privacy, security and wellbeing of others and the environment.

Specific Requirements:

a) Public Health, Privacy, Security and Wellbeing

Have due regard for the public health, privacy, security and wellbeing of others and the environment.

  • Consider impact of your work on public safety
  • Protect privacy in systems you design or maintain
  • Implement appropriate security measures
  • Think about environmental impact of technology
b) Legitimate Rights of Third Parties

Have due regard for the legitimate rights of Third Parties.

  • Respect rights of users, customers, and stakeholders
  • Consider how your work affects others
  • Balance competing interests fairly
c) Non-Discrimination

Conduct your professional activities without discrimination on the grounds of sex, sexual orientation, marital status, nationality, colour, race, ethnic origin, religion, age or disability, or of any other condition or requirement.

  • Ensure equal treatment in professional interactions
  • Design inclusive systems and services
  • Challenge discriminatory practices
d) Equal Access to IT Benefits

Promote equal access to the benefits of IT and seek to promote the inclusion of all sectors in society wherever opportunities arise.

  • Work to bridge the digital divide
  • Make technology accessible to all
  • Consider underserved communities in your work
Real-World Example: Accessibility

When developing a government website, you must ensure it meets accessibility standards (like WCAG) so people with disabilities can use it. This fulfills duties (c) non-discrimination and (d) equal access to IT benefits.

2. Professional Competence and Integrity
Core Principle

You shall have due regard for the legitimate rights of Third Parties and act with integrity in your professional relationships.

Specific Requirements:

a) Work Within Your Competence

Only undertake to do work or provide a service that is within your professional competence.

  • Know your limits and don't overpromise
  • Seek help when needed
  • Don't accept work you cannot properly complete
b) Don't Claim False Competence

NOT claim any level of competence that you do not possess.

  • Be honest about your skills and experience
  • Don't exaggerate qualifications
  • Accurately represent your expertise
c) Continuous Professional Development

Develop your professional knowledge, skills and competence on a continuing basis, maintaining awareness of technological developments, procedures, and standards that are relevant to your field.

  • Keep learning and updating your skills
  • Stay current with industry developments
  • Pursue relevant training and education
  • Follow best practices and standards
d) Know and Comply with Legislation

Ensure that you have the knowledge and understanding of Legislation and that you comply with such Legislation, in carrying out your professional responsibilities.

  • Understand relevant laws (Computer Misuse Act, GDPR, etc.)
  • Ensure compliance in your work
  • Stay updated on legal changes
e) Respect Alternative Viewpoints

Respect and value alternative viewpoints and, seek, accept and offer honest criticisms of work.

  • Be open to different perspectives
  • Provide constructive criticism
  • Accept feedback gracefully
  • Foster collaborative environments
f) Avoid Injuring Others

Avoid injuring others, their property, reputation, or employment by false or malicious or negligent action or inaction.

  • Act professionally at all times
  • Don't make false accusations
  • Take care to avoid negligent work
  • Protect colleagues' reputations
g) Reject Bribery and Unethical Inducements

Reject and will not make any offer of bribery or unethical inducement.

  • Refuse bribes and kickbacks
  • Don't offer bribes to gain advantage
  • Maintain ethical business practices
  • Report corruption when discovered
Important Note

This duty emphasizes both technical competence AND ethical integrity. Being skilled isn't enough - you must also act with honesty and professionalism!

Real-World Example: Competence Limits

If a client asks you to build a medical device control system but you only have experience in web development, you must decline (or seek proper training/assistance first). Healthcare systems require specialized knowledge - taking on work beyond your competence could endanger lives!

3. Duty to Relevant Authority
Core Principle

You shall carry out your professional responsibilities with due care and diligence in accordance with the Relevant Authority's requirements whilst exercising your professional judgement at all times.

What is "Relevant Authority"?

Relevant Authority means the person or organization that has authority over your activities as an IT professional. This includes your employer, client, or any authority that you are contractually bound to serve.

Specific Requirements:

a) Due Care and Diligence

Carry out your professional responsibilities with due care and diligence in accordance with the Relevant Authority's requirements whilst exercising your professional judgement at all times.

  • Perform work thoroughly and carefully
  • Follow employer/client requirements
  • Use professional judgment when needed
  • Meet deadlines and quality standards
b) Avoid Conflicts of Interest

Seek to avoid any situation that may give rise to a conflict of interest between you and your Relevant Authority. Disclose such conflict to the Relevant Authority if it occurs.

  • Identify potential conflicts early
  • Disclose conflicts when they arise
  • Don't use your position for personal gain
  • Avoid competing with your employer
c) Accept Professional Responsibility

Accept professional responsibility for your work and for the work of colleagues who are defined in a given context as working under your supervision.

  • Take ownership of your work
  • Be accountable for results
  • Supervise junior colleagues properly
  • Don't blame others for your mistakes
d) Protect Confidential Information

NOT disclose or authorise to be disclosed, or use for personal gain or to benefit a third party, confidential information except with the permission of your Relevant Authority, or as required by Legislation.

  • Keep employer/client information confidential
  • Don't share trade secrets
  • Only disclose when authorized or legally required
  • Don't use confidential info for personal benefit
e) Honest Reporting

NOT misrepresent or withhold information on the performance of products, systems or services (unless lawfully bound by a duty of confidentiality not to disclose such information), or take advantage of the lack of relevant knowledge or inexperience of others.

  • Report system performance honestly
  • Don't hide bugs or limitations
  • Don't exploit others' lack of technical knowledge
  • Be transparent about capabilities and risks
Real-World Example: Honest Reporting

Situation: You discover a critical security vulnerability in software you're about to release. Your manager wants to ship it anyway to meet the deadline.

Your Duty: You must NOT misrepresent the product's security. You should clearly communicate the risk to the Relevant Authority (your employer) and document your concerns. If they still proceed, you've fulfilled your duty by honest reporting.

Balancing Duties

Note the phrase "whilst exercising your professional judgement at all times." If your employer asks you to do something unethical or illegal, your professional judgment (and other duties in the Code) take precedence!

4. Duty to the Profession
Core Principle

You shall uphold the reputation and good standing of BCS in particular, and the profession in general, and accept your personal duty to uphold the profession's reputation.

Specific Requirements:

a) Uphold the Profession's Reputation

Act with integrity and respect in your professional relationships with all members of BCS and with members of other professions with whom you work in a professional capacity.

  • Behave professionally at all times
  • Treat colleagues with respect
  • Maintain professional relationships
  • Represent the IT profession well
b) Don't Bring the Profession into Disrepute

You shall conduct yourself in a manner that does not bring or is not likely to bring the profession or BCS into disrepute.

  • Avoid behavior that damages IT profession's image
  • Don't engage in unethical practices
  • Consider how your actions reflect on the profession
  • Maintain high standards of conduct
c) Seek to Improve Professional Standards

Seek to improve professional standards through participation in their development, use and enforcement.

  • Contribute to professional development
  • Follow and promote best practices
  • Participate in standards development
  • Help advance the profession
d) Uphold BCS Reputation

Uphold the reputation, good standing and dignity of BCS.

  • Represent BCS positively
  • Follow BCS rules and guidelines
  • Support BCS's mission and values
e) Support Fellow Members

Encourage and support fellow members in their professional development.

  • Mentor junior professionals
  • Share knowledge and experience
  • Help colleagues grow professionally
  • Foster a supportive professional community
The Collective Responsibility

This duty recognizes that IT professionals are part of a broader profession. Your individual actions affect how the public views all IT professionals. By maintaining high standards, you help build trust in the profession as a whole.

Real-World Example: Professional Reputation

Good Practice: When you discover a colleague has made an error, help them fix it professionally and use it as a learning opportunity. This supports their development and maintains the profession's reputation.

Bad Practice: Publicly shaming a colleague for a mistake or using errors to gain personal advantage damages professional relationships and brings the profession into disrepute.

Key Definitions

Legislation

Definition: Any statute, subordinate legislation or regulation in force in the United Kingdom from time to time.

Examples include:

  • Computer Misuse Act 1990
  • Data Protection Act 2018
  • General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)
  • Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988
  • Equality Act 2010
Third Parties

Definition: Any party (individual or entity) other than yourself or your Relevant Authority.

This includes:

  • End users of systems you develop
  • The general public
  • Other organizations affected by your work
  • Stakeholders outside your direct employment relationship
Relevant Authority

Definition: The person or organization that has authority over your activities as an IT professional.

This could be:

  • Your employer (if you're an employee)
  • Your client (if you're a contractor or consultant)
  • Any organization you are contractually bound to serve

Breaches and Disciplinary Procedures

Consequences of Breaching the Code

Breaches of this Code of Conduct may result in:

  • Disciplinary action by BCS
  • Suspension or termination of membership
  • Loss of professional certifications
  • Damage to professional reputation

In serious cases, especially those involving illegal activity, breaches may also result in criminal prosecution under relevant legislation.

Interpretation and Application

How to Apply the Code:
  • In good faith: The Code should be interpreted and applied in good faith
  • Professional judgment: Use your professional judgment when applying principles
  • Context matters: Consider the specific circumstances of each situation
  • Seek guidance: When in doubt, seek advice from BCS or professional colleagues
  • Document decisions: Keep records of how you applied the Code in difficult situations

Relevant Duties:

Key Takeaways

Remember the Four Duties:
  1. Public Interest - Put society's wellbeing first
  2. Professional Competence and Integrity - Be skilled and honest
  3. Duty to Relevant Authority - Serve your employer/client well
  4. Duty to the Profession - Uphold IT professional standards
The Code in Practice

The BCS Code of Conduct isn't just theory - it provides practical guidance for real professional situations:

  • When faced with ethical dilemmas, refer to these four duties
  • If duties conflict, public interest and legal compliance generally take priority
  • Being a professional means more than technical skill - it requires ethical judgment
  • Your professional reputation depends on consistently upholding these standards

Computer Misuse Act 1990

The Computer Misuse Act 1990 was introduced to criminalize unauthorized access to computer systems. Before this act, hacking wasn't explicitly illegal in the UK!

The Prestel Hack (1984-1987)

The Story: Robert Schifreen and Stephen Gold hacked into British Telecom's Prestel system (an early online service). They accessed the Duke of Edinburgh's personal mailbox!

The Problem: They were initially convicted under the Forgery and Counterfeiting Act 1981, but on appeal, the conviction was overturned because hacking wasn't explicitly illegal.

The Result: This case exposed a gap in UK law, leading to the creation of the Computer Misuse Act 1990.

The Three Offences

Section 1: Unauthorized Access
The Law

It is illegal to access a computer system without permission, knowing that you don't have authorization.

What this means in practice:

  • Using someone else's password without their permission
  • Accessing files you're not authorized to view
  • Breaking into computer systems for any reason

Penalty: Up to 2 years in prison and/or a fine

Example: A student discovers their professor's password is "password123" and logs into their account just to look around. Even if they don't change anything, this is illegal under Section 1.
Section 2: Unauthorized Access with Intent
The Law

It is illegal to access a computer system without permission with the intent to commit or facilitate a further crime.

What this means in practice:

  • Hacking into a system to steal data
  • Accessing systems to commit fraud
  • Breaking in to enable blackmail or extortion

Penalty: Up to 5 years in prison and/or a fine

Example: A hacker breaks into a company's database to steal credit card numbers they plan to sell. This is Section 2 because they accessed the system with intent to commit fraud.
Paul Bedworth (1993)

One of the first cases under the Computer Misuse Act. Bedworth was accused of hacking but claimed "computer addiction" as a defense. He was acquitted because the jury wasn't convinced he had criminal intent.

Section 3: Unauthorized Modification
The Law

It is illegal to modify computer material without authorization, knowing you don't have permission to do so.

What this means in practice:

  • Deleting or changing files without permission
  • Installing malware or viruses
  • Defacing websites
  • Denial of Service (DoS) attacks

Penalty: Up to 10 years in prison and/or a fine

Example: A disgruntled employee deletes customer records from the company database before leaving. This is unauthorized modification under Section 3.
Important Note
DoS attacks are specifically covered under Section 3. Even if you don't permanently delete anything, making a system unavailable counts as modification!
Test Your Understanding!

Which section would apply if someone hacks into a university system just to prove they can do it, without stealing or changing anything?

Answer: Section 1 - Unauthorized Access
Even though they didn't steal anything or have further criminal intent, the unauthorized access itself is illegal under Section 1.

Data Protection Act & GDPR

Data protection laws govern how organizations collect, store, process, and share personal information. These laws protect individuals' privacy rights.

Evolution of Data Protection Law

1984

Data Protection Act 1984 - First UK law to regulate personal data. Required registration of data controllers.

1998

Data Protection Act 1998 - Implemented EU Directive 95/46/EC. Introduced 8 data protection principles and expanded rights.

2018

Data Protection Act 2018 - Implemented GDPR in UK law. Much stronger enforcement and penalties.

2018

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) - EU-wide regulation with strict requirements and massive fines for violations.

Key Terminology

Personal Data

Definition: Any information relating to an identified or identifiable living individual.

Examples include:

  • Names, addresses, email addresses
  • Phone numbers
  • IP addresses
  • Cookie identifiers
  • Location data
  • Photos of people
Remember
Data is "personal" if you can identify someone from it, either directly or in combination with other information!
Sensitive Personal Data (Special Category Data)

Definition: Personal data that requires extra protection due to its sensitive nature.

Includes information about:

  • Racial or ethnic origin
  • Political opinions
  • Religious or philosophical beliefs
  • Trade union membership
  • Genetic data
  • Biometric data (fingerprints, facial recognition)
  • Health data
  • Sex life or sexual orientation
Extra Protection Required
Processing sensitive personal data requires explicit consent and additional safeguards. You can't just collect this data casually!
Data Subject

Definition: The living individual whose personal data is being processed.

Example: If a company stores your email address and purchase history, YOU are the data subject.

Data subjects have rights including:

  • Right to access their data
  • Right to correct inaccurate data
  • Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten")
  • Right to data portability
  • Right to object to processing
Data Controller

Definition: The organization or person that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data.

Example: A university decides to collect student email addresses for sending course updates. The university is the data controller because they decide WHY and HOW to collect this data.

Responsibilities:

  • Ensure lawful processing of data
  • Implement appropriate security measures
  • Respond to data subject requests
  • Report data breaches
  • Maintain processing records
Data Processor

Definition: An organization or person that processes personal data on behalf of the data controller.

Example: A university (data controller) uses a cloud email service like Gmail (data processor) to send emails to students. Google processes the data but doesn't decide what emails to send.

Key points:

  • Processors act on instructions from controllers
  • Must have a written contract with the controller
  • Still have legal obligations for data security
  • Cannot use data for their own purposes

The 8 Data Protection Principles (DPA 1998)

These principles form the foundation of data protection law:

1. Fairly and Lawfully Processed

Data must be processed fairly and in accordance with the law.

What this means:

  • Tell people you're collecting their data
  • Explain what you'll use it for
  • Have a legal basis for processing (consent, contract, legitimate interest, etc.)
  • Don't deceive people about how you'll use their data
Bad Example
Collecting email addresses "for newsletters" but then selling them to marketing companies. This is unfair processing!
2. Obtained for Specified Purposes

Data must be collected for specific, explicit, and legitimate purposes.

Example: If you collect phone numbers "for account verification," you can't later use them for marketing calls without getting new consent.

Best practice: Be clear and specific about why you're collecting data. Don't collect it "just in case" you might need it later.

3. Adequate, Relevant, and Not Excessive

Only collect data that you actually need for your stated purpose.

Bad Example
A job application form asking for your religion, medical history, and social media passwords when none of this is relevant to the job.
Good Practice
Data minimization - only collect what you need, nothing more!
4. Accurate and Up to Date

Personal data must be accurate and kept current where necessary.

What this means:

  • Take reasonable steps to ensure accuracy
  • Correct errors when discovered
  • Allow people to update their information
  • Delete or correct inaccurate data
Why it matters: Inaccurate data can lead to wrong decisions. Imagine being denied a loan because of incorrect financial data!
5. Not Kept Longer Than Necessary

Delete or anonymize data when you no longer need it.

Example: If you collect data for a specific research project, delete it once the project is complete (unless participants consented to longer retention).

Best practice: Implement retention policies - define how long you'll keep different types of data and automatically delete it when the time is up.

6. Processed in Line with Rights

Respect individuals' rights regarding their data.

Key rights include:

  • Right to be informed about data processing
  • Right to access their data (Subject Access Request)
  • Right to rectification (correct errors)
  • Right to erasure (deletion)
  • Right to restrict processing
  • Right to data portability
  • Right to object
Important
You must respond to Subject Access Requests within 1 month under GDPR!
7. Kept Secure

Implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to protect data.

Security measures include:

  • Encryption of sensitive data
  • Access controls (only authorized people can access data)
  • Regular security updates and patches
  • Backup systems
  • Staff training on data security
  • Physical security (locked servers, etc.)
Data Breaches
Under GDPR, serious data breaches must be reported to the ICO (Information Commissioner's Office) within 72 hours!
8. Not Transferred Outside EEA Without Protection

Special rules apply when transferring personal data outside the European Economic Area (EEA).

Why this matters: Countries outside the EEA may not have the same data protection standards.

How to transfer data legally:

  • Use countries with "adequacy decisions" (deemed to have adequate protection)
  • Use Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)
  • Use Binding Corporate Rules for large organizations
  • Get explicit consent from data subjects
Example: Using US cloud services (like AWS) requires special agreements because the US doesn't have blanket adequacy status.

GDPR Key Points

Massive Penalties!

GDPR violations can result in fines up to €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher!

New Requirements Under GDPR:
  • Privacy by Design: Build privacy into systems from the start
  • Privacy by Default: Highest privacy settings should be the default
  • Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs): Required for high-risk processing
  • Data Protection Officers (DPOs): Required for public authorities and large-scale data processing
  • Breach Notification: 72-hour reporting requirement
Test Your Understanding!

A social media company collects users' political opinions from their posts and sells this data to political campaigns. Which principles are being violated?

Multiple violations:
1. Fairly and lawfully processed - Users likely didn't consent to this use
2. Obtained for specified purposes - Data collected for social networking, not for selling
3. Special category data - Political opinions require explicit consent and extra protection!

This would result in massive GDPR fines!

Copyright Law

Copyright protects original creative works from being copied without permission. This includes software code!

Tangible vs Intangible Property

Important Distinction:

Tangible property = Physical items (laptop, phone, book)
Intangible property = Non-physical creations (ideas, code, music, art)

The Theft Act 1968 Problem

The Theft Act defines theft as "dishonestly appropriating property belonging to another with the intention of permanently depriving the other of it."

Problem: When you copy software, the original owner still has their copy! So traditional theft laws don't apply to copying intangible property.

This is why we need intellectual property laws like copyright!

Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

What Does Copyright Protect?

Copyright protects:

  • Literary works (including software code!)
  • Dramatic works
  • Musical works
  • Artistic works
  • Sound recordings
  • Films
  • Broadcasts
  • Typographical arrangements
For Computing: Software source code is protected as a "literary work" under copyright law!
Important
Copyright protects the expression of ideas, not the ideas themselves. You can't copyright an algorithm, but you can copyright the specific code implementing it.
How Do You Get Copyright?
Automatic Protection!

Copyright is automatic in the UK. As soon as you create an original work and fix it in a tangible form (write code, save a file, etc.), you own the copyright!

No registration required (unlike patents or trademarks)

Requirements:

  • Work must be original (your own creation)
  • Must show some skill and effort
  • Must be fixed in a medium (written down, saved, recorded)
Example: The moment you write and save a Python script, you automatically own the copyright to that code!
What Rights Does Copyright Give You?

Exclusive rights to:

  • Copy the work
  • Distribute copies to the public
  • Perform, show, or play the work in public
  • Communicate the work to the public (e.g., online)
  • Make adaptations (e.g., translating code to another language)
Infringement

Anyone who does these things without permission is infringing your copyright!

For software specifically:

  • Making copies of the program
  • Distributing the program
  • Making modified versions
  • Translating to another programming language
How Long Does Copyright Last?

For literary works (including software):

Life of the author + 70 years

Example: If you write code in 2025 and live until 2075, your copyright lasts until 2145!

For works created in employment: 70 years from creation

After Copyright Expires
The work enters the "public domain" and anyone can use it freely.
Who Owns the Copyright?

General rule: The creator owns the copyright

Employment Exception!

If you create something as part of your job, your employer owns the copyright, not you!

Example:
  • You write code at work → Your employer owns it
  • You write code at home for a personal project → You own it
  • You write code as a freelancer → You own it (unless contract says otherwise)
Howard and Cantor Fitzgerald

The Story: An employee developed software for his employer but wanted to keep it.

Court Decision: The employer owned the copyright because the software was created as part of his employment duties.

Lesson: If you create it for work, work owns it!

Fair Dealing and Exceptions

Some uses of copyrighted work are allowed without permission:

Fair Dealing for:

  • Research and private study (non-commercial)
  • Criticism and review (with acknowledgment)
  • News reporting
  • Teaching (in educational institutions)

For software specifically:

  • Making backup copies
  • Decompilation for interoperability (under strict conditions)
  • Error correction
Important
Fair dealing is quite narrow in UK law. When in doubt, ask for permission!

Real-World Case Studies

John Carmack, Oculus, and ZeniMax

Background: John Carmack (legendary game developer) left id Software (owned by ZeniMax) to join Oculus VR.

The Dispute: ZeniMax claimed Carmack took intellectual property (code and trade secrets) with him to Oculus.

Result: Jury found Oculus violated a non-disclosure agreement. Oculus had to pay $500 million in damages!

Lessons:

  • You can't take your employer's code when you leave
  • Even famous developers must respect IP laws
  • Non-disclosure agreements are legally binding
Threes! vs 2048 - Game Cloning

Background: Threes! was an original puzzle game. Soon after release, nearly identical clones appeared, including "2048".

The Problem: The clones copied the game mechanics exactly, just changed the numbers and visuals.

Copyright Issue: Game mechanics (the idea) aren't copyrightable, only the specific implementation (code, graphics, text) is protected.

Result: The clones were legal (even though many considered them unethical). The original creators couldn't stop them with copyright law.

Lesson: Copyright protects expression, not ideas. This is a gap in IP protection for games!

Test Your Understanding!

You're a software developer. During evenings and weekends, you build a mobile app using your own laptop at home. Your employment contract doesn't mention intellectual property. Who owns the copyright to your app?

Answer: You own the copyright!

Because you created it:
• Outside work hours
• On your own equipment
• Not as part of your employment duties

However, always check your employment contract for IP clauses. Some contracts claim ownership of anything you create, even at home!

Confidential Information & Trade Secrets

Some valuable information doesn't qualify for copyright protection (like business strategies, customer lists, or algorithms). This is where confidential information law comes in.

What is Confidential Information?

Definition: Information that:
  • Has the quality of confidence (not public knowledge)
  • Was imparted in circumstances of confidence
  • Has been used without authorization
Examples of Confidential Information

In business and technology:

  • Trade secrets: Secret formulas, algorithms, processes
  • Customer lists: Who your clients are and their contact details
  • Business strategies: Future product plans, pricing strategies
  • Technical know-how: Specialized knowledge about how things work
  • Source code: (protected by both copyright AND confidentiality)
  • Database contents: Compiled data that's not public
  • Security measures: How you protect your systems
Famous Example: The Coca-Cola recipe is a trade secret, not a patent. If they patented it, they'd have to disclose the recipe and it would enter public domain after 20 years. By keeping it secret, it's protected indefinitely!
Quality of Confidence

For information to be confidential, it must have the "quality of confidence":

Information HAS quality of confidence if:
  • It's not in the public domain
  • It's not "common knowledge"
  • It's relatively inaccessible
  • It has commercial value
Information LOSES quality of confidence if:
  • It's published or widely known
  • It can be easily reverse-engineered
  • It's obvious to anyone in the field
Example: A company's unique machine learning algorithm has quality of confidence. But "we use Python for web development" does not - that's common knowledge.
Circumstances of Confidence

The information must have been shared in circumstances where the recipient should understand it's confidential.

Circumstances that create confidence:

  • Express agreements: Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs), employment contracts
  • Implied duties: Doctor-patient, lawyer-client relationships
  • Employment relationships: Employees have a duty not to disclose employer's secrets
  • Business negotiations: Sharing information during deal discussions
Employment Scenario

You work for a tech startup and have access to their unreleased product roadmap. Even without signing an NDA, you have an implied duty of confidence not to share this with competitors or the public.

Important
This duty often continues even after you leave the company! You can't immediately use your ex-employer's trade secrets.
Unauthorized Use

For there to be a breach, the information must be used or disclosed without authorization.

Examples of unauthorized use:

  • Sharing confidential information with competitors
  • Using trade secrets to start a competing business
  • Publishing confidential data online
  • Selling customer lists to third parties
Remedies

If you breach confidentiality, courts can:

  • Grant an injunction (order you to stop)
  • Award damages (financial compensation)
  • Order delivery up (return/destroy confidential materials)

Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs)

What is an NDA?

A Non-Disclosure Agreement (also called a confidentiality agreement) is a legal contract that creates a confidential relationship between parties.

Types of NDAs:

  • Unilateral (One-way): One party discloses to another (e.g., employer to employee)
  • Bilateral (Mutual): Both parties share confidential information (e.g., two companies discussing a partnership)

Key clauses in NDAs:

  • Definition of confidential information: What's covered
  • Obligations: What you must/must not do
  • Exclusions: What's not confidential (e.g., public information)
  • Duration: How long the agreement lasts
  • Consequences: What happens if you breach
Before Signing an NDA
  • Read it carefully!
  • Understand what you're agreeing to
  • Check the duration (some last forever!)
  • Ensure it's not unreasonably broad
  • Get legal advice if you're unsure

Confidentiality vs Copyright

Key Differences:
Aspect Copyright Confidentiality
What it protects Expression of ideas Information itself
How you get it Automatic upon creation Requires confidential relationship
Duration Life + 70 years As long as it stays secret
Public disclosure Doesn't affect rights Destroys protection
Independent creation If you create same thing independently, that's okay Doesn't matter - you still can't use it if you received it in confidence
Example - Source Code:

Your company's source code is protected by BOTH copyright AND confidentiality:

  • Copyright prevents copying the code itself
  • Confidentiality prevents employees from sharing it or using the algorithms/techniques elsewhere

Professional Duties

As a Computing Professional

You have legal and ethical duties regarding confidential information:

  • During employment: Don't disclose or misuse employer's confidential information
  • After leaving: Don't use trade secrets from previous employers
  • With clients: Protect client data and business information
  • In collaborations: Respect NDAs and confidentiality agreements
Practical Scenario

Situation: You leave Company A to join Company B (a competitor). You remember details about Company A's upcoming product features.

What you CAN do:

  • Use general skills and knowledge you gained
  • Use publicly available information
  • Apply standard industry practices

What you CANNOT do:

  • Share Company A's trade secrets with Company B
  • Use specific proprietary algorithms or techniques
  • Disclose customer lists or business strategies
  • Bring Company A's code or documents

Gray area: Knowledge in your head (that's not a specific trade secret) can be hard to separate. When in doubt, don't use it!

Test Your Understanding!

You discover your company's database password is "password123" and they store customer credit cards in plain text. Can you publicly disclose this security vulnerability?

Complex Answer:

Legally: You have a duty of confidence to your employer, so public disclosure could breach this.

However: There's a public interest defense. Serious security issues that endanger customers might justify disclosure.

Best practice:
1. Report to management first
2. If ignored, report to ICO (data protection authority)
3. Consider whistleblowing protections
4. Get legal advice before public disclosure

Responsible disclosure protects both customers and yourself legally!

Key Takeaways

Remember:
  • Confidentiality protects information that copyright cannot
  • You can be bound by confidentiality even without signing anything (implied duties)
  • Confidentiality duties often continue after employment ends
  • Public disclosure destroys confidential information protection
  • Always respect NDAs and confidentiality agreements
  • When in doubt about what you can share, ask first!
