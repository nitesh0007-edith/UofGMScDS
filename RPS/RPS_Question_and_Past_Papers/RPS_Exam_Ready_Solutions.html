Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (1 mark each)

⏱ Spend: 1 minute per question
Q1. Scientific Approach 1 Mark 1 min
A scientific approach...
✓ a. compares observations with predictions
b. only considers cultural facts
c. requires gathering lots of information
Answer: (a)

Simple explanation: Science works by making predictions and checking if they're true.

You predict something will happen → You observe/test it → You compare results

Q2. Hypothesis Quality 1 Mark 1 min
"Mary's algorithm is better than John's" - What's wrong with this hypothesis?
a. Running experiment multiple times ensures generalisability
✓ b. 'Better' needs to be defined more carefully
c. Testing means running both algorithms with different data
Answer: (b)

Why: "Better" is too vague. Better how?

  • Faster?
  • More accurate?
  • Uses less memory?

Fix: "Mary's algorithm runs faster than John's"

Q3. Research Ethics 1 Mark 1 min
Ethics in human experiments means...
a. letting participants take as long as they want
✓ b. reducing risk to participants as much as possible
c. ensuring your paper gets published
Answer: (b)

Ethics = Protecting people

Main points:

  • Don't harm participants
  • Get their permission (informed consent)
  • They can quit anytime
  • Keep their data private
Q4. Nature of Hypotheses 1 Mark 1 min
A hypothesis...
✓ a. can be rejected later even if supported now
b. is always rephrased as a research question
c. should be impossible to prove wrong
Answer: (a)

Science is never 100% certain.

New evidence can change what we believe. That's OK - it's how science works!

Example: People thought the Earth was flat (old hypothesis) → New evidence proved it's round (hypothesis rejected)

MCQ Strategy
  • Read question carefully
  • Eliminate obviously wrong answers first
  • Watch for words like "always", "never" (usually wrong)
  • If unsure, make an educated guess (don't leave blank if there's no negative marking)
  • In this exam: +1 correct, -0.5 wrong, 0 blank → guess if you can eliminate one option

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (2-4 marks)

⏱ Spend: 3-5 minutes per question
GDPR Principles Example 6 Marks (2 each) 6-8 min
Describe 3 GDPR principles and give a university example for each.
Simple Exam Answer (Write This!)

1. Data Minimization (2 marks)

Principle: Only collect data you actually need.

University Example: When you enroll, the university should only ask for your name, contact details, and qualifications. They shouldn't ask about your religion or political views unless necessary for specific services.

2. Purpose Limitation (2 marks)

Principle: Use data only for the purpose you collected it.

University Example: Your email address collected for sending course materials can't be sold to marketing companies or used for unrelated purposes.

3. Transparency (2 marks)

Principle: Tell people clearly how you'll use their data.

University Example: The university must explain in the privacy policy what student data they collect, why they collect it, and how long they keep it.

Quick Writing Template

For each principle (2 marks each):

  1. State the principle name (0.5 marks)
  2. Explain what it means in simple words (0.5 marks)
  3. Give a specific university example (1 mark)
Good Research Paper Characteristics 6 Marks (2 each) 6-8 min
Discuss 3 characteristics of a good research paper.
Simple Exam Answer

1. Clear Research Question (2 marks)

A good paper has a specific question it's trying to answer. The question should be:

  • Focused and specific (not too broad)
  • Clearly stated in the introduction
  • Something you can actually test/investigate

Example: "Does caffeine improve programming performance?" (good) vs "What affects programming?" (too vague)

2. Good Methodology (2 marks)

The paper explains HOW the research was done in enough detail that someone else could repeat it.

  • Clear description of methods used
  • Explains why these methods were chosen
  • Other researchers can replicate the study

3. Evidence-Based Conclusions (2 marks)

Conclusions are based on the actual results, not just opinions.

  • Results properly analyzed (with stats if needed)
  • Admits limitations (what the study couldn't show)
  • Conclusions match the evidence

Computer Misuse Act - Viruses 3 Marks 3-4 min
How does the Computer Misuse Act deal with computer viruses?
Simple 3-Point Answer

Point 1 (1 mark): Creating viruses is NOT illegal by itself. Security researchers and antivirus companies can write viruses for testing.

Point 2 (1 mark): Using viruses WITH PERMISSION is OK. If the computer owner gives permission, you can test viruses on their system.

Point 3 (1 mark): Using viruses WITHOUT PERMISSION is illegal. If you use a virus to access, damage, or disable someone's computer without authorization, that breaks the law.

Remember This

Key idea: It's not the virus itself that's illegal - it's using it to do unauthorized things.

Section 3: Medium Questions (6-12 marks)

⏱ Spend: 8-15 minutes per question
Experiment Design Critique 9 Marks 10-12 min

Scenario: A researcher wants to know if Physics students improve more at programming than non-Physics students. The plan: Test everyone at the END of the year and compare average scores.

Explain 3 problems with this experiment and suggest fixes.

3 Problems + Solutions (3 marks each)

Problem 1: No Starting Point Measured

Issue: We don't know how good they were at the START. Physics students might already be better programmers before the course even begins. We can't measure "improvement" without a before and after.

Fix: Test students at the BEGINNING and END of the year. Calculate improvement = (end score - start score). Then compare improvement between groups.

Problem 2: Other Factors Not Controlled

Issue: Many things affect programming besides Physics. Maybe Physics students are just better at math, or more motivated, or have programmed before.

Fix: Measure these factors (math ability, prior programming, study time). Use statistics to control for them. Or match students in pairs with similar backgrounds.

Problem 3: Multiple Choice Quiz Is Limited

Issue: MCQ tests knowledge, not actual programming skill. Students can guess. Doesn't test if they can write real code.

Fix: Use multiple assessment methods: actual coding tasks, code quality rubrics, debugging exercises, plus the MCQ. This gives a fuller picture of programming ability.

Writing Structure (3 marks per problem)
  • Clearly state the problem (1 mark)
  • Explain WHY it's a problem (1 mark)
  • Suggest a specific fix (1 mark)
Within-Subject vs Between-Subject Design 6 Marks 8-10 min
You're testing if pair programming helps novice Java programmers. Discuss advantages of within-subject and between-subject designs. Which would you choose?
Quick Comparison Answer
Within-Subject (Same people do both)

Advantages:

  • Each person is their own control - eliminates differences in programming ability
  • Need fewer participants
  • Fair comparison (not affected by one group being naturally better)

Disadvantages:

  • Learning effect: People improve just from practice
  • Fatigue: Tired on second task
Between-Subject (Different people in each group)

Advantages:

  • No practice effects - each person only does one condition
  • Simpler to run
  • More realistic (students either pair program or don't throughout a course)

Disadvantages:

  • Groups might differ in ability naturally
  • Need more participants

My Choice: Between-Subject

Why: In a programming course, learning transfers between conditions. If someone learns pair programming first, they'll use those skills when working alone later. This would contaminate the results. Better to have separate groups.

Fix the disadvantage: Use random assignment and measure starting programming ability to control for differences.

Quick Template

1. List advantages of within-subject (2 marks)

2. List advantages of between-subject (2 marks)

3. State your choice + justify (2 marks)

Dependent Variables 4 Marks 4-5 min
Identify one quantitative and one qualitative dependent variable for testing pair programming. Justify each.
Quick Answer Template

Quantitative (2 marks)

Variable: Number of lab exercises completed correctly

Why this is good:

  • Easy to measure (just count correct solutions)
  • Objective (not based on opinion)
  • Directly answers if pair programming "helps"
  • Can use statistics to compare groups

Qualitative (2 marks)

Variable: Student descriptions of their learning experience

Why this is good:

  • Shows HOW and WHY pair programming helps (or doesn't)
  • Captures feelings and experiences numbers can't show
  • Reveals unexpected benefits or problems
  • Helps explain the quantitative results
Don't Overthink It!

Pick simple, obvious measures. Explain briefly why they're useful. That's all you need in 5 minutes!

Ethical Concerns 4 Marks 4-5 min
A researcher will test a chatbot on students in their own mandatory tutorial sessions. Identify 2 ethical concerns.
2 Concerns (2 marks each)

Concern 1: Pressure to Participate

Problem: Students may feel forced to participate because:

  • The researcher is their teacher (power imbalance)
  • The session is mandatory
  • They might fear it affects their grade

Why bad: Consent must be voluntary. This isn't really voluntary.

Concern 2: Can't Separate Research from Learning

Problem: The experiment is mixed into required coursework. Students can't opt out without missing learning.

Why bad: Students shouldn't have to choose between "participate in research" or "miss course content."

Quick Format

For each concern (2 marks):

  • Name the problem (0.5 marks)
  • Explain it (1 mark)
  • Say why it's ethically wrong (0.5 marks)
Survey Questions 2 Marks 2-3 min
Create one closed and one open-ended question to measure VR frustration.
Quick Answer
CLOSED QUESTION (1 mark): "How frustrated did you feel using this VR app?" 1 - Not at all frustrated 2 - Slightly frustrated 3 - Moderately frustrated 4 - Very frustrated 5 - Extremely frustrated OPEN-ENDED QUESTION (1 mark): "Please describe any frustrating moments you experienced while using the VR app. What caused the frustration?"
That's It!

Don't overthink. Just write one of each type. This should take 2-3 minutes max.

Section 4: Long Questions (15-25 marks)

⏱ Spend: 20-30 minutes per question
Full Experiment Design 25 Marks 25-30 min

Scenario: You made an AR warehouse app to help workers. You think it increases productivity. Design an experiment to test this.

Cover all aspects except literature review and proposal writing.

Structured Answer Template
Time Breakdown for 25-30 minutes
  • Hypothesis & Design: 5 min
  • Participants & Procedure: 8 min
  • Measurements & Analysis: 7 min
  • Ethics: 3 min
  • Quick review: 2-5 min
1. HYPOTHESIS (2 marks)
"Workers using the AR app will complete warehouse tasks faster and with fewer errors than workers using traditional paper-based methods."
2. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN (5 marks)

Type: Between-subjects (different workers in each group)

Independent Variable: Assistance method (AR app vs. paper instructions)

Dependent Variables:

  • Quantitative: Time to complete tasks, number of errors, parcels processed per hour
  • Qualitative: Worker feedback on ease of use, frustrations

Control Variables:

  • Same warehouse area
  • Same tasks for everyone
  • Same training time
3. PARTICIPANTS (2 marks)
  • Who: 40 warehouse workers (20 in each group)
  • Requirements: At least 1 month experience, normal vision
  • Assignment: Randomly assign to AR or paper group
4. PROCEDURE (6 marks)
STEP 1: Introduction (5 min) - Explain the study - Get written consent - Collect basic info (age, experience) STEP 2: Training (15 min) - AR group: Learn to use AR headset and app - Paper group: Review paper instruction system - Both practice until comfortable STEP 3: Main Tasks (30 min) - Complete 3 warehouse scenarios: * Simple: Pick 10 parcels from nearby locations * Medium: Pick 15 parcels, some heavy/fragile * Complex: 12 parcels with time pressure - Record time, errors, route taken STEP 4: Questionnaire (10 min) - Rate ease of use (1-7 scale) - Rate mental effort (NASA-TLX) - Open question: "What was helpful/frustrating?" STEP 5: Debrief (5 min) - Explain full purpose - Answer questions - Thank them
5. DATA ANALYSIS (3 marks)

Quantitative:

  • Calculate average time for each group
  • Use t-test to compare groups (p < 0.05 = significant)
  • Count errors in each group

Qualitative:

  • Read all comments
  • Group similar themes (e.g., "navigation easy," "screen too bright")
  • Count how many mentioned each theme
6. ETHICS (3 marks)
  • Get ethics approval from university committee before starting
  • Informed consent: Written form explaining purpose, time needed, right to quit
  • Safety: AR can cause dizziness or trips - warn participants, clear obstacles, supervisor present
  • Privacy: Anonymize data, can't identify individual workers
  • No work impact: Performance doesn't affect their job
7. LIMITATIONS (2 marks)
  • Short study (1 hour) - doesn't show long-term effects
  • One warehouse - might not work same elsewhere
  • Novelty effect - AR might seem better just because it's new
8. CONCLUSION (2 marks)

If AR group is significantly faster with fewer errors, we can conclude the AR app improves productivity. If there's no difference or paper is better, the app needs redesign. Use both numbers and worker feedback to make recommendations.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid:
  • Don't write too much on one section - spread marks across all parts
  • Don't forget ethics - always worth 2-3 marks
  • Don't skip the hypothesis at the start
  • Don't forget to say HOW you'll analyze data

Quick Reference: Key Concepts

Experimental Design Basics
Concept Simple Explanation Example
Hypothesis A testable prediction "Caffeine improves coding speed"
Independent Variable What you change Caffeine (yes/no)
Dependent Variable What you measure Lines of code per hour
Control Variable What you keep the same Same programming task, same time
Within-Subject Same people, both conditions Everyone tries coffee AND no coffee
Between-Subject Different people per condition Group A gets coffee, Group B doesn't
Statistics Quick Guide
  • Mean: Average (add all numbers, divide by count)
  • T-test: Compares 2 groups to see if difference is real or just luck
  • p < 0.05: Less than 5% chance the result is random (we call this "significant")
  • Null hypothesis: "There's no difference between groups"
  • Alternative hypothesis: "There IS a difference"
Ethics Checklist

Always include these in ANY experiment answer:

  • ☑ Get ethics committee approval
  • ☑ Informed consent (explain study, get written agreement)
  • ☑ Right to withdraw (can quit anytime, no penalty)
  • ☑ Confidentiality (keep data private, anonymous)
  • ☑ Minimize harm (physical and psychological)
  • ☑ Debrief (explain purpose after, answer questions)
GDPR Principles (Memorize These 3)
  1. Data Minimization: Only collect what you need
  2. Purpose Limitation: Use data only for stated purpose
  3. Transparency: Tell people what you do with their data

Other principles: Accuracy, Storage Limitation, Integrity/Confidentiality, Accountability

Final Exam Strategy

Before You Start
  • Read through ENTIRE exam first (2-3 minutes)
  • Mark easy vs hard questions
  • Plan your time per question
  • Do easy questions first to build confidence
While Writing
  • Watch the marks: 1 mark = 1 point, don't write a paragraph
  • Use bullet points: Faster and clearer
  • Answer the question: Don't write everything you know about a topic
  • If stuck: Skip it, come back later
  • Show working: Even if you're not sure, write something
Time Check Points
  • 30 minutes in: Should have ~20 marks done
  • 60 minutes in: Should have ~40 marks done
  • 80 minutes in: Finishing up, reviewing
  • Last 10 minutes: Quick review, fill any gaps
⚠️ Common Time Wasters - AVOID THESE!
  • ❌ Writing too much for low-mark questions
  • ❌ Perfectionism - good enough is good enough!
  • ❌ Getting stuck on one hard question
  • ❌ Not reading the question properly
  • ❌ Copying from notes without thinking (open book ≠ copy everything)
What to Do in Last 10 Minutes

  3. Make sure MCQs are marked clearly
  4. Check your name is on every page
  5. Don't stress - you've done your best!
