Question 1 - Solutions 13 Marks Total
1(a) Computer Misuse Act [3 marks]
Discuss how the creation and use of computer viruses is prohibited under the Computer Misuse Act.
Model Answer

Key Point 1 (1 mark): The Computer Misuse Act does NOT prohibit the act of creating viruses in itself. Simply writing virus code is not illegal under the Act.

Key Point 2 (1 mark): Viruses can be used on equipment or data under the Computer Misuse Act if the owner's permission is first obtained. For example, security researchers can legally test viruses on their own systems or with explicit authorization.

Key Point 3 (1 mark): The creation or use of viruses is only prohibited if the virus is created or used to access or modify data or impair a computer without authorization to do so. The key element is unauthorized access or damage.

💡 Example to Illustrate:

Legal: A cybersecurity company creating a test virus to evaluate their antivirus software on their own systems.

Illegal: Creating and deploying a virus to access or damage another person's or organization's computer systems without their permission.

Marking Breakdown:

✓ Stating that creating viruses is NOT inherently prohibited [1]
✓ Stating viruses can be used with owner's permission [1]
✓ Stating prohibition only applies to unauthorized access/modification/impairment [1]
1(b) Database Copyright [4 marks]
A company has applied to copyright a database they have created. Their database contains a simple ordering of 10,000 novels alphabetically by title. Discuss whether the company is likely to succeed in their attempt to copyright the database described above.
Model Answer

Conclusion (1 mark): The company is unlikely to gain copyright protection for the database as described.

Justification:

Reason 1 - Lack of Substantial Investment (1 mark): For database copyright, there must be a substantial investment in obtaining, verifying, or presenting the contents of the database. Simply ordering 10,000 novels alphabetically does not represent substantial investment - alphabetical sorting can be done automatically with minimal effort.

Reason 2 - Lack of Originality (1 mark): The contents of the database must be original and the selection/arrangement must constitute the author's own intellectual creation. An alphabetical ordering is a standard, mechanical process that lacks the creativity or originality required for copyright protection.

Reason 3 - Insufficient Complexity (1 mark): Ordering alphabetically does not have sufficient complexity or depth to qualify for copyright protection. In contrast, a more sophisticated ordering system would be more likely to qualify - for example:

  • A unique clustering by novel themes over time across different genres
  • A custom categorization system based on literary analysis
  • A complex recommendation system based on reader preferences and connections
📚 Comparison:

Not Copyrightable: Phone directory sorted alphabetically by surname

Potentially Copyrightable: A curated collection of novels organized by a unique thematic framework analyzing post-modern narrative techniques across cultures and time periods

Marking Breakdown:

✓ Stating the company is unlikely to succeed [1]
✓ Explaining lack of substantial investment [1]
✓ Explaining lack of originality in selection/arrangement [1]
✓ Providing example of why ordering lacks sufficient complexity [1]
1(c) Research Ethics [4 marks]
A researcher is planning to conduct an experiment to investigate if a chatbot they have developed helps novice Java programmers solve lab exercises during a programming course. The researcher plans to conduct the experiment on a Java Programming course they will teach next year during the mandatory tutorial sessions within the course.

Identify two potential ethical concerns with this research.
Model Answer

Ethical Concern 1: Coercion and Power Dynamics (2 marks)

  • Issue (1 mark): Students might feel pressured into taking part in the research because the researcher is their teacher.
  • Justification (1 mark): The power imbalance between instructor and student could make students feel they cannot refuse participation without negative consequences to their grades or standing in the course. This undermines the principle of voluntary informed consent, as students may agree to participate not out of genuine willingness but fear of academic repercussions.

Ethical Concern 2: Inability to Provide True Informed Consent (2 marks)

  • Issue (1 mark): Students might be unable to give informed consent (or any genuine consent) since the experiment is interwoven with an essential teaching activity.
  • Justification (1 mark): The tutorial sessions are described as "mandatory," meaning students cannot opt out of the sessions. Since the experiment is conducted during these mandatory sessions, students effectively have no choice but to participate in the research. This violates the fundamental ethical principle that research participation must be voluntary.
🔍 Additional Ethical Concerns (also acceptable):
  • Conflict of Interest: The researcher is evaluating their own chatbot while teaching the course, potentially introducing bias
  • Educational Inequality: Students in different conditions may receive different quality of educational support
  • Right to Withdraw: Students cannot withdraw from mandatory sessions without affecting their education
  • Data Privacy: Student performance data might be collected without proper consent procedures

Marking Breakdown:

✓ Identifying first ethical concern [1]
✓ Well-justified explanation of first concern [1]
✓ Identifying second ethical concern [1]
✓ Well-justified explanation of second concern [1]
1(d) Survey Design [2 marks]
You have been tasked with creating a survey to investigate consumers' frustration using a new virtual reality application, "MBP VR". Describe one closed and one open-ended question that you could use in the survey to capture this.
Model Answer

Closed Question (1 mark total):

Question: "On a scale of 1-5, to what extent were you frustrated while using MBP VR?"

  • 1 = Not at all frustrated
  • 2 = Slightly frustrated
  • 3 = Moderately frustrated
  • 4 = Very frustrated
  • 5 = Extremely frustrated

Marks: 0.5 for relevant closed question, 0.5 for good formatting/scale

Open-Ended Question (1 mark total):

Question: "Can you describe, if at all, at which moments you were frustrated while using MBP VR and why?"

OR

Question: "Please explain any frustrating experiences you had while using MBP VR. What caused these frustrations?"

Marks: 0.5 for relevant open question, 0.5 for good formatting/openness

💡 Why These Question Types?

Closed Question Benefits: Provides quantitative data that can be easily analyzed statistically; allows comparison across users; simple for respondents to answer quickly.

Open-Ended Question Benefits: Captures rich qualitative insights; reveals specific issues you might not have anticipated; allows users to explain the context and reasons for frustration.

Together: They provide both breadth (how many people are frustrated, to what degree) and depth (why they're frustrated, what specifically causes it).

Marking Breakdown:

✓ Relevant closed question [0.5]
✓ Well-formatted closed question with clear scale/options [0.5]
✓ Relevant open-ended question [0.5]
✓ Well-formatted open-ended question that allows detailed responses [0.5]

Model Answer

Hypothesis (2 marks):

"The LocoPro locomotion system will provide a more immersive experience than LocoOriginal."

[1 mark for correctly stated hypothesis, +1 mark for clearly indicating dependent variable]

Alternative Acceptable Hypotheses:

  • "LocoPro will be easier to control than LocoOriginal" (DV: ease of control)
  • "Players using LocoPro will report higher immersion scores than those using LocoOriginal" (DV: immersion score)
  • "LocoPro will result in better player experience compared to LocoOriginal" (DV: player experience)

💡 What Makes a Good Hypothesis?

  • Specific: Clearly states what you're comparing (LocoPro vs LocoOriginal)
  • Testable: You can design an experiment to test it
  • Clear DV: States or implies what will be measured (immersion, ease of control)
  • Directional: Predicts which condition will be better

2(b) Experimental Design [19 marks]
Propose an experiment to investigate this hypothesis. You should cover all aspects of a research project, except performing a literature survey and writing a research proposal.
Comprehensive Model Answer

1. ASSUMPTIONS & CONSTRAINTS [Max 3 marks]

  • Prior Experience: Participants are not experienced with LocoOriginal prior to conducting the experiment. This ensures fair comparison without bias from familiarity.
  • Participant Diversity: Participants will be recruited with a variety of prior experience with VR gaming, and a mixture of genders, ages, and backgrounds to ensure generalizability of results.
  • Controlled Environment: The experiment takes place in a controlled lab environment to ensure:
    • Sufficient empty space for safe VR movement
    • Participants aren't at risk of tripping over obstacles
    • Sufficient space for the locomotion systems to function correctly
    • Consistent lighting and environmental conditions

[Up to 3 marks for reasonable, well-justified assumptions]

2. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN [Max 12 marks]

Independent Variable & Conditions [1 mark]

Independent Variable: Locomotion method (2 levels)

  • Condition 1: LocoPro system
  • Condition 2: LocoOriginal system

Control Variables [2 marks]

Purpose: Reduce confounding factors and ensure fair comparison

  • Hardware: Same VR headset and controllers for all participants
  • Environment: Same physical testing space
  • Tasks: Same navigation tasks for both conditions
  • Duration: Same time allocation for each condition
  • Instructions: Standardized briefing and tutorial for all participants
  • Virtual Environment: Same VR game/environment used for testing

Dependent Variables [2 marks]

Quantitative Measures:

  • Task Completion Time: Time taken to navigate through a set course (measured in seconds)
  • Navigation Accuracy: Number of errors or collisions during navigation
  • Game Performance Score: Points achieved during gameplay (if applicable)

Subjective Measures:

  • Perceived Immersion: Standardized immersion questionnaire (e.g., IEQ - Immersive Experience Questionnaire) - scored 1-7
  • Ease of Control: Subjective rating on 5-point Likert scale (1=Very Difficult, 5=Very Easy)
  • Overall Satisfaction: Post-task questionnaire rating overall experience (1-10 scale)
  • NASA TLX Workload Assessment: Standardized measure of perceived workload

Type of Experimental Design [1 mark]

Chosen Design: Within-subjects design (repeated measures)

Justification:

  • Controls for individual differences: Each participant acts as their own control, reducing variance from different gaming abilities, motion sickness susceptibility, etc.
  • Statistical power: Greater statistical power with fewer participants needed
  • Counterbalancing: Will use counterbalancing to control for order effects (half start with LocoPro, half with LocoOriginal)

Alternative: Between-subjects would also be acceptable if justified (e.g., to avoid learning effects)

Sample Size & Participants

Sample Size: 30 participants (sufficient for repeated-measures t-test with medium effect size)

Recruitment: University students and staff recruited via email, posters, and online forums

Inclusion Criteria:

  • Age 18-65
  • No history of severe motion sickness
  • Normal or corrected-to-normal vision
  • Mix of VR experience levels (novice to experienced)

Assignment Method: Randomized counterbalancing (15 participants do LocoPro first, 15 do LocoOriginal first)

Experimental Tasks [2 marks]

Participants will complete the following tasks for EACH locomotion system:

  1. Training Task (5 minutes): Tutorial level to familiarize participants with the locomotion controls
  2. Navigation Task (10 minutes): Navigate through a virtual maze to find specific waypoints - measures completion time and accuracy
  3. Gameplay Task (15 minutes): Play a section of the VR game using the locomotion system - measures performance score and engagement

Total time per condition: ~30 minutes

Break between conditions: 5-minute break to reduce fatigue and motion sickness

Data Collection Method [1 mark]

  • Automatic Tracking: VR system automatically logs completion times, collision counts, performance scores
  • Questionnaires: Digital questionnaires completed immediately after each condition
  • Observer Notes: Researcher records observable behaviors (e.g., signs of frustration, motion sickness)
  • Video Recording: (With consent) Record participant movements for later analysis

Form of Data Collected [2 marks]

Quantitative Data:

  • Continuous: Task completion times (seconds), navigation accuracy (percentage)
  • Discrete: Number of collisions, performance scores
  • Ordinal: Likert scale responses (1-5, 1-7)
  • Ratio: Times, distances, counts (all have true zero)

Qualitative Data:

  • Open-ended questionnaire responses about user experience
  • Observer notes on participant behavior

Data Format: Stored in CSV files for quantitative data; text files for qualitative data; all anonymized with participant IDs

Pilot Study [1 mark]

Pilot Testing (n=5 participants):

  • Test data collection procedures and identify technical issues
  • Verify questionnaires are understandable
  • Ensure tasks are appropriate difficulty and duration
  • Check that data processing scripts work correctly
  • Identify any potential motion sickness issues

3. ANALYSIS METHODOLOGY [Max 3 marks]

Statistical Analysis Plan:

Primary Analysis - Quantitative Data:

  • Statistical Test: Repeated-measures t-tests (paired samples t-test)
  • Null Hypothesis (H₀): There is no significant difference in immersion/control between LocoPro and LocoOriginal
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H₁): LocoPro provides significantly higher immersion/easier control than LocoOriginal
  • Significance Level: α = 0.05 (p < 0.05 considered statistically significant)
  • Effect Size: Calculate Cohen's d to determine practical significance

For Each Dependent Variable:

  • Calculate descriptive statistics (mean, standard deviation, median) for both conditions
  • Perform paired t-test comparing LocoPro vs LocoOriginal
  • Check assumptions (normality using Shapiro-Wilk test)
  • If assumptions violated, use non-parametric alternative (Wilcoxon signed-rank test)

Qualitative Analysis:

  • Method: Thematic analysis using open and axial coding
  • Identify themes in open-ended questionnaire responses
  • Code user comments about frustrations, preferences, experiences
  • Use qualitative findings to explain quantitative results

[Up to 3 marks for specific, appropriate analysis methods with correct terminology]

4. ETHICS [Max 3 marks]

Ethical Approval [1 mark]:
  • Submit ethics application to University Ethics Committee before recruiting participants
  • Obtain formal approval before commencing study
Informed Consent [1 mark]:
  • Provide participants with detailed information sheet explaining:
    • Purpose of the study
    • What they will be asked to do
    • Time commitment (~75 minutes total)
    • Potential risks (motion sickness, eye strain)
    • How data will be used and stored
  • Obtain written informed consent before participation
  • Participants can ask questions before consenting
Risk Management [1 mark]:
  • Motion Sickness: Warn participants about potential VR-induced motion sickness; allow them to stop immediately if feeling unwell; provide seating/breaks
  • Physical Safety: Clear play area of obstacles; researcher monitors participants; emergency stop procedures in place
  • Eye Strain: Limit session duration; provide breaks; screen for vision problems
Additional Ethical Safeguards:
  • Right to Withdraw: Participants can withdraw at any time without giving a reason and without penalty
  • Data Protection: All data anonymized; participant IDs used instead of names; secure storage compliant with GDPR
  • Confidentiality: Personal information kept separate from research data; only aggregated results published
  • Debriefing: Participants debriefed after completion; results shared upon request

5. CONCLUSIONS [Max 2 marks]

Drawing Conclusions from Results [1 mark]:

  • If statistical tests show significant differences (p < 0.05) with LocoPro scoring higher on immersion/ease of control, we can conclude that LocoPro provides a better player experience
  • If no significant difference found, the hypothesis is not supported
  • Qualitative data will provide insights into WHY one system is preferred

Limitations & Future Work:

  • Results may only generalize to similar VR games/environments
  • Lab setting may not reflect real-world home VR use
  • Short-term study - long-term effects unknown
  • Future work: Test in different game genres, longer-term studies, different user populations
