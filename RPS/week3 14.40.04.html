🔬 The Scientific Method

What Is It?

The scientific method is like a recipe for discovering truth. Just like following steps to bake a cake, scientists follow steps to discover how the world works.

The 8 Steps (Simplified)

1

Ask a Question

Identify what you want to understand about the world.

Example: "Why do plants grow toward sunlight?"
2

Do Your Research

Look at what others have already discovered and observe what's happening.

3

Make a Guess (Hypothesis)

Create a testable explanation for what you observe.

Example: "I think plants grow toward sunlight because they need light for energy"
4

Test Your Guess (Experiment)

Set up an experiment to see if you're right. Make sure others can repeat your test!

5

Analyze Your Results

Look carefully at what the data shows.

6

Draw Conclusions

Decide if your guess was right or wrong based on the evidence.

7

Share Your Findings

Tell other scientists what you discovered so they can learn from your work.

8

Let Others Test It

Other scientists will try your experiment to verify your results.

⚡ Key Insights from Famous Scientists

From Richard Feynman:

  • "You can never prove you're completely right" - You can only know for sure when you're wrong
  • When your experiment works, it doesn't mean your theory is 100% true forever
  • It just means it hasn't been proven wrong YET
  • Tomorrow's experiment might prove you wrong - and that's okay!

From Neil deGrasse Tyson:

  • Defend what you think is true with evidence, theory, and reason
  • Don't rely on opinion and belief alone
  • Be willing to change your mind when proven wrong
  • Scientific truth emerges through repeated verification
"We are scientists, we have theories and experiments, we don't believe anything." — Neil deGrasse Tyson

💭 Arguments (Philosophically)

What is an Argument?

Not the kind where people yell at each other! In science and philosophy, an argument is:

Premises (facts/statements) → Conclusion (what those facts mean)

Simple Example

Premise 1: All dogs are mammals
Premise 2: Rex is a dog
Conclusion: Therefore, Rex is a mammal ✓

Scientific Inference

Scientific inference is the process of reaching conclusions based on available evidence and theories.

  • We have one (or more) premise
  • From which we draw a conclusion
  • The quality of the conclusion depends on the validity of premises and reasoning

Argument Quality Terminology

Term What It Means Applies To
Valid If premises are true, does the conclusion logically follow? Deductive arguments
Sound Valid AND all premises are actually true Deductive arguments
Strong Premises make the conclusion highly probable Inductive arguments
Cogent Strong AND all premises are actually true Inductive arguments

🧠 Two Types of Reasoning

The Main Difference

Inductive reasoning aims at developing a theory

Deductive reasoning aims at testing an existing theory

🔷

Deductive Reasoning

Pattern: General → Specific

Top-Down Approach

  1. Start with a BIG general rule
  2. Apply it to a specific case
  3. Get a certain conclusion
Example:
General Rule: All insects have 6 legs
Specific Case: Spiders have 8 legs
Conclusion: Spiders are NOT insects ✓
Key Point: If premises are true, conclusion MUST be true (100% certain)
🔶

Inductive Reasoning

Pattern: Specific → General

Bottom-Up Approach

  1. Start with specific observations
  2. Notice a pattern
  3. Make a general conclusion
Example:
Observation: Every orange cat I've met purrs loudly
Pattern: I've seen this many times
Conclusion: All orange cats probably purr loudly
Key Point: Even if observations are true, conclusion is PROBABLY true (not 100%)

🔥 Important!

Most research uses INDUCTIVE reasoning!

We observe a sample to draw conclusions about a population.

🎮 Interactive Demo: Test Your Understanding

Is this deductive or inductive reasoning?

"Every swan I've seen is white. Therefore, all swans are probably white."

Answer: INDUCTIVE

This is inductive reasoning because we're going from specific observations (swans I've seen) to a general conclusion (all swans). Notice the word "probably" - this indicates it's not 100% certain!

⚠️ Logical Fallacies

What's a Fallacy?

A fallacy is an error in reasoning that makes an argument weak or invalid.

Fallacies can be committed intentionally (to trick people) or accidentally (honest mistakes).

Type 1: Formal Fallacies (Structural Errors)

The Raccoon Fallacy

Premise 1: All black bears are omnivores
Premise 2: All raccoons are omnivores
Conclusion: All raccoons are black bears ❌

Why it's wrong: Just because two things share a quality doesn't make them the same thing!

Type 2: Informal Fallacies (Content Errors)

🚫

False Dilemma

What it is: Pretending there are only 2 options when there are actually more

Example:
"Stacey spoke out against capitalism; therefore, she must be a communist"

Why wrong: Stacey could be many other things - democratic socialist, anarchist, or support a mixed economy!
🎭

Straw Man Argument

What it is: Distorting someone's argument to make it easier to attack

Example:
Parent: "You can't go to the party tonight, you have an exam tomorrow"
Child: "Why do you HATE me?"

Why wrong: The parent said nothing about hate! The child twisted "can't go to party" into "parent hates me"

🎮 Spot the Fallacy!

"Either you support my policy, or you hate our country!"

Answer: False Dilemma

This presents only two options when there are many others: You could partially support it, support an alternative policy, be neutral, etc.

🎲 Possibility vs. Probability

The Problem

People often say "Well, it's POSSIBLE!" as if that proves they're right.

But the real question is: Is it PROBABLE?

The Truth

With enough creativity, almost anything is POSSIBLE!

But that doesn't make it likely to happen.

🌙 The Moon Base Example

Question: Is it possible I'll establish the first moon base?

Sure, if:

  1. I quit my job
  2. Start a successful billion-dollar company
  3. Befriend Elon Musk
  4. Convince him to fund a moon base
  5. Convince him to let me lead it
  6. Successfully establish the base

Is it possible? Yes.
Is it probable? No!

🪒 Occam's Razor

"The simplest explanation is usually the probable one"

Important Notes:
  • This is a GUIDE, not a perfect rule
  • When you have no evidence and multiple theories, test the simplest one first
  • Why? Because it's easier to test!
  • This doesn't mean ignore complex explanations - just start with simple ones
Example:
If you hear hoofbeats outside:
  • Simple explanation: Horses 🐴
  • Complex explanation: Zebras escaped from the zoo 🦓
Start by checking for horses!

⚡ Correlation ≠ Causation

Just because X and Y happen together doesn't mean X caused Y!

Example:
Observation: Ice cream sales and drowning deaths both increase in summer

Bad Conclusion: Ice cream causes drowning! ❌

Real Explanation: Hot weather causes both:
  • More people buy ice cream
  • More people go swimming (higher drowning risk)
Solution: Controlled experiments!
  • Control other factors
  • Compare treatment group vs. control group
  • Isolate what you're actually testing

🎯 The Hypothesis

What Is a Hypothesis?

A hypothesis is like an educated guess that scientists make to explain something they've observed. It's not just any guess - it has to be specific enough that you can test it!

📋 The 4 Requirements of a Good Hypothesis

A good hypothesis must be:

🎯

1. Specific

Explains what you expect to happen in a certain circumstance

✓ "Eating 500 fewer calories daily will reduce my weight"

✗ "Eating less might help"

🧪

2. Testable

Is falsifiable - can be disproven by empirical data

✓ "Plants grow faster with fertilizer"

✗ "There is invisible magic everywhere"

✂️

3. Parsimonious

Is as simple as feasible (remember Occam's Razor!)

✓ "Coffee helps me stay awake in class"

✗ "Coffee and red socks help me stay awake"

🔮

4. Predictive

Makes a prediction that can be tested

✓ "If I study more, I'll get better grades"

✗ "Studying is important" (no prediction!)

⚠️ Hypothesis vs Theory - They're Different!

Hypothesis Theory
A testable prediction A hypothesis proven by testing
Not yet validated Empirically validated
Could be wrong Has survived many tests (but can still be disproven!)

🔬 From Hypothesis to Experiment: A Walkthrough

1

Start With a Bad Hypothesis

"GlasgowGPT is better than any other LLM algorithm"

Problem: What does "better" mean? It's not measurable!
2

Define Your Terms

  • "Better" = gives more accurate responses
  • "Other LLM algorithms" = Claude, ChatGPT, and Grok
3

Design a Fair Test

Give each LLM verifiable factual questions (e.g., "What is the capital of Egypt?")

Control Variables:
  • Use same account type (paid vs unpaid)
  • Use accounts with no history
  • Same question order
  • Same latest version
4

Refined Hypothesis

"GlasgowGPT is more factually accurate (on average) than Claude, ChatGPT and Grok when answering factual questions"

Why "on average"? One counterexample doesn't disprove everything - we care about overall performance!

5

Convert to Research Question

"Which LLM gives more factually accurate answers (on average): GlasgowGPT, Claude, ChatGPT, or Grok?"

💡 Pro Tip: In computer science, research questions are often easier to address than hypotheses!

📝 More Hypothesis Examples

✅ Good Hypothesis: Weight Loss

H1: "If I eat 500 less kcal per day, I will lose weight."

  • ✓ Testable: by weighing myself
  • ✓ Falsifiable: I might not lose weight
  • ✓ Predictive: reduced bodyweight
  • ✓ Explains observation: I'm not losing weight because I eat too much

✅ Good Hypothesis: Coffee and Notes

H2: "If I drink coffee before lectures, I'll take more notes."

  • ✓ Testable: quantity of notes
  • ✓ Falsifiable: I might still fall asleep
  • ✓ Predictive: increased note-taking
  • ✓ Explains observation: I don't take many notes because I'm tired

❌ Problematic Hypothesis: Red Socks

H4: "If I practice Japanese for 15 minutes AND wear red socks, my reading level will improve."

  • ✓ Testable, falsifiable, predictive
  • ✗ NOT PARSIMONIOUS! - Socks are probably irrelevant

Keep your hypotheses simple - don't add unnecessary elements!

⚖️ Research Ethics

What Are Ethics?

Ethics = "Moral principles that govern a person's behaviour or the conducting of an activity" (Oxford Dictionary)

Acting ethically means acting in a way that embodies what people would consider as good values.

🤔 Why Do We Need Research Ethics?

As scientists, we have an ethical/moral obligation to be responsible with the work we do.

  • Universities and companies have ethical systems in place
  • Research without ethics is typically not publishable
  • Ethics limits what we can do - but for good reason!
  • History shows what happens without ethics...

⚠️ Historical Examples: What NOT to Do

😰 Milgram's Obedience Experiments (1961)

What happened:

  • Subjects were ordered to deliver increasingly strong electrical shocks to another person
  • The "victim" was an actor (no real shocks), but subjects believed it was real
  • Voltage went from 30V up to 450V (labeled "XXX")
  • Many participants delivered maximum shocks even when the "victim" begged to stop
The Ethical Problem: The experiment caused considerable psychological distress for the participants, even though the results were interesting.

😠 Facebook Mood Experiment (2012)

What happened:

  • Facebook hid certain elements from 689,003 people's news feed
  • Found they could make people feel more positive or negative
  • BIG PROBLEM: Done WITHOUT users' knowledge or consent!
The Ethical Problem: They manipulated nearly 700,000 people's emotional states without telling them!

📜 Laws and Regulations

Ethics Is Now Embodied in Laws

  • Computer Misuse Act
  • GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)
  • Health & Safety regulations
  • Intellectual Property laws

Important Codes of Ethics

  • ACM Code of Ethics: acm.org/code-of-ethics
  • BPS Code of Human Research Ethics
  • EPSRC research ethics codes

✅ University of Glasgow Requirements

Participant Rights (Must Be Followed!)

✍️

Informed Consent

Participants must be able to give informed consent before participating

🚪

Right to Withdraw

Participants can stop at any time without giving a reason

📊

Data Transparency

Participants must know what happens to their data

📧

Contact Details

Must provide researcher contact information

📝 The Ethics Approval Process

At University of Glasgow, ethical approval is required for any experiment involving human participants or data:

  1. Submit an application describing your experiment
  2. Outline potential ethical issues
  3. Explain how you've addressed them
  4. Declare adherence to ESRC/BPS frameworks
  5. 1-3 committee members review your proposal

⚠️ Ethics Beyond Experiments

Publication Ethics

Ethical behaviour is needed throughout the WHOLE research process, not just when dealing with humans!

  • Authorship: Only list real contributors
  • Conflicts of Interest: Disclose any conflicts
  • Data Fabrication: Never make up data!
  • Plagiarism: Never copy others' work
  • LLM Usage: ACM now requires disclosure of AI tool usage

Useful Resources

  • Committee on Publication Ethics: publicationethics.org
  • Retraction Watch: retractionwatch.com - Blog about retracted papers

✅ Quick Ethics Checklist

Before conducting research, ensure:

  • ☐ Participants can give informed consent
  • ☐ Participants know they can withdraw at any time
  • ☐ Participants know what happens to their data
  • ☐ You have researcher contact details to provide
  • ☐ Commercial funding is disclosed (if applicable)
  • ☐ Data confidentiality is maintained
  • ☐ Research complies with ESRC/BPS codes
  • ☐ Ethical approval has been obtained
  • ☐ LLM usage is disclosed (if applicable)
